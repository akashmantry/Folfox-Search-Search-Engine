<p>The <b>1988 Atlantic hurricane season</b> was a moderately active <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="Atlantic hurricane season">season</a> that proved costly and deadly, with 15&#160;<a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">tropical cyclones</a> directly affecting land. The season officially began on June 1,&#160;1988, and lasted until November 30,&#160;1988, although activity began on May 30 when a tropical depression developed in the <a href="/wiki/Caribbean_Sea" title="Caribbean Sea">Caribbean Sea</a>. The June through November dates conventionally delimit the period of each year when most <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">tropical cyclones</a> form in the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_basin" class="mw-redirect" title="Atlantic basin">Atlantic basin</a>. The first cyclone to attain tropical storm status was Alberto on August 8, nearly a month later than usual.<sup id="cite_ref-TCclimate_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TCclimate-1">[1]</a></sup> The final storm of the year, <a href="/wiki/Tropical_Storm_Keith_(1988)" title="Tropical Storm Keith (1988)">Tropical Storm Keith</a>, became <a href="/wiki/Extratropical_cyclone" title="Extratropical cyclone">extratropical</a> on November 24.</p>
<p>The season produced 19&#160;tropical depressions of which 12 attained tropical storm status. One tropical storm was operationally classified as a tropical depression but was reclassified in post-analysis. Five tropical cyclones reached hurricane status of which three became <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone_scales" title="Tropical cyclone scales">major hurricanes</a> reaching Category&#160;3 on the <a href="/wiki/Saffir-Simpson_Hurricane_Scale" class="mw-redirect" title="Saffir-Simpson Hurricane Scale">Saffir-Simpson Hurricane Scale</a>.</p>
<p>The most notable cyclone of the season was <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Gilbert" title="Hurricane Gilbert">Hurricane Gilbert</a>, which at the time was the strongest <a href="/wiki/North_Atlantic_tropical_cyclone" class="mw-redirect" title="North Atlantic tropical cyclone">Atlantic hurricane</a> on record. The hurricane tracked through the Caribbean Sea and the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Mexico" title="Gulf of Mexico">Gulf of Mexico</a> and caused devastation in <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">Mexico</a> and many island nations, particularly <a href="/wiki/Jamaica" title="Jamaica">Jamaica</a>. Its passage caused US$5&#160;billion in damage (1988 USD; $10.1&#160;billion 2017&#160;USD) and more than 300&#160;deaths, mostly in Mexico. <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Joan" class="mw-redirect" title="Hurricane Joan">Hurricane Joan</a>, striking <a href="/wiki/Nicaragua" title="Nicaragua">Nicaragua</a> as a Category&#160;4 hurricane, caused about US$2&#160;billion in damage (1988 USD; $4.05&#160;billion 2017&#160;USD) and more than 200&#160;deaths. The hurricane crossed into the eastern Pacific Ocean and was reclassified as Tropical Storm Miriam.</p>

<p>Forecasts of hurricane activity are issued before each hurricane season by noted hurricane experts such as <a href="/wiki/William_M._Gray" title="William M. Gray">Dr. William M. Gray</a> and his associates at <a href="/wiki/Colorado_State_University" title="Colorado State University">Colorado State University</a>. A normal season as defined by NOAA has six to fourteen named storms of which four to eight reach hurricane strength and one to three become major hurricanes. The June 1988 forecast was that eleven storms would form and that seven would reach hurricane status. The forecast did not specify how many hurricanes would reach <i>major hurricane</i> status.<sup id="cite_ref-CSUpredic_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CSUpredic-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Season_activity">Season activity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1988_Atlantic_hurricane_season&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Season activity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Atlantic hurricane season officially began on June 1,<sup id="cite_ref-TCclimate_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TCclimate-1">[1]</a></sup> but activity in 1988 began two&#160;days earlier with the formation of <a href="/wiki/1988_Atlantic_hurricane_season#Tropical_Depression_One" title="1988 Atlantic hurricane season">Tropical Depression One</a> on May 30. It was an above average season in which 19&#160;<a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">tropical depressions</a> formed.<sup id="cite_ref-TDs1988_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TDs1988-3">[3]</a></sup> Twelve&#160;depressions attained tropical storm status, and five of these attained hurricane status, of which three reached <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone_scales" title="Tropical cyclone scales">major hurricane status</a>. Four&#160;hurricanes and three&#160;tropical storms made <a href="/wiki/Landfall_(meteorology)" class="mw-redirect" title="Landfall (meteorology)">landfall</a> during the season and caused 550&#160;deaths and $7&#160;billion in damage (1988&#160;USD). The last storm of the season, <a href="/wiki/1988_Atlantic_hurricane_season#Tropical_Storm_Keith" title="1988 Atlantic hurricane season">Tropical Storm Keith</a>, dissipated on November 24,<sup id="cite_ref-mwr_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-4">[4]</a></sup> only 6&#160;days before the official end of the season on November 30.<sup id="cite_ref-TCclimate_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TCclimate-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>The activity in the first two&#160;months of the season was limited because of strong <a href="/wiki/Wind_shear" title="Wind shear">wind shear</a> from an upper tropospheric flow. Although vigorous <a href="/wiki/Tropical_wave" title="Tropical wave">tropical waves</a> moved off the coast of Africa, most of them quickly diminished in intensity as they crossed the tropical Atlantic Ocean. As a result, no tropical depressions formed in June or July. Decreased wind shear in August allowed tropical waves to develop into tropical cyclones.<sup id="cite_ref-TDs1988_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TDs1988-3">[3]</a></sup> The official <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone_track_forecasting" title="Tropical cyclone track forecasting">storm track forecast</a> errors were <span class="nowrap">30 to 40 percent</span> lower than the average for the previous 10&#160;years.<sup id="cite_ref-mwr_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-4">[4]</a></sup> The <span class="nowrap">24-, 48-, and 72-hour</span> forecasts were the most accurate in more than 18&#160;years and were also more accurate than in each subsequent season until <a href="/wiki/1996_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="1996 Atlantic hurricane season">1996</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-forecasts_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-forecasts-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>The season's activity was reflected with a cumulative <a href="/wiki/Accumulated_cyclone_energy" title="Accumulated cyclone energy">accumulated cyclone energy</a> (ACE) rating of 103, which is classified as "near normal".<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-background_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-background-7">[7]</a></sup> ACE is, broadly speaking, a measure of the power of the hurricane multiplied by the length of time it existed, so storms that last a long time, as well as particularly strong hurricanes, have high ACEs. ACE is calculated for all named storms at 6-hourly intervials, when a system exceeds 34 knots (39 miles per hour; 63 kilometres per hour).<sup id="cite_ref-background_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-background-7">[7]</a></sup></p>

<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Tropical_Depression_One_(1988)" title="Tropical Depression One (1988)">Tropical Depression One (1988)</a></div>
<p>The first tropical depression of the season formed on May 30 in the northwest Caribbean Sea. The system encountered unfavorable conditions as it moved northward toward <a href="/wiki/Cuba" title="Cuba">Cuba</a>, and a <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Hunters" title="Hurricane Hunters">reconnaissance airplane</a> sent to investigate it could not find a well-defined center.<sup id="cite_ref-ap0601am_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ap0601am-8">[8]</a></sup> The depression remained weak and degenerated on June 2 into an open <a href="/wiki/Trough_(meteorology)" title="Trough (meteorology)">trough</a> of low pressure in the <a href="/wiki/Florida_Straits" class="mw-redirect" title="Florida Straits">Florida Straits</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-TDs1988_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TDs1988-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Rainfall from the depression and its precursor peaked at 40.35&#160;in (1025&#160;mm), including a daily peak of 34.13&#160;in (867&#160;mm).<sup id="cite_ref-CUBARAIN_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CUBARAIN-9">[9]</a></sup> The rainfall most affected the province of <a href="/wiki/Cienfuegos_Province" title="Cienfuegos Province">Cienfuegos</a>, though the provinces of <a href="/wiki/Villa_Clara_Province" title="Villa Clara Province">Villa Clara</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sancti_Sp%C3%ADritus_Province" title="Sancti Spíritus Province">Sancti Spíritus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ciego_de_%C3%81vila_Province" title="Ciego de Ávila Province">Ciego de Ávila</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Camag%C3%BCey_Province" title="Camagüey Province">Camagüey</a> were also impacted.<sup id="cite_ref-cubagua_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cubagua-10">[10]</a></sup> A tornado in the city of <a href="/wiki/Camag%C3%BCey" title="Camagüey">Camagüey</a> destroyed five <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet</a> planes and multiple buildings.<sup id="cite_ref-ap0602am_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ap0602am-11">[11]</a></sup> Flooding prompted officials to use rescue crews, helicopters, and amphibious vehicles to evacuate 65,000&#160;residents in low-lying areas to higher grounds. The storm left many without power and communications, severely damaged the country's transportation infrastructure, and destroyed six&#160;bridges.<sup id="cite_ref-ap0602am_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ap0602am-11">[11]</a></sup> Flooding from the depression damaged 1,000&#160;houses and destroyed 200&#160;homes in Camagüey Province alone.<sup id="cite_ref-ria0608_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ria0608-12">[12]</a></sup> Throughout Cuba, the depression affected about 90,000&#160;people, injuring dozens<sup id="cite_ref-rwtd1_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rwtd1-13">[13]</a></sup> and killing a total of 37&#160;people,<sup id="cite_ref-cubagua_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cubagua-10">[10]</a></sup> including three who died from electrocution.<sup id="cite_ref-ap0602am_11-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ap0602am-11">[11]</a></sup> In Florida, the depression produced light rain, including 3.18&#160;in (81&#160;mm) at <a href="/wiki/Pompano_Beach,_Florida" title="Pompano Beach, Florida">Pompano Beach</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-td1rain_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-td1rain-14">[14]</a></sup></p>

<p>The season's first named storm originated on August 4 within a weak trough of low pressure that formed off the coast of <a href="/wiki/South_Carolina" title="South Carolina">South Carolina</a>. The next day a low level circulation was detected by satellite, indicating that a tropical depression was forming. By August 6 the storm was designated the second tropical depression of the season.<sup id="cite_ref-mwr_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-4">[4]</a></sup> An approaching weak <a href="/wiki/Weather_fronts" class="mw-redirect" title="Weather fronts">frontal trough</a> pushed the depression northeastward and enhanced its upper level outflow. On August 7 the system was designated Tropical Storm Alberto while located just south of <a href="/wiki/Nantucket,_Massachusetts" title="Nantucket, Massachusetts">Nantucket, Massachusetts</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-albertotcr1_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-albertotcr1-15">[15]</a></sup> The storm accelerated northeastward at 29&#160;mph (47&#160;km/h) and struck western <a href="/wiki/Nova_Scotia" title="Nova Scotia">Nova Scotia</a> that evening with little impact.<sup id="cite_ref-albertotcr1_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-albertotcr1-15">[15]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-chc88_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-chc88-16">[16]</a></sup> On August 8 Alberto became <a href="/wiki/Extratropical" class="mw-redirect" title="Extratropical">extratropical</a> over the cold waters of the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Saint_Lawrence" title="Gulf of Saint Lawrence">Gulf of Saint Lawrence</a>. Shortly thereafter it dissipated just north of <a href="/wiki/Newfoundland_(island)" title="Newfoundland (island)">Newfoundland</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-albertotcr1_15-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-albertotcr1-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>In Maine, Alberto produced light rainfall, reaching 2.4&#160;inches (60&#160;mm) near <a href="/wiki/Millinocket,_Maine" title="Millinocket, Maine">Millinocket</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-albany_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-albany-17">[17]</a></sup> The storm produced peak wind gusts of 48&#160;mph (78&#160;km/h) at <a href="/wiki/Yarmouth,_Nova_Scotia" title="Yarmouth, Nova Scotia">Yarmouth, Nova Scotia</a>. Rainfall reached 1.78&#160;inches (45.1&#160;mm) in <a href="/wiki/Saint_John,_New_Brunswick" title="Saint John, New Brunswick">Saint John, New Brunswick</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-chc88_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-chc88-16">[16]</a></sup> most of which fell in a short amount of time. The rainfall caused localized flooding, which briefly closed some streets.<sup id="cite_ref-ts88_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ts88-18">[18]</a></sup> The extratropical remnants of Alberto also produced light rain and some clouds along western <a href="/wiki/Newfoundland_(island)" title="Newfoundland (island)">Newfoundland</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-chc88_16-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-chc88-16">[16]</a></sup></p>

<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Tropical_Storm_Beryl_(1988)" title="Tropical Storm Beryl (1988)">Tropical Storm Beryl (1988)</a></div>
<p>The third tropical depression of the season formed on August 7 from a surface <a href="/wiki/Low_pressure" class="mw-redirect" title="Low pressure">low</a> over southeastern <a href="/wiki/Louisiana" title="Louisiana">Louisiana</a>. The slow moving system organized as it drifted toward the mouth of the <a href="/wiki/Mississippi_River" title="Mississippi River">Mississippi River</a>. It soon had enough convective organization for the <a href="/wiki/National_Hurricane_Center" title="National Hurricane Center">National Hurricane Center</a> (NHC) to issue an initial advisory on Tropical Depression Three.<sup id="cite_ref-beryltcr1_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-beryltcr1-19">[19]</a></sup> By August 8 surface winds increased enough to issue tropical storm warnings for Louisiana to the <a href="/wiki/Florida_Panhandle" title="Florida Panhandle">Florida Panhandle</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-mwr_4-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-4">[4]</a></sup> Over the open Gulf, Beryl produced sustained winds of minimal tropical storm force and tropical storm force gusts over coastal Louisiana, <a href="/wiki/Mississippi" title="Mississippi">Mississippi</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Alabama" title="Alabama">Alabama</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-beryltcr2_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-beryltcr2-20">[20]</a></sup> Excessive rain fell along the central Gulf Coast, including local amounts of 16&#160;in (410&#160;mm) at <a href="/wiki/Dauphin_Island,_Alabama" title="Dauphin Island, Alabama">Dauphin Island, Alabama</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-berylrain_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-berylrain-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>Maintaining a well-structured outflow, Beryl's circulation on August 9 moved over warm water, where conditions were favorable for further <a href="/wiki/Rapid_deepening" class="mw-redirect" title="Rapid deepening">intensification</a>. However, a front approached from the northwest and reversed the storm's course into southeastern Louisiana.<sup id="cite_ref-berylrain_21-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-berylrain-21">[21]</a></sup> The next morning Beryl had weakened to a tropical depression as it moved over the <a href="/wiki/Bayou_Teche" title="Bayou Teche">Bayou Teche</a>. Heavy downpours from system's remnants brought more than 12&#160;in (300&#160;mm) of rain to parts of eastern Texas. Overall damage from the storm was light, and only one known death was attributed to the storm.<sup id="cite_ref-beryltcr3_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-beryltcr3-22">[22]</a></sup></p>

<p>On August 12 a westward-moving tropical wave developed into Tropical Depression Four near the southern <a href="/wiki/Bahamas" class="mw-redirect" title="Bahamas">Bahamas</a>. The depression tracked north-northwest along Florida's coast and made landfall near <a href="/wiki/Jacksonville,_Florida" title="Jacksonville, Florida">Jacksonville, Florida</a>, the next day.<sup id="cite_ref-TDs1988_3-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TDs1988-3">[3]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-upi0813bc_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-upi0813bc-23">[23]</a></sup> The system spawned gusty winds and thunderstorms along the coasts of Florida and Georgia but caused little damage.<sup id="cite_ref-upi0813bc_23-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-upi0813bc-23">[23]</a></sup> The storm moved over south Georgia and the central Gulf Coast while dropping up to 7&#160;in (178&#160;mm) of rain on the <a href="/wiki/Southeastern_United_States" title="Southeastern United States">Southeast</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-td4rain_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-td4rain-24">[24]</a></sup> According to the <a href="/wiki/National_Weather_Service" title="National Weather Service">National Weather Service</a>, winds in some <a href="/wiki/Squalls" class="mw-redirect" title="Squalls">squalls</a> to the north and east of the center reached up to 50&#160;mph (80&#160;km/h).<sup id="cite_ref-ap0813am_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ap0813am-25">[25]</a></sup> The system finally dissipated as it reemerged over water near the mouth of the <a href="/wiki/Mississippi_River" title="Mississippi River">Mississippi</a>.</p>
<p>Early predictions from hurricane forecasters said that the depression would strengthen into the season's third tropical storm.<sup id="cite_ref-upi0814am_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-upi0814am-26">[26]</a></sup> Because of unfavorable upper level conditions and interaction with Bahama islands, the system lost its well defined center as it moved towards Florida's east coast.<sup id="cite_ref-upi0813bc_23-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-upi0813bc-23">[23]</a></sup></p>

<p>A tropical wave in the far eastern Atlantic developed into the fifth tropical depression on August 20. The storm drifted north-northwest of the <a href="/wiki/Cape_Verde" title="Cape Verde">Cape Verde</a> islands for the next three&#160;days with little change in strength.<sup id="cite_ref-TDs1988_3-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TDs1988-3">[3]</a></sup> Forecasters were concerned because the depression formed in the breeding ground where other powerful East Coast hurricanes have started. Though the storm was still very weak, they initially predicted it would strengthen.<sup id="cite_ref-upi0820am_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-upi0820am-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>By August 24 the depression's forward speed had increased to 15&#160;mph (24&#160;km/h) as its movement turned west. Cool ocean temperatures weakened the system and diminished its prospects for restrengthening,<sup id="cite_ref-ap0823am_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ap0823am-28">[28]</a></sup> and on August 26 Tropical Depression Five degenerated into a tropical wave. The remnants redeveloped on August 30 about 180 miles (290&#160;km) southeast of <a href="/wiki/North_Carolina" title="North Carolina">North Carolina</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-TDs1988_3-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TDs1988-3">[3]</a></sup> and the Washington office of the <a href="/wiki/National_Weather_Service" title="National Weather Service">National Weather Service</a> continued to track the system as a gale center until it merged with a front off the <a href="/wiki/East_Coast_of_the_United_States" title="East Coast of the United States">East Coast</a> on September 1.</p>

<p>Tropical Depression Six developed from a tropical wave that moved off the northwest African coast on August 12. The system crossed the tropical Atlantic as a wave until it began organizing near 55°&#160;W on August 19.<sup id="cite_ref-kristytcr1_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kristytcr1-29">[29]</a></sup> The next day this system was designated a tropical depression while it approached the <a href="/wiki/Windward_Islands" title="Windward Islands">Windward Islands</a>. After crossing the islands, the depression continued westward into the central Caribbean and encountered less-favorable conditions.<sup id="cite_ref-upi0821bc_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-upi0821bc-30">[30]</a></sup> Though poorly organized on August 21, the depression was expected to strengthen into a tropical storm over the western Caribbean's warmer waters. Nevertheless, it was downgraded to a tropical wave at 80°&#160;W near the island of <a href="/wiki/Jamaica" title="Jamaica">Jamaica</a> on August 23.<sup id="cite_ref-upi0823am_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-upi0823am-31">[31]</a></sup> The disturbance moved over Central America with minimal convection but redeveloped into <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Kristy_(1988)" class="mw-redirect" title="Hurricane Kristy (1988)">Hurricane Kristy</a> once it reached the eastern Pacific.<sup id="cite_ref-kristytcr1_29-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kristytcr1-29">[29]</a></sup> The system's main effect on land was squally weather on the Windward Islands.<sup id="cite_ref-TDs1988_3-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TDs1988-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Tropical_Storm_Chris_(1988)" title="Tropical Storm Chris (1988)">Tropical Storm Chris (1988)</a></div>
<p>Chris formed from a strong tropical wave that moved off the west coast of Africa on August 15. By August 21 convection in the northern part of the wave detached and organized into Tropical Depression Seven.<sup id="cite_ref-debbytcr1_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-debbytcr1-32">[32]</a></sup> The storm tracked westward along the southern periphery of a subtropical high pressure ridge over the mid-Atlantic.<sup id="cite_ref-mwr_4-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-4">[4]</a></sup> For the next seven&#160;days, surface and reconnaissance observations found little evidence that the storm was strengthening. As a result, it remained a tropical depression as it moved across portions of the <a href="/wiki/Lesser_Antilles" title="Lesser Antilles">Lesser</a> and <a href="/wiki/Greater_Antilles" title="Greater Antilles">Greater Antilles</a> as well as the <a href="/wiki/Bahamas" class="mw-redirect" title="Bahamas">Bahamas</a>.</p>
<p>The depression passed south of <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rico" title="Puerto Rico">Puerto Rico</a> on August 24 and dumped more than 14&#160;in (360&#160;mm) of rain on parts of the island.<sup id="cite_ref-chrisrain_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-chrisrain-33">[33]</a></sup> Three&#160;deaths in Puerto Rico were attributed to the weather.<sup id="cite_ref-christcr2_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-christcr2-34">[34]</a></sup> On August 28 the storm was upgraded to Tropical Storm Chris as it traveled northward just offshore of Florida. It made landfall near <a href="/wiki/Savannah,_Georgia" title="Savannah, Georgia">Savannah, Georgia</a>, bringing light wind and rain damage to the area.<sup id="cite_ref-christcr2_34-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-christcr2-34">[34]</a></sup> Weakening to a depression, Chris poured heavy rains on South Carolina, where it merged with a cold front and became extratropical. The low accelerated over the <a href="/wiki/East_Coast_of_the_United_States" title="East Coast of the United States">Eastern Seaboard</a> through Nova Scotia and finally dissipated on August 30.<sup id="cite_ref-chrisrain_33-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-chrisrain-33">[33]</a></sup> Heavy thunderstorms spawned a tornado in South Carolina that resulted in another death.<sup id="cite_ref-christcr1_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-christcr1-35">[35]</a></sup></p>

<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Debby_(1988)" title="Hurricane Debby (1988)">Hurricane Debby (1988)</a></div>
<p>Debby formed from the southern part of a tropical wave that became Tropical Storm Chris. In the mid-tropical Atlantic, the northern area of convection detached and became Tropical Depression Seven. The southern portion continued moving westward as a disorganized area of showers.<sup id="cite_ref-mwr_4-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-4">[4]</a></sup> The system did not develop until the low-level center emerged from the <a href="/wiki/Yucat%C3%A1n_Peninsula" title="Yucatán Peninsula">Yucatán</a> into the <a href="/wiki/Bay_of_Campeche" title="Bay of Campeche">Bay of Campeche</a> on August 31. It is estimated that the storm became Tropical Depression Eight just offshore at around 12&#160;p.m. local time.<sup id="cite_ref-debbytcr1_32-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-debbytcr1-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<p>Drifting west-northwest over the Gulf of Mexico, the depression organized and reached tropical storm-strength early on September 2. Later that day, based on observations from aircraft reconnaissance, Debby was upgraded to a hurricane.<sup id="cite_ref-debbytcr1_32-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-debbytcr1-32">[32]</a></sup> At peak intensity, the hurricane's center was just 30&#160;mi (48&#160;km) from the coast. With little change in intensity, Debby made landfall near <a href="/wiki/Tuxpan,_Veracruz" class="mw-redirect" title="Tuxpan, Veracruz">Tuxpan, Veracruz</a>, six&#160;hours later.<sup id="cite_ref-debbytcr1_32-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-debbytcr1-32">[32]</a></sup> The storm brought high winds, inland flooding, and mudslides and caused 10&#160;deaths.<sup id="cite_ref-debbytcr2_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-debbytcr2-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<p>Debby weakened considerably over the <a href="/wiki/Sierra_Madre_Oriental" title="Sierra Madre Oriental">Sierra Madre Oriental</a> mountains, although the remnants continued moving across Mexico. The tight center tracked towards the Pacific coast and reemerged near <a href="/wiki/Manzanillo,_Colima" title="Manzanillo, Colima">Manzanillo</a> on September 5. Upon entering the <a href="/wiki/Pacific_hurricane" title="Pacific hurricane">Eastern Pacific</a>, the system became <a href="/wiki/1988_Pacific_hurricane_season#Tropical_Depression_Seventeen-E" title="1988 Pacific hurricane season">Tropical Depression Seventeen-E</a> before dissipating in the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_California" title="Gulf of California">Gulf of California</a> on September 8.</p>

<p>On September 2 a cluster of thunderstorms associated with a northwestward-moving tropical wave developed a <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclogenesis" title="Tropical cyclogenesis">surface low</a> near <a href="/wiki/Bermuda" title="Bermuda">Bermuda</a>. Though the surface low remained poorly defined and separate from the convection, the system became a tropical depression on September 3.<sup id="cite_ref-ernestotcr1_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ernestotcr1-37">[37]</a></sup> Under the influence of <a href="/wiki/Westerlies" title="Westerlies">southwesterlies</a>, the depression accelerated northeastward at 50&#160;mph (80&#160;km/h). Late on September 3 it was upgraded to Tropical Storm Ernesto. The storm continued to strengthen as it lost tropical characteristics.<sup id="cite_ref-ernestotcr3_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ernestotcr3-38">[38]</a></sup> A large <a href="/wiki/Extratropical_cyclone" title="Extratropical cyclone">extratropical storm</a> over the North Atlantic absorbed Ernesto on September 5.</p>
<p>Throughout its life, Ernesto remained at sea and brought strong winds to the open waters of the ocean. The only land area affected by the storm was in the <a href="/wiki/Azores" title="Azores">Azores</a>, where it brought near storm-force winds to <a href="/wiki/Flores_Island_(Azores)" title="Flores Island (Azores)">Flores Island</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-mwr_4-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-4">[4]</a></sup> No damage or casualties were reported.<sup id="cite_ref-ernestotcr1_37-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ernestotcr1-37">[37]</a></sup></p>

<p>A broad low pressure area formed in the western Gulf of Mexico on September 2 and quickly developed through the next day. By September 3 convection was organized enough to declare the system a tropical depression about 160&#160;mi (260&#160;km) west-southwest of <a href="/wiki/Morgan_City,_Louisiana" title="Morgan City, Louisiana">Morgan City, Louisiana</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-ap0903am_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ap0903am-39">[39]</a></sup> Forecasters issued tropical storm warnings for the coast from <a href="/wiki/Cameron,_Louisiana" title="Cameron, Louisiana">Cameron, Louisiana</a>, to <a href="/wiki/Apalachicola,_Florida" title="Apalachicola, Florida">Apalachicola, Florida</a>, while the storm moved rapidly northeastward at 15&#160;to&#160;20&#160;mph (32&#160;km/h).<sup id="cite_ref-TDs1988_3-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TDs1988-3">[3]</a></sup> However, the depression degenerated a few hours later when it merged with the cold front that had caused its acceleration. Oil rigs in the Gulf of Mexico reported winds gusts to 40&#160;mph (64&#160;km/h), and moderate to heavy rains drenched large portions of southeast Texas and Louisiana.<sup id="cite_ref-TDs1988_3-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TDs1988-3">[3]</a></sup> The wave dampened over the next 24&#160;hours and brought heavy rain to the rest of the southeast, including a maximum of 8.4&#160;in (210&#160;mm) in <a href="/wiki/Biloxi,_Mississippi" title="Biloxi, Mississippi">Biloxi, Mississippi</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-td10rain_40-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-td10rain-40">[40]</a></sup> No major damage was reported.</p>

<p>A well-organized disturbance moved off the African coast on September 6 and rapidly developed into Tropical Depression Eleven. The NHC began issuing advisories on September 8 while it was 350&#160;mi (560&#160;km) northeast of <a href="/wiki/Cape_Verde" title="Cape Verde">Cape Verde</a>. An after-the-fact review of satellite and ship reports indicated that the depression reached tropical storm-strength on September 7.<sup id="cite_ref-unnamedtcr1_41-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-unnamedtcr1-41">[41]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-unnamedtcr4_42-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-unnamedtcr4-42">[42]</a></sup> However, because of its extreme eastern track, the storm's observational track did not include this information.</p>
<p>For three&#160;days a large trough of low pressure northwest of the system steered it north-northwest towards cooler waters. This unnamed storm was later added to the list of tropical storms in the annual summary for the Atlantic hurricane season.<sup id="cite_ref-mwr_4-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-4">[4]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hurricane_Florence">Hurricane Florence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1988_Atlantic_hurricane_season&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Hurricane Florence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Florence_(1988)" title="Hurricane Florence (1988)">Hurricane Florence (1988)</a></div>
<p>A cloud band accompanying a cold front exited the coast of Texas into the Gulf of Mexico on September 4. The band split into two over the central Gulf when the southern portion stalled and the northern portion developed into a frontal wave that tracked northeastward.<sup id="cite_ref-florencetcr1_43-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-florencetcr1-43">[43]</a></sup> Convection over the southern portion increased and wrapped around the center of the cloud band. On September 7 the system formed a surface circulation, and tropical depression advisories began that day.</p>
<p>The depression drifted eastward under the influence of the dissipating frontal trough and intensified into Tropical Storm Florence, as confirmed by <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Hunters" title="Hurricane Hunters">Hurricane Hunters</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-florencetcr1_43-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-florencetcr1-43">[43]</a></sup> The storm turned northward on September 9 and accelerated toward the northern Gulf Coast under the influence of a mid- to upper-level trough. Florence became a hurricane just hours before landfall on the western <a href="/wiki/Mississippi_River_Delta" title="Mississippi River Delta">Mississippi Delta</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-mwr_4-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-4">[4]</a></sup> The storm rapidly weakened over southeastern <a href="/wiki/Louisiana" title="Louisiana">Louisiana</a> and lost all its deep convection as it passed over the New Orleans area. Florence became a depression on September 10 near <a href="/wiki/Baton_Rouge" class="mw-redirect" title="Baton Rouge">Baton Rouge</a> and dissipated the next day over northeast Texas.<sup id="cite_ref-florencetcr1_43-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-florencetcr1-43">[43]</a></sup></p>
<p>Early in its duration the system dropped moderate amounts of rainfall across the Yucatán Peninsula.<sup id="cite_ref-florencerain_44-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-florencerain-44">[44]</a></sup> Upon striking Louisiana, storm surge water levels rose moderately above normal just east of where the center moved ashore.<sup id="cite_ref-florencetcr2_45-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-florencetcr2-45">[45]</a></sup> Gusty winds caused power outages to more than 100,000&#160;people. In Alabama one&#160;man died while trying to secure his boat. Rainfall from the hurricane caused severe river flooding in portions of the Florida Panhandle in an area already severely affected by heavy rainfall, and the flooding damaged or destroyed dozens of houses in Santa Rosa County.<sup id="cite_ref-florencetcr3_46-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-florencetcr3-46">[46]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hurricane_Gilbert">Hurricane Gilbert</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1988_Atlantic_hurricane_season&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Hurricane Gilbert">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Gilbert" title="Hurricane Gilbert">Hurricane Gilbert</a></div>
<p>The 12th tropical depression formed just east of the Lesser Antilles on September 8. As it moved west-northwest, it became Tropical Storm Gilbert over the islands on September 9. The tropical storm turned west and rapidly intensified to a major hurricane on September 11. Gilbert continued to strengthen as it brushed the southern coast of Hispaniola. It passed directly over Jamaica as a Category&#160;3 hurricane and brought torrential rains to the island's mountainous areas. When the center reemerged over water, Gilbert rapidly intensified again. On September 13 the central pressure dropped 72 millibars (2.1&#160;inHg), the fastest deepening of an Atlantic hurricane on record until <a href="/wiki/2005_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="2005 Atlantic hurricane season">2005</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Wilma" title="Hurricane Wilma">Hurricane Wilma</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-WilmaTCR_47-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WilmaTCR-47">[47]</a></sup> Gilbert's pressure of 888 millibars (26.2&#160;inHg) at the time was the lowest sea level pressure ever recorded in the <a href="/wiki/Western_Hemisphere" title="Western Hemisphere">Western Hemisphere</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-gilberttcr1_48-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gilberttcr1-48">[48]</a></sup></p>
<p>Gilbert weakened slightly before landfall on the Yucatán Peninsula, although it struck at Category&#160;5 strength. As the eye moved over land, the storm rapidly lost strength, reemerging on September 15 in the Gulf of Mexico as a Category&#160;2 hurricane. Hurricane Gilbert continued its northwest track and restrengthened to a minimal Category&#160;4 hurricane. On September 16, Gilbert made its final landfall in northeast Mexico near the town of <a href="/wiki/La_Pesca" title="La Pesca">La Pesca</a> with maximum sustained winds of 125&#160;mph (201&#160;km/h). The center passed south of <a href="/wiki/Monterrey" title="Monterrey">Monterrey</a>, Mexico, on September 17 and brought heavy flooding to the city. Gilbert's remnants turned north and eventually merged with a developing frontal low pressure system over <a href="/wiki/Missouri" title="Missouri">Missouri</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-gilberttcr1_48-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gilberttcr1-48">[48]</a></sup></p>
<p>Hurricane Gilbert was the most intense hurricane ever observed in the Atlantic basin until Hurricane Wilma broke this record in <a href="/wiki/2005_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="2005 Atlantic hurricane season">2005</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-WilmaTCR_47-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WilmaTCR-47">[47]</a></sup> The storm caused $5&#160;billion (1988&#160;USD) in damage across the Caribbean and into Central America. Gilbert was the first hurricane to make landfall in Jamaica since <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Charlie_(1951)" title="Hurricane Charlie (1951)">Hurricane Charlie</a> in 1951. Until 2007's <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Dean_(2007)" class="mw-redirect" title="Hurricane Dean (2007)">Hurricane Dean</a>, it was also the most recent storm to make landfall as a Category&#160;5 hurricane in Mexico. The death toll from Gilbert was reported to be 318&#160;people, mostly from Mexico.<sup id="cite_ref-gilberttcr2_49-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gilberttcr2-49">[49]</a></sup></p>

<p>A tropical wave with deeply organized convection crossed the coast of Africa on September 15. The system was forced west due to a strong ridge in the eastern Atlantic. On September 19 at 1800 UTC, the system was upgraded to Tropical Depression Fourteen. By 0600 UTC on September 20, the depression was strengthened, and was upgraded to Tropical Storm Helene. Helene began to turn to the northwest on September 21 due to a major trough in the eastern Atlantic. Later on September 21, Helene intensified into a hurricane. Favorable conditions allowed the storm to continue strengthening, and on September 22, Helene became a major hurricane. Late on the following day, Helene attained its peak intensity maximum sustained winds were at 145&#160;mph (233&#160;km/h) and the minimum pressure of 938&#160;mbar (27.7&#160;inHg).<sup id="cite_ref-helenetcr1_50-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-helenetcr1-50">[50]</a></sup></p>
<p>After reaching peak intensity, Helene weakened as it tracked generally northward through the open Atlantic. By early on September 29, Helene briefly restrengthened into a Category 2 hurricane and reached a secondary peak of 105&#160;mph (169&#160;km/h). However, later that day, Helene weakened back to a Category 1 hurricane while accelerating to the northeast. At 1200 UTC on September 30, Helene transitioned into an extratropical cyclone while centered well south of <a href="/wiki/Iceland" title="Iceland">Iceland</a>. The precursor tropical wave produced thunderstorms and gusty winds ranging between 23 and 34&#160;mph (37 and 55&#160;km/h) in Cape Verde on September 17.<sup id="cite_ref-helenetcr1_50-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-helenetcr1-50">[50]</a></sup></p>

<p>While Hurricane Helene was spinning in the central Atlantic, a tropical wave that moved off the coast of Africa in late September rapidly organized. On September 27 the storm became the fifteenth tropical depression of the season while it was about 265&#160;mi (426&#160;km) south-southeast of <a href="/wiki/Cape_Verde" title="Cape Verde">Cape Verde</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-ap0927am_51-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ap0927am-51">[51]</a></sup> The depression tracked westward at 15&#160;to&#160;20&#160;mph (32&#160;km/h) but weakened rapidly. The next day it was downgraded to a tropical wave while still in the far eastern Atlantic, and never reformed in the Atlantic.<sup id="cite_ref-TDs1988_3-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TDs1988-3">[3]</a></sup> Aside from a brief threat to the <a href="/wiki/Cape_Verde" title="Cape Verde">Cape Verde</a> islands, the system remained far from any landmasses throughout its life.</p>

<p>Isaac moved off the coast of Africa on September 23. It traveled westward at a low latitude along the <a href="/wiki/Intertropical_Convergence_Zone" title="Intertropical Convergence Zone">Intertropical Convergence Zone</a> (or ITCZ), and its convection gradually grew more organized.<sup id="cite_ref-isaactcr1_52-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-isaactcr1-52">[52]</a></sup> On September 29 it was identified as Tropical Depression Sixteen about 900&#160;mi (1,400&#160;km) southeast of <a href="/wiki/Barbados" title="Barbados">Barbados</a>. The westward path of the storm shifted two&#160;degrees northward, possibly as a result of the formation of a new center. On September 30 the depression was upgraded when an Air Force reconnaissance plane discovered tropical storm-force winds.<sup id="cite_ref-mwr_4-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-4">[4]</a></sup> Westerly vertical wind shear prevented deep convection at the center of the storm. As Isaac approached the islands, northern parts of the Lesser Antilles were issued tropical storm warnings.<sup id="cite_ref-mwr_4-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-4">[4]</a></sup> Nevertheless, the storm lasted only a short time in the shearing environment. Isaac was downgraded to a depression on October 1 and completely dissipated shortly thereafter.<sup id="cite_ref-isaactcr1_52-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-isaactcr1-52">[52]</a></sup> The remnants of Isaac eventually regenerated in the eastern Pacific Ocean basin as <a href="/wiki/Tropical_Depression_Twenty-E_(1988)" class="mw-redirect" title="Tropical Depression Twenty-E (1988)">Tropical Depression Twenty-E</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-tc20ed1103Z_53-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tc20ed1103Z-53">[53]</a></sup></p>
<p>As a tropical cyclone, Isaac did not significantly affect land.<sup id="cite_ref-prelim2_54-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-prelim2-54">[54]</a></sup> However, the remnants dropped heavy rainfall across Trinidad and Tobago, causing flooding and mudslides that injured 20&#160;people<sup id="cite_ref-upi_55-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-upi-55">[55]</a></sup> and left at least 30 homeless.<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Flash_flood" title="Flash flood">Flash flooding</a> in <a href="/wiki/Morvant" title="Morvant">Morvant</a> killed two people.<sup id="cite_ref-upi_55-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-upi-55">[55]</a></sup> Across the country, the storm damaged roads and bridges.<sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup></p>

<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Joan%E2%80%93Miriam" title="Hurricane Joan–Miriam">Hurricane Joan–Miriam</a></div>
<p>On October 10 the 17th tropical depression of the season organized from a disturbance in the ITCZ. For the next two&#160;days the system traveled northwest while it strengthened into Tropical Storm Joan.<sup id="cite_ref-joantcr7_58-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-joantcr7-58">[58]</a></sup> After passing through the southern Lesser Antilles, Joan traveled westward along the South American coast as a minimal tropical storm. It crossed the <a href="/wiki/Guajira_Peninsula" title="Guajira Peninsula">Guajira Peninsula</a> on October 17 and quickly attained hurricane strength just 30&#160;mi (48&#160;km) from the coast. Hurricane Joan strengthened into a major hurricane on October 19 while drifting westward. The hurricane executed a tight cyclonic loop in which it weakened greatly but rapidly strengthened upon resuming its westward track.<sup id="cite_ref-joantcr2_59-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-joantcr2-59">[59]</a></sup> Joan reached its peak intensity just before making landfall near <a href="/wiki/Bluefields" title="Bluefields">Bluefields</a>, Nicaragua, on October 22 as a Category&#160;4 hurricane. Joan at the time was the southernmost Category&#160;4 hurricane ever recorded, but this record has since been broken by <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Ivan" title="Hurricane Ivan">Hurricane Ivan</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-IvanTCR_60-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IvanTCR-60">[60]</a></sup> Joan remained well organized as it crossed Nicaragua and emerged in the eastern Pacific Ocean basin as <a href="/wiki/1988_Pacific_hurricane_season#Tropical_Storm_Miriam" title="1988 Pacific hurricane season">Tropical Storm Miriam</a>. Miriam gradually weakened until dissipating on November 2.</p>
<p>Hurricane Joan killed 148&#160;people in Nicaragua and 68&#160;others in affected nations.<sup id="cite_ref-joantcr3_61-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-joantcr3-61">[61]</a></sup> The hurricane damage in Nicaragua amounted to half of the $2&#160;billion (1988 USD; $4.05&#160;billion 2017&#160;USD) total. Joan also brought heavy rainfall and mudslides to countries along the extreme southern Caribbean.<sup id="cite_ref-joantcr2_59-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-joantcr2-59">[59]</a></sup> Its track along the northern coast of South America was very rare; Joan was one of only a few Atlantic tropical cyclones to move in this way.<sup id="cite_ref-joantcr1_62-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-joantcr1-62">[62]</a></sup> Joan was also the first tropical cyclone to cross from the Atlantic basin since <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Greta-Olivia" class="mw-redirect" title="Hurricane Greta-Olivia">Hurricane Greta</a> of 1978.<sup id="cite_ref-noaafaqe_63-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-noaafaqe-63">[63]</a></sup></p>

<p>A westward-moving tropical wave that left the coast of Africa in early October tracked closely behind Hurricane Joan through the southern Caribbean.<sup id="cite_ref-ap1020am_64-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ap1020am-64">[64]</a></sup> In an unusual occurrence the disturbance developed into the 18th tropical depression about 500&#160;mi (800&#160;km) behind the powerful hurricane. An Air Force reconnaissance check of tropical weather on October 19 spotted the depression near Colombia's Guajira Peninsula. Hurricane Joan's small size allowed the depression to remain out-of-reach as it developed. However, the outflow of the hurricane sheared the depression and sapped its energy.<sup id="cite_ref-upi1021pm_65-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-upi1021pm-65">[65]</a></sup> The system gradually dissipated on October 21 while Joan was experiencing rapid strengthening just before its arrival on the coast of Nicaragua.<sup id="cite_ref-joantcr2_59-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-joantcr2-59">[59]</a></sup> The depression brought heavy rain to the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands_Antilles" title="Netherlands Antilles">Netherlands Antilles</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-TDs1988_3-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TDs1988-3">[3]</a></sup> News reports blamed Tropical Depression Eighteen and other tropical systems for bringing swarms of <a href="/wiki/Desert_locust" title="Desert locust">pink locusts</a> from Africa to <a href="/wiki/Trinidad" title="Trinidad">Trinidad</a> and other Caribbean nations.<sup id="cite_ref-ap1020am_64-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ap1020am-64">[64]</a></sup></p>

<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Tropical_Storm_Keith_(1988)" title="Tropical Storm Keith (1988)">Tropical Storm Keith (1988)</a></div>
<p>The last storm of the season formed from a tropical wave on November 17 to the south of <a href="/wiki/Haiti" title="Haiti">Haiti</a>. It moved westward through the <a href="/wiki/Caribbean_Sea" title="Caribbean Sea">Caribbean Sea</a> and became organized enough to attain tropical storm status on November 20. Keith rapidly organized and peaked with winds of 70&#160;mph (110&#160;km/h) before making landfall on the northeastern portion of the <a href="/wiki/Yucat%C3%A1n_Peninsula" title="Yucatán Peninsula">Yucatán Peninsula</a> on November 21. An upper level trough forced it to the northeast, where upper-level shear and cooler drier air weakened it to minimal storm strength in a pattern typical for November.<sup id="cite_ref-mwr_4-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-4">[4]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-keithtcr1_66-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-keithtcr1-66">[66]</a></sup> Keith restrengthened over the southeastern Gulf of Mexico and struck near <a href="/wiki/Sarasota,_Florida" title="Sarasota, Florida">Sarasota, Florida</a>, on November 23. After crossing the state, it became <a href="/wiki/Extratropical" class="mw-redirect" title="Extratropical">extratropical</a> on November 24 near <a href="/wiki/Bermuda" title="Bermuda">Bermuda</a> and became an intense extratropical system over the Atlantic with sustained winds of minimal hurricane force.<sup id="cite_ref-mwr_4-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-4">[4]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-keithtcr2_67-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-keithtcr2-67">[67]</a></sup></p>
<p>Early in its duration Keith produced moderate to heavy rainfall in <a href="/wiki/Honduras" title="Honduras">Honduras</a>, Jamaica, and <a href="/wiki/Cuba" title="Cuba">Cuba</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-ap1120_68-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ap1120-68">[68]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-ap1122_69-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ap1122-69">[69]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-keithtcr3_70-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-keithtcr3-70">[70]</a></sup> Minimal damage was reported in <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">Mexico</a>, still recovering from the devastating effects of <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Gilbert" title="Hurricane Gilbert">Hurricane Gilbert</a> two&#160;months earlier.<sup id="cite_ref-gam1122_71-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gam1122-71">[71]</a></sup> Keith, the last of four named tropical cyclones to hit the United States during the season, produced moderate rainfall, a rough storm surge, and gusty winds across central Florida.<sup id="cite_ref-mwr_4-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-4">[4]</a></sup> Overall damage was widespread but fairly minor, totaling about $7.3&#160;million (1988&#160;USD, $12.7&#160;million 2007&#160;USD).<sup id="cite_ref-keithtcr3_70-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-keithtcr3-70">[70]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-damtotpi_72-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-damtotpi-72">[72]</a></sup> Damage near the coast occurred mainly from storm surge and beach erosion, while damage further inland was limited to flooding and downed trees and power lines. No fatalities were reported.<sup id="cite_ref-keithtcr3_70-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-keithtcr3-70">[70]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Retirement">Retirement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1988_Atlantic_hurricane_season&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Retirement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">See also: <a href="/wiki/List_of_retired_Atlantic_hurricane_names" title="List of retired Atlantic hurricane names">List of retired Atlantic hurricane names</a></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/World_Meteorological_Organization" title="World Meteorological Organization">World Meteorological Organization</a> retired two names in the spring of 1989: Gilbert and Joan. They were replaced by Gordon and Joyce in the <a href="/wiki/1994_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="1994 Atlantic hurricane season">1994 season</a>.</p>
