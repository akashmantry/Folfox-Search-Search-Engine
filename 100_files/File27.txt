<p>The 1989 season was the Phillies 107th season. The Phillies finished in 6th place in the <a href="/wiki/National_League_East" title="National League East">National League East</a> for the second consecutive season. It would also be <a href="/wiki/Mike_Schmidt" title="Mike Schmidt">Mike Schmidt</a>'s final season.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Regular_season">Regular season</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1989_Philadelphia_Phillies_season&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Regular season">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Injuries to Mike Schmidt's knees and back caused him to miss much of the 1988 season. After a poor start to the 1989 season, Schmidt chose to suddenly announce his retirement in <a href="/wiki/San_Diego,_California" class="mw-redirect" title="San Diego, California">San Diego</a>, on May 29. Known as "Captain Cool" by many in Philadelphia sports circles, Schmidt surprised many with an emotional, and occasionally tearful, retirement speech. In honor of his career, fans voted Schmidt to the <a href="/wiki/1989_Major_League_Baseball_All-Star_Game" title="1989 Major League Baseball All-Star Game">NL All-Star team</a>; Schmidt declined to play but was announced with the team. His last game was May 28, 1989, against the <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco_Giants" title="San Francisco Giants">San Francisco Giants</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Phillies played 163 regular season games due to a <a href="/wiki/Tie_(draw)#Baseball" title="Tie (draw)">tie game</a> on June 5 when they hosted the <a href="/wiki/Pittsburgh_Pirates" title="Pittsburgh Pirates">Pittsburgh Pirates</a>. With the score tied 3-3, the game ended due to rain after only 8 innings had been played.<sup id="cite_ref-1989stats_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1989stats-8">[8]</a></sup> Under the playing rules in place at the time, all player and team statistics generated during the tie game would stand (except since neither team had won, there could not be a winning or losing pitcher); a replacement game would be played at a later date. This game would mark the last regular season tie game for the Phillies as playing rules changed in 2007 allowing tie games to continue from the point of interruption at a later date.</p>
<p>On June 8, 1989, <a href="/wiki/Steve_Jeltz" title="Steve Jeltz">Steve Jeltz</a> hit two of his five career home runs in a game against the Pittsburgh Pirates, despite not starting the game. A switch-hitter, Jeltz hit one of these homers from the right side of the plate and one from the left side. This was the first time in the history of the Phillies that this feat had been accomplished. It was in this same game that, after the Pirates scored 10 runs in the top of the first, Pirate broadcaster <a href="/wiki/Jim_Rooker" title="Jim Rooker">Jim Rooker</a> said on-air, "If we lose this game, I'll walk home." The Phillies came back to win 15-11, and after the season Rooker conducted a 300-mile charity walk from Philadelphia to Pittsburgh.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Notable_transactions">Notable transactions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1989_Philadelphia_Phillies_season&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Notable transactions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul>
<li>June 1, 1989: <a href="/wiki/Shane_Turner" title="Shane Turner">Shane Turner</a> was traded by the Phillies to the <a href="/wiki/Baltimore_Orioles" title="Baltimore Orioles">Baltimore Orioles</a> for John Posey (minors).<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></li>
<li>June 2, 1989: <a href="/wiki/Chris_James_(baseball)" title="Chris James (baseball)">Chris James</a> was traded by the Phillies to the <a href="/wiki/San_Diego_Padres" title="San Diego Padres">San Diego Padres</a> for <a href="/wiki/John_Kruk" title="John Kruk">John Kruk</a> and <a href="/wiki/Randy_Ready" title="Randy Ready">Randy Ready</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></li>
<li>June 18, 1989: <a href="/wiki/Juan_Samuel" title="Juan Samuel">Juan Samuel</a> was traded by the Phillies to the <a href="/wiki/New_York_Mets" title="New York Mets">New York Mets</a> for <a href="/wiki/Lenny_Dykstra" title="Lenny Dykstra">Lenny Dykstra</a>, <a href="/wiki/Roger_McDowell" title="Roger McDowell">Roger McDowell</a> and a player to be named later. The Mets completed the trade by sending <a href="/wiki/Tom_Edens" title="Tom Edens">Tom Edens</a> to the Phillies on July 27.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></li>
<li>June 18, 1989: <a href="/wiki/Steve_Bedrosian" title="Steve Bedrosian">Steve Bedrosian</a> and a player to be named later were traded by the Phillies to the <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco_Giants" title="San Francisco Giants">San Francisco Giants</a> for <a href="/wiki/Charlie_Hayes" title="Charlie Hayes">Charlie Hayes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dennis_Cook" title="Dennis Cook">Dennis Cook</a> and <a href="/wiki/Terry_Mulholland" title="Terry Mulholland">Terry Mulholland</a>. The Phillies completed the deal by sending <a href="/wiki/Rick_Parker" title="Rick Parker">Rick Parker</a> to the Giants on August 7.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></li>
<li>August 7, 1989: Greg A. Harris was selected off waivers by the Boston Red Sox from the Philadelphia Phillies.<sup id="cite_ref-baseball-reference.com_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-baseball-reference.com-3">[3]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
