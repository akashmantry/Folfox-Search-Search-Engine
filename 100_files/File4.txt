<p>The <b>Đakovo concentration camp</b> (<a href="/wiki/Croatian_language" title="Croatian language">Croatian</a>: <span lang="hr" xml:lang="hr"><i>Đakovo koncentracioni logor</i></span>) was a <a href="/wiki/Concentration_camp" class="mw-redirect" title="Concentration camp">concentration camp</a> established in 1941 in <a href="/wiki/%C4%90akovo" title="Đakovo">Đakovo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Independent_State_of_Croatia" title="Independent State of Croatia">Independent State of Croatia</a> (modern-day <a href="/wiki/Croatia" title="Croatia">Croatia</a>). It was established in the deserted flour mill <i>Cereale</i> owned by the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic_Archdiocese_of_%C4%90akovo-Osijek" title="Roman Catholic Archdiocese of Đakovo-Osijek">Roman Catholic Diocese of Đakovo</a> although the Đakovo bishop, supported by <a href="/wiki/Archbishop_of_Zagreb" class="mw-redirect" title="Archbishop of Zagreb">Archbishop of Zagreb</a> <a href="/wiki/Aloysius_Stepinac" title="Aloysius Stepinac">Aloysius Stepinac</a>, firmly opposed the idea of <a href="/wiki/Usta%C5%A1e" title="Ustaše">Ustaše</a> to establish a concentration camp within the borders of the Bishop's domain. The camp was established on 1 December 1941 mainly for <a href="/wiki/Jews" title="Jews">Jewish</a> women and children, including some <a href="/wiki/Serbs" title="Serbs">Serb</a> girls. It was operational until its disestablishment on 7 July 1942. Around 3,000 women and children were its inmates and subjected to beatings, rapes, dog attacks and death from sickness and starvation. At least 650 women and children died in it. During the camp's disestablishment, the remaining inmates were transported to other camps and killed.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Establishment">Establishment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=%C4%90akovo_concentration_camp&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Establishment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Diocese of Đakovo did not give its approval for the establishment of the camp in their flour mill.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Archbishop_of_Zagreb" class="mw-redirect" title="Archbishop of Zagreb">Archbishop of Zagreb</a> <a href="/wiki/Aloysius_Stepinac" title="Aloysius Stepinac">Aloysius Stepinac</a> supported the office of the Đakovo bishop in their firm opposition to the idea of <a href="/wiki/Usta%C5%A1e" title="Ustaše">Ustaše</a> to establish a concentration camp within the borders of the Bishop's domain.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> In spite of their opposition, Ustaše established concentration camp in early December 1941<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> mostly for Jewish women and children, 3,000 of them being inmates of the camp.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Inmates">Inmates</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=%C4%90akovo_concentration_camp&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Inmates">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The first two transports of inmates brought 1,830 Jewish women and children and 50 Serb girls to Đakovo camp, followed by transport of about 1,200 women and children transported from <a href="/wiki/Stara_Gradi%C5%A1ka_concentration_camp" title="Stara Gradiška concentration camp">Stara Gradiška camp</a> on 24 February 1942.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>One fifth of inmates were registered as victims of this camp, most of them died after Ustaše took over complete control of the camp<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> on 29 March 1942.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> They began a terrible terror of camps inmates who were subjected to beatings, death from sickness, starvation, Ustaše threw bread crumbs among starving children and set guard dogs to attack them,<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> girls were raped and killed.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> More than 516 or 650<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> corps of people who died in Đakovo concentration camp are buried on Đakovo cemetery.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>Ustaše forcibly transported hundreds of people infected with typhus from Stara Gradiška to Đakovo camp to spread the disease.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Disestablishment">Disestablishment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=%C4%90akovo_concentration_camp&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Disestablishment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The camp was disestablished in period 15 June - 7 July 1942.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> The disestablishment was organized by Jozo Matković, Ustaše Lieutenant. The remaining inmates numbering between 2,000 and 3,000 Jewish women and children were transported to other camps and killed.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
