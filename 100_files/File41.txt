<p><i><b>A Song Flung Up to Heaven</b></i> is the sixth book in author <a href="/wiki/Maya_Angelou" title="Maya Angelou">Maya Angelou</a>'s series of <a href="/wiki/Autobiographies" class="mw-redirect" title="Autobiographies">autobiographies</a>. Set between 1965 and 1968, it begins where Angelou's previous book <i><a href="/wiki/All_God%27s_Children_Need_Traveling_Shoes" title="All God's Children Need Traveling Shoes">All God's Children Need Traveling Shoes</a></i> ends, with Angelou's trip from <a href="/wiki/Accra,_Ghana" class="mw-redirect" title="Accra, Ghana">Accra, Ghana</a>, where she had lived for the past four years, back to the United States. Two "calamitous events"<sup id="cite_ref-publishers_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-publishers-1">[1]</a></sup> frame the beginning and end of the book—the assassinations of <a href="/wiki/Malcolm_X" title="Malcolm X">Malcolm X</a> and <a href="/wiki/Martin_Luther_King,_Jr." class="mw-redirect" title="Martin Luther King, Jr.">Martin Luther King, Jr.</a> Angelou describes how she dealt with these events and the sweeping changes in both the country and in her personal life, and how she coped with her return home. The book ends with Angelou at "the threshold of her literary career",<sup id="cite_ref-long_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-long-2">[2]</a></sup> writing the opening lines to her first autobiography, <i><a href="/wiki/I_Know_Why_the_Caged_Bird_Sings" title="I Know Why the Caged Bird Sings">I Know Why the Caged Bird Sings</a></i>.</p>
<p>As she had begun to do in <i>Caged Bird</i>, and continued throughout her series, Angelou upheld the long tradition of African-American autobiography. At the same time she made a deliberate attempt to challenge the usual structure of the autobiography by critiquing, changing, and expanding the genre. Most reviewers agreed that the book was made up of a series of vignettes. By the time <i>Song</i> was written in 2002, sixteen years after her previous autobiography, Angelou had experienced great fame and recognition as an author and poet. She recited her poem <i><a href="/wiki/On_the_Pulse_of_Morning" title="On the Pulse of Morning">On the Pulse of Morning</a></i> at the inauguration of President <a href="/wiki/Bill_Clinton" title="Bill Clinton">Bill Clinton</a> in 1993, becoming the first poet to make an inaugural recitation since <a href="/wiki/Robert_Frost" title="Robert Frost">Robert Frost</a> at <a href="/wiki/John_F._Kennedy" title="John F. Kennedy">John F. Kennedy's</a> in 1961. She had become recognized and highly respected as a spokesperson for Blacks and women. Angelou was, as scholar Joanne Braxton has stated, "without a doubt,&#160;... America's most visible black woman autobiographer".<sup id="cite_ref-braxton_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-braxton-3">[3]</a></sup> She had also become, as reviewer <a href="/wiki/Richard_A._Long" title="Richard A. Long">Richard Long</a> stated, "a major autobiographical voice of the time".<sup id="cite_ref-long_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-long-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>The title of <i>Song</i> was based upon the same poem, by African-American poet <a href="/wiki/Paul_Lawrence_Dunbar" class="mw-redirect" title="Paul Lawrence Dunbar">Paul Lawrence Dunbar</a>, the basis of her first autobiography. Like Angelou's other autobiographies, the book was greeted with both praise and disappointment, although reviews were generally positive. Reviewers praised Angelou for "the culmination of a unique autobiographical achievement",<sup id="cite_ref-busby_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-busby-4">[4]</a></sup> while others criticized her for coming across as "smug".<sup id="cite_ref-mcwhorter-35_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mcwhorter-35-5">[5]</a></sup> The 2002 <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spoken-word_album&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Spoken-word album (page does not exist)">spoken-word album</a> by the same name, based on the book, received a <a href="/wiki/Grammy_Award_for_Best_Spoken_Word_Album" title="Grammy Award for Best Spoken Word Album">Grammy Award for Best Spoken Word Album</a> in 2003.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=A_Song_Flung_Up_to_Heaven&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>A Song Flung Up to Heaven</i> (2002) is the sixth of Maya Angelou's series of autobiographies, and at the time of its publication it was considered to be the final installment. It was completed 16 years after the publication of her previous autobiography, <i><a href="/wiki/All_God%27s_Children_Need_Traveling_Shoes" title="All God's Children Need Traveling Shoes">All God's Children Need Traveling Shoes</a></i> (1986) and over thirty years after the publication of her first, <i><a href="/wiki/I_Know_Why_the_Caged_Bird_Sings" title="I Know Why the Caged Bird Sings">I Know Why the Caged Bird Sings</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-gillespie-175_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gillespie-175-6">[6]</a></sup> Angelou wrote two collections of essays in the interim, <i><a href="/wiki/Wouldn%27t_Take_Nothing_for_My_Journey_Now" title="Wouldn't Take Nothing for My Journey Now">Wouldn't Take Nothing for My Journey Now</a></i> (1993) and <i><a href="/wiki/Even_the_Stars_Look_Lonesome" title="Even the Stars Look Lonesome">Even the Stars Look Lonesome</a></i> (1997), which writer <a href="/wiki/Hilton_Als" title="Hilton Als">Hilton Als</a> called her "wisdom books" and "homilies strung together with autobiographical texts".<sup id="cite_ref-songbird_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-songbird-7">[7]</a></sup> She also continued her poetry with several volumes, including a collection of her poems, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Complete_Collected_Poems_of_Maya_Angelou" title="The Complete Collected Poems of Maya Angelou">The Complete Collected Poems of Maya Angelou</a></i> (1994). In 1993, Angelou recited her poem <i><a href="/wiki/On_the_Pulse_of_Morning" title="On the Pulse of Morning">On the Pulse of Morning</a></i> at the inauguration of President <a href="/wiki/Bill_Clinton" title="Bill Clinton">Bill Clinton</a>, becoming the first poet to make an inaugural recitation since <a href="/wiki/Robert_Frost" title="Robert Frost">Robert Frost</a> at <a href="/wiki/John_F._Kennedy" title="John F. Kennedy">John F. Kennedy's</a> inauguration in 1961.<sup id="cite_ref-wordsmith_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wordsmith-8">[8]</a></sup> Her recitation resulted in more fame and recognition for her previous works, and broadened her appeal "across racial, economic, and educational boundaries".<sup id="cite_ref-berkman_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-berkman-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>By 2002, when <i>Song</i> was published, Angelou had become recognized and highly respected as a spokesperson for Blacks and women.<sup id="cite_ref-poetry_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-poetry-10">[10]</a></sup> She was, as scholar Joanne Braxton has stated, "without a doubt,&#160;... America's most visible black woman autobiographer".<sup id="cite_ref-braxton_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-braxton-3">[3]</a></sup> She had also become "a major autobiographical voice of the time".<sup id="cite_ref-long_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-long-2">[2]</a></sup> Angelou was one of the first African-American female writers to publicly discuss her personal life, and one of the first to use herself as a central character in her books.<sup id="cite_ref-songbird_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-songbird-7">[7]</a></sup> Writer Julian Mayfield, who called her first autobiography "a work of art that eludes description",<sup id="cite_ref-songbird_7-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-songbird-7">[7]</a></sup> stated that Angelou's series set a precedent not only for other Black women writers, but for the genre of autobiography as a whole.<sup id="cite_ref-songbird_7-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-songbird-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Als called Angelou one of the "pioneers of self-exposure", willing to focus honestly on the more negative aspects of her personality and choices.<sup id="cite_ref-songbird_7-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-songbird-7">[7]</a></sup> For example, while Angelou was composing her second autobiography, <i><a href="/wiki/Gather_Together_in_My_Name" title="Gather Together in My Name">Gather Together in My Name</a></i>, she was concerned about how her readers would react to her disclosure that she had been a prostitute. Her husband Paul Du Feu talked her into publishing the book by encouraging her to "tell the truth as a writer" and to "be honest about it".<sup id="cite_ref-lupton-14_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lupton-14-11">[11]</a></sup> "Song" took 16 years to write because it was so painful for her to relive the events she described, including the assassinations of <a href="/wiki/Malcolm_X" title="Malcolm X">Malcolm X</a> and <a href="/wiki/Martin_Luther_King,_Jr." class="mw-redirect" title="Martin Luther King, Jr.">Martin Luther King, Jr.</a><sup id="cite_ref-long_2-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-long-2">[2]</a></sup> She did not celebrate her birthday, April 4, for many years because it was also the anniversary of King's death, choosing instead to send his widow <a href="/wiki/Coretta_Scott_King" title="Coretta Scott King">Coretta Scott King</a> flowers.<sup id="cite_ref-80years_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-80years-12">[12]</a></sup> Although <i>Song</i> was considered the final installment in her series of autobiographies, Angelou continued writing about her life story through essays,<sup id="cite_ref-connolly_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-connolly-13">[13]</a></sup> and at the age of 85, published her seventh autobiography <i><a href="/wiki/Mom_%26_Me_%26_Mom" title="Mom &amp; Me &amp; Mom">Mom &amp; Me &amp; Mom</a></i> (2013), which focused on her relationship with her mother.<sup id="cite_ref-gilmor_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gilmor-14">[14]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Spoken_word_album" title="Spoken word album">spoken word album</a> based on <i>Song</i> and narrated by Angelou received a <a href="/wiki/Grammy_Award_for_Best_Spoken_Word_Album" title="Grammy Award for Best Spoken Word Album">Grammy Award for Best Spoken Word Album</a> in 2003.<sup id="cite_ref-winners_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-winners-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Title">Title</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=A_Song_Flung_Up_to_Heaven&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Title">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Paul_Laurence_Dunbar.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ac/Paul_Laurence_Dunbar.jpg/170px-Paul_Laurence_Dunbar.jpg" width="170" height="215" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ac/Paul_Laurence_Dunbar.jpg/255px-Paul_Laurence_Dunbar.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/a/ac/Paul_Laurence_Dunbar.jpg 2x" data-file-width="282" data-file-height="357" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Paul_Laurence_Dunbar.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Paul_Laurence_Dunbar" title="Paul Laurence Dunbar">Paul Laurence Dunbar</a>, whose poetry inspired both the titles of Angelou's first and sixth autobiographies in her series</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Angelou returned to the same poem she based the title of <i>Caged Bird</i> upon for the title of <i>A Song Flung Up to Heaven,</i> from the third stanza of the <a href="/wiki/Paul_Laurence_Dunbar" title="Paul Laurence Dunbar">Paul Laurence Dunbar</a> poem "Sympathy". Along with <a href="/wiki/Shakespeare" class="mw-redirect" title="Shakespeare">Shakespeare</a>, Angelou has credited Dunbar with forming her "writing ambition".<sup id="cite_ref-tate-158_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tate-158-16">[16]</a></sup> The caged bird, a symbol for the chained <a href="/wiki/Slave" class="mw-redirect" title="Slave">slave</a>, is an image Angelou uses throughout all her writings.<sup id="cite_ref-lupton-66_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lupton-66-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<blockquote>
<p>I know why the caged bird sings, ah me,<br />
When his wing is bruised and his bosom sore,<br />
When he beats his bars and would be free;<br />
It is not a carol of joy or glee,<br />
But a prayer that he sends from his heart's deep core,<br />
But a plea, that upward to Heaven he flings—<br />
I know why the caged bird sings.<sup id="cite_ref-dunbar_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dunbar-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Plot_summary">Plot summary</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=A_Song_Flung_Up_to_Heaven&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Plot summary">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>A Song Flung Up to Heaven</i>, which takes place between 1965 and 1968, picks up where Angelou's previous book, <i>All God's Children Need Traveling Shoes</i>, ends, with Angelou's airplane trip from <a href="/wiki/Accra,_Ghana" class="mw-redirect" title="Accra, Ghana">Accra, Ghana</a>, where she has spent the previous four years, back to the United States. Two "calamitous events"<sup id="cite_ref-publishers_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-publishers-1">[1]</a></sup> frame the beginning and end of the book—the assassinations of Malcolm X and Martin Luther King, Jr. Her nineteen-year-old son Guy has become an adult and is attending college in Ghana, and she is leaving a controlling relationship—her "romantic other", whom she described as "a powerful West African man who had swept into my life with the urgency of a Southern hurricane".<sup id="cite_ref-angelou-6_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-angelou-6-19">[19]</a></sup> She had also been invited to return to the US by Malcolm X, whom she had become friends with during his visit to Accra, to help her create the <a href="/wiki/Organization_of_African_Unity" class="mw-redirect" title="Organization of African Unity">Organization of African Unity</a>.</p>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:MLK_and_Malcolm_X_USNWR_cropped.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e1/MLK_and_Malcolm_X_USNWR_cropped.jpg/170px-MLK_and_Malcolm_X_USNWR_cropped.jpg" width="170" height="186" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e1/MLK_and_Malcolm_X_USNWR_cropped.jpg/255px-MLK_and_Malcolm_X_USNWR_cropped.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e1/MLK_and_Malcolm_X_USNWR_cropped.jpg/340px-MLK_and_Malcolm_X_USNWR_cropped.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2183" data-file-height="2392" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:MLK_and_Malcolm_X_USNWR_cropped.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The assassinations of <a href="/wiki/Martin_Luther_King,_Jr." class="mw-redirect" title="Martin Luther King, Jr.">Martin Luther King, Jr.</a> and <a href="/wiki/Malcolm_X" title="Malcolm X">Malcolm X</a> frame the events in <i>A Song Flung Up to Heaven</i>.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>She postpones meeting with Malcolm X for a month and visits her mother and brother in <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco" title="San Francisco">San Francisco</a>. Malcolm X is assassinated two days later. Devastated and grief-stricken, she moves to <a href="/wiki/Hawaii" title="Hawaii">Hawaii</a> to be near her brother and to resume her singing and performing career, which she had given up before leaving for Africa several years earlier. She realizes, after seeing <a href="/wiki/Della_Reese" title="Della Reese">Della Reese</a> perform, that she lacks the desire, commitment, and talent to be a singer. She instead returns to her writing career, but this time in <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles" title="Los Angeles">Los Angeles</a> instead of in <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a> as she had earlier in her life. To earn extra money, Angelou becomes a market researcher in Watts and gets to know the neighborhood and its people. She witnesses the 1965 <a href="/wiki/Watts_Riots" class="mw-redirect" title="Watts Riots">Watts Riots</a>, knowing that doing so could lead to her arrest, and she is genuinely disappointed that it does not.</p>
<p>At one point, Angelou's lover from Ghana, whom she calls "the African", arrives in Los Angeles to take her back to Accra. Angelou enlists the aid of her mother and brother; they come to her rescue once again by diverting the African first to Mexico and then back to Ghana. Guy, during a visit to his grandmother in San Francisco, gets into another car accident, similar to what happened before he began college in Ghana.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> His maturity is striking to his mother, and she leaves him in the care of his grandmother.</p>
<p>Angelou returns to New York, where she dedicates herself to her writing and renews many of the friendships made there in the past. She also describes her personal and professional relationships with <a href="/wiki/Ruby_Dee" title="Ruby Dee">Ruby Dee</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ossie_Davis" title="Ossie Davis">Ossie Davis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Beah_Richards" title="Beah Richards">Beah Richards</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Frank_Silvera" title="Frank Silvera">Frank Silvera</a>. Martin Luther King, Jr. asks her to travel around the country promoting the <a href="/wiki/Southern_Christian_Leadership_Conference" title="Southern Christian Leadership Conference">Southern Christian Leadership Conference</a>. She agrees, but "postpones again",<sup id="cite_ref-songbird_7-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-songbird-7">[7]</a></sup> and he is assassinated on her 40th birthday. Again devastated, she isolates herself until invited to a dinner party also attended by her friend <a href="/wiki/James_Baldwin" title="James Baldwin">James Baldwin</a> and cartoonist <a href="/wiki/Jules_Feiffer" title="Jules Feiffer">Jules Feiffer</a> and his wife Judy. Judy Feiffer, inspired by her tales about Angelou's childhood, contacts editor Robert Loomis, who challenges Angelou to write her autobiography as literature. She accepts his challenge, and <i>Song</i> ends with Angelou at "the threshold of her literary career",<sup id="cite_ref-long_2-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-long-2">[2]</a></sup> writing the opening lines to her first autobiography, <i>I Know Why the Caged Bird Sings</i>:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>"What are you looking at me for. I didn't come to stay".<sup id="cite_ref-angelou-210_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-angelou-210-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Style_and_genre">Style and genre</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=A_Song_Flung_Up_to_Heaven&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Style and genre">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">See also: <a href="/wiki/Themes_in_Maya_Angelou%27s_autobiographies" title="Themes in Maya Angelou's autobiographies">Themes in Maya Angelou's autobiographies</a></div>
<p>Starting with <i>Caged Bird</i>, Angelou made a deliberate attempt while writing all her autobiographies, including <i>Song</i>, to challenge the usual structure of the autobiography by critiquing, changing, and expanding the genre.<sup id="cite_ref-lupton-98_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lupton-98-22">[22]</a></sup> Her use of fiction-writing techniques such as dialogue, characterization, and thematic development has often led reviewers to categorize her books as <a href="/wiki/Autobiographical_fiction" class="mw-redirect" title="Autobiographical fiction">autobiographical fiction</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-lupton2930_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lupton2930-23">[23]</a></sup> Angelou stated in a 1989 interview that she was the only "serious" writer to choose the genre to express herself.<sup id="cite_ref-lupton-30_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lupton-30-24">[24]</a></sup> As critic Susan Gilbert stated, Angelou was reporting not one person's story, but the collective's.<sup id="cite_ref-gilbert_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gilbert-25">[25]</a></sup> Scholar <a href="/wiki/Selwyn_R._Cudjoe" class="mw-redirect" title="Selwyn R. Cudjoe">Selwyn R. Cudjoe</a> agreed, and viewed Angelou as representative of the convention in African-American autobiography as a public gesture that spoke for an entire group of people.<sup id="cite_ref-cudjoe_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cudjoe-26">[26]</a></sup> Angelou's editor <a href="/wiki/Robert_Loomis" title="Robert Loomis">Robert Loomis</a> was able to dare her into writing <i>Caged Bird</i> by challenging her to write an autobiography that could be considered "high art",<sup id="cite_ref-walker_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-walker-27">[27]</a></sup> which she continued throughout her series, including her final autobiography.<sup id="cite_ref-publishers_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-publishers-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Angelou's autobiographies conform to the genre's standard structure: they were written by a single author, they were chronological, and they contained elements of character, technique, and theme.<sup id="cite_ref-lupton-32_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lupton-32-28">[28]</a></sup> In a 1983 interview with African-American literature critic <a href="/wiki/Claudia_Tate" title="Claudia Tate">Claudia Tate</a>, Angelou called her books autobiographies.<sup id="cite_ref-tate-153_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tate-153-29">[29]</a></sup> When speaking of her unique use of the genre, Angelou acknowledged that she has followed the <a href="/wiki/Slave_narrative" title="Slave narrative">slave narrative</a> tradition of "speaking in the first-person singular talking about the first-person plural, always saying I meaning 'we'".<sup id="cite_ref-poetry_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-poetry-10">[10]</a></sup> Reviewer <a href="/wiki/Elsie_B._Washington" title="Elsie B. Washington">Elsie B. Washington</a> agreed, and stated that <i>A Song Flung Up to Heaven</i> "offers a glimpse into the life of a literary icon in the making" influenced by historical events and personalities such as Malcolm X, Martin Luther King, Jr., and James Baldwin.<sup id="cite_ref-washington-57_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-washington-57-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p>Angelou recognized that there were fictional aspects to all her books; she tended to "diverge from the conventional notion of autobiography as truth".<sup id="cite_ref-lupton-34_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lupton-34-31">[31]</a></sup> Her approach paralleled the conventions of many African-American autobiographies written during the <a href="/wiki/Abolitionism_in_the_United_States" title="Abolitionism in the United States">abolitionist</a> period in the US, when truth was often censored for purposes of self-protection.<sup id="cite_ref-crispin-26_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-crispin-26-32">[32]</a></sup> Author Lyman B. Hagen has placed Angelou in the long tradition of African-American autobiography, but insisted that she has created a unique interpretation of the autobiographical form.<sup id="cite_ref-hagen67_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hagen67-33">[33]</a></sup> In a 1998 interview with journalist <a href="/wiki/George_Plimpton" title="George Plimpton">George Plimpton</a>, Angelou discussed her writing process, and "the sometimes slippery notion of truth in nonfiction" and memoirs.<sup id="cite_ref-journalism_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-journalism-34">[34]</a></sup> When asked if she changed the truth to improve her story, she stated, "Sometimes I make a diameter from a composite of three or four people, because the essence in only one person is not sufficiently strong to be written about."<sup id="cite_ref-plimpton_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-plimpton-35">[35]</a></sup> Although Angelou has never admitted to changing the facts in her stories, she has used these facts to make an impact with the reader. As Hagen stated, "One can assume that 'the essence of the data' is present in Angelou's work".<sup id="cite_ref-hagen-18_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hagen-18-36">[36]</a></sup> Hagen also stated that Angelou "fictionalizes, to enhance interest".<sup id="cite_ref-hagen-18_36-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hagen-18-36">[36]</a></sup> Angelou's long-time editor, Robert Loomis, agreed, stating that she could rewrite any of her books by changing the order of her facts to make a different impact on the reader.<sup id="cite_ref-hagen-18_36-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hagen-18-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Critical_reception">Critical reception</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=A_Song_Flung_Up_to_Heaven&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Critical reception">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Like Angelou's previous autobiographies, <i>Song</i> received mostly positive reviews, although as the <a href="/wiki/Poetry_Foundation" title="Poetry Foundation">Poetry Foundation</a> has said: "Most critics have judged Angelou's subsequent autobiographies in light of her first, and <i>I Know Why the Caged Bird Sings</i> remains the most highly praised."<sup id="cite_ref-poetry_10-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-poetry-10">[10]</a></sup> Kim Hubbard of <i><a href="/wiki/People_Magazine" class="mw-redirect" title="People Magazine">People Magazine</a></i>, for example, found <i>Song</i> unsatisfying and "hastily assembled", but poetic like <i>Caged Bird</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-hubbard_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hubbard-37">[37]</a></sup> Many reviewers appreciated what <i>Kirkus Reviews</i> called Angelou's "nice structural turn" of framing <i>Song</i> with two assassinations.<sup id="cite_ref-kirkus_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kirkus-38">[38]</a></sup> Paula Friedman of <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times_Book_Review" title="The New York Times Book Review">The New York Times Book Review</a></i> appreciated Angelou's "occasions of critical self-assessment and modesty" not present in many other autobiographies.<sup id="cite_ref-friedman_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-friedman-39">[39]</a></sup> Patricia Elam of <i>New Crisis</i> agreed, stating that there is much to admire both about the book and about the "large life", full of tension, laughter, and love, it describes. Elam also called <i>Song</i> "a spirit-moving work that describes Angelou's journey through an authentic and artistic life".<sup id="cite_ref-elam_40-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-elam-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
<div class="quotebox" style="float: right; clear: right; margin: 0.5em 0 0.8em 1.4em; width:40%; padding: 6px; border: 1px solid #aaa; font-size: 88%; background-color: #F9F9F9;">
<div class="" style="position: relative; text-align: left;">
<div>
<p>This new book is like an inspired conversation—the kind you might have with a wise and comfortable stranger seated next to you on a long bus ride. It doesn't wrench or disturb the way her first book did, but instead shares with subtlety and sometimes intimacy, experiences from a well-traveled life. Rather than struggling to recall the details of a distant past, Angelou sketches the scenes with broad familiarity, as if using a memory brush, and the reader is transported just the same.</p>
</div>
</div>
<cite style="display: block; text-align: left;">Patricia Elam, <i>New Crisis</i> (2002)<sup id="cite_ref-elam_40-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-elam-40">[40]</a></sup></cite></div>
<p>Reviewer <a href="/wiki/Margaret_Busby" title="Margaret Busby">Margaret Busby</a>, who saw this book "not so much an ending as a beginning", called <i>Song</i> "the culmination of a unique autobiographical achievement, a glorious celebration of indomitable spirit".<sup id="cite_ref-busby_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-busby-4">[4]</a></sup> Like other reviewers, Busby considered <i>Song</i> a series of "beautifully crafted vignettes" and found the book concise and readable.<sup id="cite_ref-busby_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-busby-4">[4]</a></sup> Scholar <a href="/wiki/John_McWhorter" title="John McWhorter">John McWhorter</a> did not look at Angelou's use of vignettes as positively, and stated that all of her books were short, divided into "ever shorter" chapters as her series progressed, and "sometimes seem written for children rather than adults".<sup id="cite_ref-mcwhorter-40_41-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mcwhorter-40-41">[41]</a></sup> McWhorter recognized, however, that Angelou's precise prose and "striking and even jarring simplicity" <sup id="cite_ref-mcwhorter-40_41-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mcwhorter-40-41">[41]</a></sup> was due to Angelou's purposes of depicting African-American culture in a positive way. Busby also recognized Angelou's ability to find inspirational lessons from adversity, both nationally and personally, although the emphasis in this book was on the personal, especially her dilemmas as a mother and as a lover.<sup id="cite_ref-busby_4-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-busby-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Amy Strong of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Library_Journal" class="mw-redirect" title="The Library Journal">The Library Journal</a></i>, perhaps because Angelou's life during the time the book took place was full of more personal loss than conflict and struggle, considered <i>Song</i> less profound and intense than the previous books in Angelou's series. She predicted that <i>Song</i>′s direct and plainspoken style would be popular.<sup id="cite_ref-strong_42-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-strong-42">[42]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Publishers_Weekly" title="Publishers Weekly">Publishers Weekly</a></i>, in its review of the book, agreed with Strong and saw "a certain resignation" in <i>Song</i>, instead of "the contentiousness" in Angelou's other autobiographies. The reviewer also stated that those who lived through the era Angelou described would appreciate her assessment of it, and that <i>Song</i> was "a story of tragedy and triumph, well stated and clearly stamped by her own unique blend of Afro-Americanism".<sup id="cite_ref-publishers_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-publishers-1">[1]</a></sup> The assassinations in <i>Song</i> provided the book with depth as Angelou described the events of her life, which would be "mere meanderings" if described by a less skilled writer. The reviewer was able to see Angelou's "gracious spirit" and found the book "satisfying", although he considered it a "sometimes flat account" that lacked "the spiritual tone of Angelou's essays, the openness of her poetry and the drama of her other autobiographies".<sup id="cite_ref-publishers_1-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-publishers-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Both McWhorter and scholar Hilton Als found Angelou's writing throughout her series self-important. Although McWhorter has admitted to being charmed by Angelou's sense of authority she has inserted into her works, which he calls her "black-mother wit", he considered Angelou's autobiographies after <i>Caged Bird</i> "smug", and has stated that she "implicitly dares the reader to question her private line to God and Truth".<sup id="cite_ref-mcwhorter-35_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mcwhorter-35-5">[5]</a></sup> Als agreed, stating what made <i>Song</i> different from her preceding volumes is her "ever-increasing unreliability".<sup id="cite_ref-songbird_7-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-songbird-7">[7]</a></sup> Als stated that Angelou, in her six autobiographies, "has given us&#160;... the self-aggrandizing, homespun, and sometimes oddly prudish story of a black woman who, when faced with the trials of life, simply makes do".<sup id="cite_ref-songbird_7-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-songbird-7">[7]</a></sup> Als believed that Angelou's essays, written in the 1990s, were a better culmination of her work as an autobiographer.<sup id="cite_ref-songbird_7-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-songbird-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
