<p><b>Abū Ṭālib <a href="/wiki/Patronymic#Arabic" title="Patronymic">ibn</a> ‘Abd al-Muṭṭalib</b> (<a href="/wiki/Arabic_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Arabic language">Arabic</a>: <span lang="ar" dir="rtl" xml:lang="ar">ابو طالب بن عبد المطلب</span>‎‎; <abbr title="circa">c.</abbr><span style="white-space:nowrap;"> 539</span>&#160;– <abbr title="circa">c.</abbr><span style="white-space:nowrap;"> 619</span>), <i><a href="/wiki/N%C3%A9e" class="mw-redirect" title="Née">née</a></i> <b>‘Imran</b> <a href="/wiki/Arabic_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Arabic language">Arabic</a>: <span lang="ar" dir="rtl" xml:lang="ar">عِـمـران</span>‎‎) or <b>‘Abd Manaf</b> (<a href="/wiki/Arabic_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Arabic language">Arabic</a>: <span lang="ar" dir="rtl" xml:lang="ar">عَـبـد مَـنـاف</span>‎‎),<sup id="cite_ref-Duas2013_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Duas2013-1">[1]</a></sup> was the leader of <a href="/wiki/Banu_Hashim" title="Banu Hashim">Banu Hashim</a>, a clan of the <a href="/wiki/Quraysh_(tribe)" class="mw-redirect" title="Quraysh (tribe)">Qurayshi tribe</a> of <a href="/wiki/Mecca" title="Mecca">Mecca</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Hijaz" class="mw-redirect" title="Hijaz">Hijaz</a>, <a href="/wiki/Arabian_Peninsula" title="Arabian Peninsula">Arabian Peninsula</a>. After the death of his father ‘<a href="/wiki/Abd_al-Muttalib" class="mw-redirect" title="Abd al-Muttalib">Abd al-Muttalib</a>, he inherited this position and the offices of <i>Siqaya</i> and <i>Rifada</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Encyclopaedia_of_Islam_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Encyclopaedia_of_Islam-2">[2]</a></sup> He was well respected in Mecca despite a declining fortune.<sup id="cite_ref-Armstrong_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Armstrong-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Abu Talib was an uncle of the <a href="/wiki/Prophets_and_messengers_in_Islam" title="Prophets and messengers in Islam">Islamic <i>Nabi</i></a> (<a href="/wiki/Arabic_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Arabic language">Arabic</a>: <span lang="ar" dir="rtl" xml:lang="ar">نَـبِي</span>‎‎, <a href="/wiki/Prophet" title="Prophet">Prophet</a>) <a href="/wiki/Muhammad_in_Islam" title="Muhammad in Islam">Muhammad</a>, and father of the <a href="/wiki/Rashidun_Caliph" class="mw-redirect" title="Rashidun Caliph">Rashid Caliph</a> ‘<a href="/wiki/Ali" title="Ali">Ali</a>, who is also regarded as the first <a href="/wiki/Shia_Islam" title="Shia Islam">Shi'ite</a> Imam. There is a great debate among Muslim scholars on whether he died a Muslim or a non-Muslim.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Relationship_with_Muhammad">Relationship with Muhammad</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Abu_Talib_ibn_Abd_al-Muttalib&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Relationship with Muhammad">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Abū Ṭālib was a brother of <a href="/wiki/Muhammad" title="Muhammad">Muhammad</a>'s father, <a href="/wiki/Abdullah_ibn_Abdul-Muttalib" title="Abdullah ibn Abdul-Muttalib">'Abdullāh ibn Abdul-Muttalib</a>, who had died before Muhammad's birth. After the death of Muhammad's mother <a href="/wiki/Aminah_bint_Wahab" class="mw-redirect" title="Aminah bint Wahab">Āminah bint Wahb</a>, Muhammad as a child was taken into the care of his grandfather, ‘Abdul-Muttalib. When Muhammad reached eight years of age, 'Abdul-Muttalib died. One of Muhammad's uncles was to take him in. The oldest, Al-Harith was not wealthy enough to take him in. Abu Talib, despite his poverty, took in Muhammad because of his generosity.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Although Abu Talib was responsible for <i>Siqaya</i> and <i>Rifada</i> (Food and Beverages) of <a href="/wiki/Hajj" title="Hajj">Hajj</a> pilgrims, he was poor.</p>
<p>Muhammad loved his uncle very much, and Abu Talib loved him in return.<sup id="cite_ref-Rubin_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rubin-5">[5]</a></sup> Abu Talib is remembered as a gifted poet, and many poetic verses in support of Muhammad are attributed to him.<sup id="cite_ref-Encyclopaedia_of_Islam_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Encyclopaedia_of_Islam-2">[2]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Lings_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lings-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Once, as Abu Talib was about to leave for a trading expedition, Muhammad wept and could not bear to be separated from him. To this Abu Talib responded, "By God I will take him with me, and we shall never part from each other." <sup id="cite_ref-Tabari_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tabari-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Later in life, as an adult, Muhammad saw that Abu Talib was struggling financially after a severe drought. Muhammad decided to take charge of one of Abu Talib's children and he convinced <a href="/wiki/Al-%E2%80%98Abbas_ibn_%E2%80%98Abd_al-Muttalib" title="Al-‘Abbas ibn ‘Abd al-Muttalib">al-'Abbas</a> to do the same. They discussed this matter with Abū Ṭālib, who asked that his favorite child 'Aqīl be left with him. Al-'Abbās chose <a href="/wiki/Ja%27far_ibn_Abi_Talib" title="Ja'far ibn Abi Talib">Ja'far</a>, and Muhammad chose <a href="/wiki/Ali_ibn_Abu_Talib" class="mw-redirect" title="Ali ibn Abu Talib">'Alī</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-ref3_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ref3-9">[9]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-firstMuslims_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-firstMuslims-10">[10]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Protecting_Muhammad">Protecting Muhammad</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Abu_Talib_ibn_Abd_al-Muttalib&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Protecting Muhammad">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In tribal society, a tribal affiliation is important, otherwise a man can be killed with impunity.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> As leader of the Banu Hashim, Abu Talib acted as a protector to Muhammad. After Muhammad began preaching the message of <a href="/wiki/Islam" title="Islam">Islam</a>, members of the other <a href="/wiki/Quraysh_tribe" class="mw-redirect" title="Quraysh tribe">Qurayshite</a> clans increasingly came to feel threatened by Muḥammad. In attempts to quiet him, they pressured Abū Ṭālib to silence his nephew or control him. Despite these pressures, Abū Ṭālib maintained his support of Muḥammad, defending him from the other leaders of the Quraysh. Leaders of the Quraysh directly confronted Abu Talib several times. Abu Talib brushed them off and continued to support Muhammad even when it put a rift between him and the Quraysh. In one account, the Quraysh even threatened to fight the Banu Hashim over this conflict.<sup id="cite_ref-Rubin_1995_150_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rubin_1995_150-14">[14]</a></sup> In a particular narration of one such confrontation, Abu Talib summoned Muhammad to speak with the Quraysh. Muhammad asked the Quraysh leaders to say the <a href="/wiki/Shahada" title="Shahada">shahada</a> and they were astounded.<sup id="cite_ref-The_History_of_al-Tabari_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-The_History_of_al-Tabari-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Quraysh even tried to bribe Abu Talib. The Quraysh told Abu Talib that if he let them handle Muhammad he could adopt 'Umarah ibn al Walid ibn al Mughirah, the handsomest youth in Quraysh.<sup id="cite_ref-Rubin_1995_150_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rubin_1995_150-14">[14]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>When this also failed, the Quraysh elicited the support of other tribes to boycott trading with or marrying members of the Banu Hashim lineage. This boycott started seven years after Muhammad first received revelation and lasted for three years.<sup id="cite_ref-Encyclopaedia_of_Islam_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Encyclopaedia_of_Islam-2">[2]</a></sup> The goal was to put pressure on the Hashimites and even starve them into submission.<sup id="cite_ref-Armstrong_1993_129_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Armstrong_1993_129-18">[18]</a></sup> For the sake of security many members of the Banu Hashim moved near to Abu Talib (Encyclopedia of Islam) and the place became like a ghetto.<sup id="cite_ref-Armstrong_1993_129_18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Armstrong_1993_129-18">[18]</a></sup> This didn't cause undue hardship <sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> because many had family members in other tribes that would smuggle goods to them.<sup id="cite_ref-Armstrong_1993_129_18-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Armstrong_1993_129-18">[18]</a></sup> Abu Talib's brother, <a href="/wiki/Abu_Lahab" class="mw-redirect" title="Abu Lahab">Abu Lahab</a>, sided with the Quraysh on this issue; he moved to a house in the district of Abd Shams to demonstrate support for the Quraysh.<sup id="cite_ref-Armstrong_1993_129_18-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Armstrong_1993_129-18">[18]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> He thought Muhammad was either mad or an impostor.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>Protecting Muhammad put considerable pressure on Abu Talib and the Banu Hashim. In one instance Abu Talib exclaimed to Muhammad, "Save me and yourself, and do not put a greater burden on me than I cannot bear". Muhammad responded, "Oh uncle! By God Almighty I swear, even if they should put the sun in my right hand and the moon in my left that I abjure this cause, I shall not do so until God has vindicated it or caused me to perish in the process."<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> Seeing his nephew's emotion, Abu Talib responded, "Go, nephew, and say what you like. By God, I will never hand you over for any reason."<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Death">Death</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Abu_Talib_ibn_Abd_al-Muttalib&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Death">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Abū Ṭālib's died circa 619, at more than 80 years of age, about 10 years after the start of Muhammad's mission.<sup id="cite_ref-Encyclopaedia_of_Islam_2-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Encyclopaedia_of_Islam-2">[2]</a></sup> This year is known as the <a href="/wiki/Year_of_Sorrow" title="Year of Sorrow">Year of Sorrow</a> for Muhammad, because not only did his uncle Abu Talib die, but also his wife <a href="/wiki/Khadijah_bint_Khuwaylid" class="mw-redirect" title="Khadijah bint Khuwaylid">Khadijah bint Khuwaylid</a>, within a month of Abu Talib.</p>
<p>Before Abu Talib died, Muhammad asked him to pronounce the <a href="/wiki/Shahadah" class="mw-redirect" title="Shahadah">Shahadah</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-The_History_of_al-Tabari_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-The_History_of_al-Tabari-15">[15]</a></sup> In another tradition Abu Talib was dissuaded from saying the Shahadah by the Quraysh, false statement.<sup id="cite_ref-Donner_245_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Donner_245-24">[24]</a></sup> According to the historiographer Fred McGraw Donner, both of these traditions have very old <a href="/wiki/Isnad" class="mw-redirect" title="Isnad">isnads</a> but the first variation has two different isnads which might suggest that the second variation is a modification of the older, first variation.<sup id="cite_ref-Donner_245_24-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Donner_245-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>In yet another variation of Abu Talib's death, al-'Abbas, who was sitting next to Abu Talib as he died, saw Abu Talib moving his lips. Al-'Abbas claimed that Abu Talib had said the shahada but Muhammad replied that he had not heard it.<sup id="cite_ref-Encyclopaedia_of_Islam_2-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Encyclopaedia_of_Islam-2">[2]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> Some Muslims see this as proof that Abu Talib died a Muslim. However, the majority of sources state that Abu Talib died a pagan.</p>
<p>After Abu Talib's death, Muhammad was left unprotected. Abu Talib's brother and successor as the Chief of the family, that is Abu Lahab, did not protect him, as he was an enemy of Muhammad, so Muhammad and his followers faced incredible persecution. Muhammad is quoted as exclaiming, "By God, Quraysh never harmed me so much as after the death of Abu Talib."<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> The early Muslims relocated to Medina in order to escape persecution by the Quraysh.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sunni_and_Shi.E2.80.98ite_views">Sunni and Shi‘ite views</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Abu_Talib_ibn_Abd_al-Muttalib&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Sunni and Shi‘ite views">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The memory of Abu Talib is influenced by political aims of the Sunnis and Shias.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> The character of Abu Talib was elemental in the <a href="/wiki/Abbasid" class="mw-redirect" title="Abbasid">Abbasid</a>/Shia power struggle.</p>
<p>Originally, the Abbasids, being Shi‘ites, worked with Ajamis to overthrow the <a href="/wiki/Umayyad" class="mw-redirect" title="Umayyad">Umayyad</a> dynasty, and both tried to legitimize their claim to power through ancestral relationship to Muhammad.<sup id="cite_ref-Donner_237_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Donner_237-30">[30]</a></sup> The Abbasids traced their ancestry to <a href="/wiki/Al-Abbas_ibn_Abd_al-Muttalib" class="mw-redirect" title="Al-Abbas ibn Abd al-Muttalib">al-Abbas</a>, while the Shias traced their ancestry to 'Ali, son of Abu Talib. Therefore, in order to assert their credibility, the Abbasids (who embraced Sunni Islam) tried to discredit Abu Talib by emphasizing that he died a pagan.<sup id="cite_ref-Donner_245_24-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Donner_245-24">[24]</a></sup> All historical error can say history is always having different angles.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Historical_Sunni_view">Historical Sunni view</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Abu_Talib_ibn_Abd_al-Muttalib&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Historical Sunni view">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>It is reporeted in the Sunni hadith collections of Sahih Muslim and Bukhari that the Quranic verse 28:56 ("O Prophet! Verily, you guide not whom you like, but Allah guides whom He will") was revealed concerning Abu Talib's rejection of Islam at the hands of his nephew.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Abbasids tried to emphasize Muhammad's disappointment in Abu Talib; in one tradition recorded by the historian <a href="/wiki/Al-Mada%27ini" title="Al-Mada'ini">al-Mada'ini</a>, and widely circulated by the Abbasids, one of two men states, "I wish that Abu Talib had embraced Islam, for the Apostle of God would have been delighted at that. But he was an unbeliever."<sup id="cite_ref-Donner_238_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Donner_238-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<p>Along the same lines, there is a similar account where 'Ali informs Muhammad of Abu Talib's death by saying, "Your uncle, the erring old man, has died."<sup id="cite_ref-Donner_239_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Donner_239-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Historical_Shi.E2.80.98ite_view">Historical Shi‘ite view</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Abu_Talib_ibn_Abd_al-Muttalib&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Historical Shi‘ite view">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Shi‘ites believe that the father of the first imam, 'Ali, must be nearly as great as the imam himself. Shias elevate Abu Talib and see him as a heroic defender of Muhammad. Many sources from this perspective claim that Abu Talib was indeed Muslim, he just kept his faith a secret so that he could better protect Muhammad (150 Rubin).</p>
<p>In one account, when Abu Talib was ill, Muhammad fed grapes to him that God forbade unbelievers to eat. This implies that Abu Talib had accepted Islam despite his outward actions.<sup id="cite_ref-Donner_239_34-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Donner_239-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<p>Some Shi‘ites go so far as to insist that even the ancestors of Abu Talib were Muslim. Abu Talib was a descendant of <a href="/wiki/Ishmael_in_Islam" title="Ishmael in Islam">Isma‘il</a> <a href="/wiki/Patronymic#Arabic" title="Patronymic">ibn</a> <a href="/wiki/Abraham_in_Islam" title="Abraham in Islam">Ibrahim</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-Donner_2405_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Donner_2405-35">[35]</a></sup> and Shi‘ites believe that the "divine transmigration of the spirit" is applied to ancestors as well as descendants.<sup id="cite_ref-Armstrong2_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Armstrong2-36">[36]</a></sup> However, according to the <a href="/wiki/Qur%E2%80%99an" class="mw-redirect" title="Qur’an">Qur’an</a>, Ibrahim's father <a href="/wiki/Terah#Islamic_tradition" title="Terah">Azar</a> was an <a href="/wiki/Shirk_(Islam)" title="Shirk (Islam)">Idolator</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kafir" title="Kafir">Disbeliever</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Cite_quran.7C6.7C74.7Ce.3D90.7Cs.3Dns_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cite_quran.7C6.7C74.7Ce.3D90.7Cs.3Dns-37">[37]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Cite_quran.7C9.7C113.7Ce.3D114.7Cs.3Dns_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cite_quran.7C9.7C113.7Ce.3D114.7Cs.3Dns-38">[38]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Cite_quran.7C19.7C41.7Ce.3D50.7Cs.3Dns_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cite_quran.7C19.7C41.7Ce.3D50.7Cs.3Dns-39">[39]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Ibn_Kathir.2C_QAN_40-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ibn_Kathir.2C_QAN-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
<p>Others claimed that even if Abu Talib did not accept Islam, he did a great service to Islam by protecting Muhammad from the Quraysh, sentence itself is confirm that only Muslim can do what Abu Talib manage. They put the blame of Abu Talib's failure to convert on the Quarysh, no need to blame as this is a close chapter Abu Talib was not to blame.<sup id="cite_ref-Donner_2405_35-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Donner_2405-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Family">Family</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Abu_Talib_ibn_Abd_al-Muttalib&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Family">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Abu Talib was married to <a href="/wiki/Fatimah_bint_Asad" title="Fatimah bint Asad">Fatimah bint Asad</a>. They had four sons:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Talib_ibn_Abi_Talib" title="Talib ibn Abi Talib">Ṭālib ibn Abī Ṭālib</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Aqeel_ibn_Abi_Talib" title="Aqeel ibn Abi Talib">'Aqīl ibn Abī Ṭālib</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Ja%60far_bin_Abi_Talib" class="mw-redirect" title="Ja`far bin Abi Talib">Ja'far ibn Abī Ṭālib</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Ali_ibn_Abi_Talib" class="mw-redirect" title="Ali ibn Abi Talib">'Alī ibn Abī Ṭālib</a></li>
</ul>
<p>and three daughters:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Fakhitah_bint_Abi_Talib" title="Fakhitah bint Abi Talib">Fākhitah bint Abī Ṭālib</a> (Umm Hānī)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Jumanah_bint_Abi_Talib" title="Jumanah bint Abi Talib">Jumānah bint Abī Ṭālib</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Rayta_bint_Abi_Talib" title="Rayta bint Abi Talib">Rayta bint Abī Ṭālib (Asma or Umm Talib)</a></li>
</ul>
