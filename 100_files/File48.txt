<p><i><b>Acacia</b></i>, commonly known as the <b>wattles</b> or <b>acacias</b>, is a large <a href="/wiki/Genus" title="Genus">genus</a> of shrubs, <a href="/wiki/Liana" title="Liana">lianas</a> and trees in the subfamily Mimosoideae of the pea family <a href="/wiki/Fabaceae" title="Fabaceae">Fabaceae</a>. Initially it comprised a group of plant species native to Africa and Australia, with the first species <i><a href="/wiki/Vachellia_nilotica" title="Vachellia nilotica">A. nilotica</a></i> described by Linnaeus. Controversy erupted in the early 2000s when it became evident that the genus as it stood was not <a href="/wiki/Monophyly" title="Monophyly">monophyletic</a>, and that several divergent lineages needed to be placed in separate genera. It turned out that one lineage comprising over 900 species mainly native to Australia was not closely related to the mainly African lineage that contained <i>A. nilotica</i>—the first and <a href="/wiki/Type_species" title="Type species">type species</a>. This meant that the Australian lineage (by far the most prolific in number of species) would need to be renamed. Botanist Les Pedley named this group <i><b>Racosperma</b></i>, which was inconsistently adopted. Australian botanists proposed that this would be more disruptive than setting a different type species (<a href="/wiki/Acacia_penninervis" title="Acacia penninervis"><i>A. penninervis</i></a>) and allowing this large number of species to remain <i>Acacia</i>, resulting in the African lineage being renamed to <i><a href="/wiki/Vachellia" title="Vachellia">Vachellia</a></i>. This was officially adopted, but many botanists from Africa and elsewhere disagreed that this was necessary.</p>
<p>A number of species have been introduced to various parts of the world, and two million hectares of commercial plantations have been established.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> The heterogeneous group<sup id="cite_ref-mur_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mur-4">[4]</a></sup> varies considerably in habit, from mat-like <a href="/wiki/Subshrub" title="Subshrub">subshrubs</a> to canopy trees in forest.<sup id="cite_ref-orch1_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-orch1-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy">Taxonomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Acacia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The genus was first described from Africa by C. F. P. von Martius in 1829. Several hundred combinations in <i>Acacia</i> were published by Pedley in 2003.<sup id="cite_ref-ped1_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ped1-1">[1]</a></sup> The genus of 981<sup id="cite_ref-ped2_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ped2-6">[6]</a></sup> species, <i><a href="/wiki/Acacia_sensu_lato" title="Acacia sensu lato">Acacia s.l.</a></i>, in the subfamily Mimosoideae of the pea family Fabaceae is <a href="/wiki/Monophyly" title="Monophyly">monophyletic</a>. All but 10 of its species are native to <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-ped2_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ped2-6">[6]</a></sup> where it constitutes the largest plant genus.<sup id="cite_ref-mur_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mur-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Following a controversial decision to choose a new <a href="/wiki/Type_(biology)" title="Type (biology)">type</a> for <i>Acacia</i> in 2005, the Australian component of <i>Acacia s.l.</i> now retains the name <i>Acacia</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-thiel_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-thiel-7">[7]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-brum_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-brum-8">[8]</a></sup> At the 2011 <a href="/wiki/International_Botanical_Congress" title="International Botanical Congress">International Botanical Congress</a> held in <a href="/wiki/Melbourne" title="Melbourne">Melbourne</a>, the decision to use the name <i>Acacia</i>, rather than the proposed <i>Racosperma</i> for this genus, was upheld.<sup id="cite_ref-acaciaresolution1_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-acaciaresolution1-9">[9]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-acaciaresolution2_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-acaciaresolution2-10">[10]</a></sup> Other <i><a href="/wiki/Acacia_sensu_lato" title="Acacia sensu lato">Acacia s.l.</a></i> taxa continue to be called <i>Acacia</i> by those who choose to consider the entire group as one genus.<sup id="cite_ref-acaciaresolution2_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-acaciaresolution2-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>Australian species of the genus <i><a href="/wiki/Paraserianthes" title="Paraserianthes">Paraserianthes</a></i> <i><a href="/wiki/Sensu_lato" class="mw-redirect" title="Sensu lato">s.l.</a></i> are deemed its closest relatives, particularly with <i><a href="/wiki/Paraserianthes_lophantha" title="Paraserianthes lophantha">P. lophantha</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Brown_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brown-11">[11]</a></sup> The nearest relatives of <i>Acacia</i> and <i>Paraserianthes s.l.</i> in turn include the Australian and <a href="/wiki/South_East_Asia" class="mw-redirect" title="South East Asia">South East Asian</a> genera <i><a href="/wiki/Archidendron" title="Archidendron">Archidendron</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Archidendropsis" title="Archidendropsis">Archidendropsis</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Pararchidendron" title="Pararchidendron">Pararchidendron</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Wallaceodendron" title="Wallaceodendron">Wallaceodendron</a></i>, all of the tribe <a href="/wiki/Ingeae" class="mw-redirect" title="Ingeae">Ingeae</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-bro_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bro-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Acacia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The origin of "<a href="/wiki/Wattle_(construction)" title="Wattle (construction)">wattle</a>" may be an <a href="/wiki/Proto-Germanic_language" title="Proto-Germanic language">Old Teutonic</a> word meaning "to weave".<sup id="cite_ref-aus_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-aus-13">[13]</a></sup> From around 700 A.D. <i>watul</i> was used in <a href="/wiki/Old_English" title="Old English">Old English</a> to refer to the interwoven branches and sticks which formed fences, walls and roofs. Since about 1810 it refers to the Australian legumes that provide these branches.<sup id="cite_ref-aus_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-aus-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Evolution">Evolution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Acacia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Evolution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Acacias in Australia probably evolved their fire resistance about 20 million years ago when fossilised charcoal deposits show a large increase, indicating that fire was a factor even then.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> With no major mountain ranges or rivers to prevent their spread, the wattles began to spread all over the continent as it dried and fires became more common.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> They began to form dry, open forests with species of the genera <i><a href="/wiki/Casuarina" title="Casuarina">Casuarina</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Eucalyptus" title="Eucalyptus">Eucalyptus</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Callitris" title="Callitris">Callitris</a></i> (cypress-pines).</p>
<p>The southernmost species in the genus are <i><a href="/wiki/Acacia_dealbata" title="Acacia dealbata">Acacia dealbata</a></i> (silver wattle), <i><a href="/wiki/Acacia_longifolia" title="Acacia longifolia">Acacia longifolia</a></i> (coast wattle or Sydney golden wattle), <i><a href="/wiki/Acacia_mearnsii" title="Acacia mearnsii">Acacia mearnsii</a></i> (black wattle), and <i><a href="/wiki/Acacia_melanoxylon" title="Acacia melanoxylon">Acacia melanoxylon</a></i> (blackwood), reaching 43°30' S in <a href="/wiki/Tasmania" title="Tasmania">Tasmania</a>, Australia.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_habitat">Distribution and habitat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Acacia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Distribution and habitat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>They are present in all terrestrial habitats, including alpine settings, rainforests, woodlands, grasslands, coastal dunes and deserts.<sup id="cite_ref-orch1_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-orch1-5">[5]</a></sup> In drier woodlands or forest they are an important component of the understory. Elsewhere they may be dominant, as in the <a href="/wiki/Brigalow_Belt" title="Brigalow Belt">Brigalow Belt</a>, <a href="/wiki/Myall_Lakes_National_Park" title="Myall Lakes National Park">Myall woodlands</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Eremaean_province" title="Eremaean province">eremaean</a> <a href="/wiki/Mulga_Lands" title="Mulga Lands">Mulga</a> woodlands.<sup id="cite_ref-orch1_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-orch1-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>In Australia, <i>Acacia</i> forest is the second most common forest type after <i><a href="/wiki/Eucalyptus" title="Eucalyptus">Eucalypt</a></i> forest, covering 980,000 square kilometres (378,380&#160;sq&#160;mi) or 8% of total forest area. <i>Acacia</i> is also the nation’s largest genus of flowering plants with almost 1,000 species found.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Acacia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Several of its species bear vertically oriented <a href="/wiki/Petiole_(botany)" title="Petiole (botany)">phyllodes</a>, which are green, broadened leaf petioles that function like leaf blades,<sup id="cite_ref-wpa1_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wpa1-15">[15]</a></sup> an adaptation to hot climates and droughts.<sup id="cite_ref-tan_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tan-16">[16]</a></sup> Some phyllodinous species have a colourful aril on the seed.<sup id="cite_ref-delin_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-delin-2">[2]</a></sup> A few species have <a href="/wiki/Phylloclade" title="Phylloclade">cladodes</a> rather than leaves.<sup id="cite_ref-eol_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-eol-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Uses">Uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Acacia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Aborigines of Australia have traditionally harvested the seeds of some species, to be ground into flour and eaten as a paste or baked into a cake. The seeds contain as much as 25% more protein than common cereals, and they store well for long periods due to the hard seed coats.<sup id="cite_ref-tan_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tan-16">[16]</a></sup> In addition to utilizing the edible seed and gum, they employed the timber for implements, weapons, fuel and musical instruments.<sup id="cite_ref-orch1_5-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-orch1-5">[5]</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Egypt" title="Ancient Egypt">ancient Egypt</a>, an ointment made from the ground leaves of the plant were used to treat <a href="/wiki/Hemorrhoid" title="Hemorrhoid">hemorrhoids</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Charles2002.28book.29_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Charles2002.28book.29-18">[18]</a></sup> A number of species, most notably <i><a href="/wiki/Acacia_mangium" title="Acacia mangium">A. mangium</a></i> (Hickory wattle), <i><a href="/wiki/Acacia_mearnsii" title="Acacia mearnsii">A. mearnsii</a></i> (Black wattle) and <i><a href="/wiki/Acacia_saligna" title="Acacia saligna">A. saligna</a></i> (Coojong), are economically important and are widely planted globally for wood products, tannin, firewood and fodder.<sup id="cite_ref-thiel_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-thiel-7">[7]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Acacia_melanoxylon" title="Acacia melanoxylon">A. melanoxylon</a></i> (Blackwood) and <i><a href="/wiki/Acacia_aneura" title="Acacia aneura">A. aneura</a></i> (Mulga) supply some of the most attractive timbers in the genus.<sup id="cite_ref-orch1_5-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-orch1-5">[5]</a></sup> Black wattle bark supported the <a href="/wiki/Tanning_(leather)" title="Tanning (leather)">tanning</a> industries of several countries, and may supply tannins for production of waterproof <a href="/wiki/Adhesive" title="Adhesive">adhesives</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-orch1_5-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-orch1-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Acacia is repeatedly mentioned in the <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Exodus" title="Book of Exodus">Book of Exodus</a>, perhaps referring to <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Acacia_raddiana&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Acacia raddiana (page does not exist)">Acacia raddiana</a></i>, in regards to the construction of the <a href="/wiki/Tabernacle" title="Tabernacle">Tabernacle</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-ODU_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ODU-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Species">Species</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Acacia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Species">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">See also: <a href="/wiki/List_of_Acacia_species" title="List of Acacia species">List of <i>Acacia</i> species</a></div>
<p>One species is native to <a href="/wiki/Madagascar" title="Madagascar">Madagascar</a>, 12 to <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a>, and the remaining species (over 900) are native to <a href="/wiki/Australasia" title="Australasia">Australasia</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Islands" title="Pacific Islands">Pacific Islands</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-thiel_7-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-thiel-7">[7]</a></sup> These species were all given combinations by Pedley when he erected the genus <i>Racosperma</i>, hence <i><a href="/wiki/Acacia_pulchella" title="Acacia pulchella">Acacia pulchella</a></i>, for example, became <i><a href="/wiki/Racosperma_pulchellum" class="mw-redirect" title="Racosperma pulchellum">Racosperma pulchellum</a></i>. However these were not upheld with the retypification of <i>Acacia</i>.</p>
