			<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Adiaphora</h1>
									<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
									<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
								<div id="contentSub"></div>
												<div id="jump-to-nav" class="mw-jump">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
				</div>
				<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><p><b>Adiaphoron</b> (plural: <b>adiaphora</b> from the <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a> ἀδιάφορα, the negation of διάφορα - Latin <a href="/wiki/Differentia" title="Differentia">differentia</a> - meaning "not differentiable").</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Stoicism" title="Stoicism">Stoicism</a> "adiaphora" indicates things outside of <a href="/wiki/Moral_law" class="mw-redirect" title="Moral law">moral law</a> — that is, actions that <a href="/wiki/Morality" title="Morality">morality</a> neither mandates nor forbids. In the context of Stoicism "adiaphora" is usually translated as "indifferents."</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Pyrrhonism" title="Pyrrhonism">Pyrrhonism</a>, "adiaphora" indicates things which cannot be logically differentiated. Unlike in Stoicism, the term has no specific connection to morality.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Christianity" title="Christianity">Christianity</a>, "adiaphora" are matters not regarded as essential to <a href="/wiki/Faith_in_Christianity" title="Faith in Christianity">faith</a>, but nevertheless as permissible for Christians or allowed in church. What is specifically considered adiaphora depends on the specific <a href="/wiki/Christian_theology" title="Christian theology">theology</a> in view.</p>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Stoicism">Stoicism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Adiaphora&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Stoicism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Stoics distinguished all the objects of human pursuit into three classes: good, bad, and adiaphora (indifferent). <a href="/wiki/Virtue" title="Virtue">Virtue</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wisdom" title="Wisdom">wisdom</a>, <a href="/wiki/Justice" title="Justice">justice</a>, <a href="/wiki/Temperance_movement" title="Temperance movement">temperance</a>, and the like, were denominated <a href="/wiki/Good_and_evil" title="Good and evil">good; their opposites were bad</a>. Besides these there were many other objects of pursuit such as <a href="/wiki/Wealth" title="Wealth">wealth</a>, <a href="/wiki/Celebrity" title="Celebrity">fame</a>, etc. of themselves neither good nor bad. These were thought therefore in ethics to occupy neutral territory, and were denominated "adiaphora". This distinction amounted practically to an exclusion of the adiaphora from the field of morals.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Pyrrhonism">Pyrrhonism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Adiaphora&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Pyrrhonism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The concept of adiaphora holds a central place in <a href="/wiki/Pyrrhonism" title="Pyrrhonism">Pyrrhonism</a>. Pyrrho claimed that all pragmata (matters, affairs, questions, topics) are adiaphora (not differentiable), astathmēta (unstable, unbalanced, unmeasurable), and anepikrita (unjudgeable, undecidable). Therefore, neither our senses nor our beliefs and theories are able to identify truth or falsehood.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Christianity">Christianity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Adiaphora&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Christianity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">See also: <a href="/wiki/Christian_views_on_the_old_covenant" class="mw-redirect" title="Christian views on the old covenant">Christian views on the old covenant</a> and <a href="/wiki/Paul_the_Apostle_and_Judaism" title="Paul the Apostle and Judaism">Paul the Apostle and Judaism</a></div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">"Christian liberty" redirects here. For religious liberty in general, see <a href="/wiki/Freedom_of_religion" title="Freedom of religion">Freedom of religion</a>.</div>
<p>New Testament examples of adiaphora are often cited from Paul's <a href="/wiki/First_Epistle_to_the_Corinthians" title="First Epistle to the Corinthians">First Epistle to the Corinthians</a>. Some of this epistle was written in response to a question from the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Corinth#Biblical_Corinth" title="Ancient Corinth">Corinthian Christians</a> regarding whether it was permissible for a Christian to eat food offered to <a href="/wiki/Idolatry" title="Idolatry">idols</a>. In response, Paul replied:</p>
<dl>
<dd><i>...food does not bring us near to God; we are no worse if we do not eat, and no better if we do. Be careful, however, that the exercise of your freedom does not become a stumbling block to the weak.</i> (1 Corinthians 8:8–9 <a href="/wiki/New_International_Version" title="New International Version">New International Version</a>)</dd>
</dl>
<p>However, upon study of several other Pauline passages ones sees that Paul is not necessarily saying that there are such things as <i>adiaphora</i>. Elsewhere he says:</p>
<dl>
<dd><i>And whatever you do, whether in word or deed, do it all in the name of the Lord Jesus, giving thanks to God the Father through him.</i> (Colossians 3:17 <a href="/wiki/New_International_Version" title="New International Version">NIV</a>)</dd>
</dl>
<p>The <i>adiaphora</i> are morally acceptable or unacceptable by God based upon the motive and end of the doer. In this sense there are no <i>indifferent</i> things.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Lutheranism">Lutheranism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Adiaphora&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Lutheranism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">See also: <a href="/wiki/Law_and_Gospel" title="Law and Gospel">Law and Gospel</a> and <a href="/wiki/Antinomianism#Lutheranism" title="Antinomianism">Antinomianism §&#160;Lutheranism</a></div>
<p>The issue of what constituted <i>adiaphora</i> became a major dispute during the <a href="/wiki/Protestant_Reformation" class="mw-redirect" title="Protestant Reformation">Protestant Reformation</a>. In 1548, two years after the death of <a href="/wiki/Martin_Luther" title="Martin Luther">Martin Luther</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Charles_V,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Charles V, Holy Roman Emperor">Holy Roman Emperor Charles V</a> tried to unite Catholics and Protestants in his realm with a law called the <a href="/wiki/Augsburg_Interim" title="Augsburg Interim">Augsburg Interim</a>. This law was rejected by <a href="/wiki/Philipp_Melanchthon" class="mw-redirect" title="Philipp Melanchthon">Philipp Melanchthon</a>, on the account that it did not ensure <a href="/wiki/Justification_by_faith" class="mw-redirect" title="Justification by faith">justification by faith</a> as a fundamental doctrine. Later he was persuaded to accept a compromise known as the <a href="/wiki/Leipzig_Interim" title="Leipzig Interim">Leipzig Interim</a>, deciding that doctrinal differences not related to justification by faith were <i>adiaphora</i> or matters of indifference. Melanchthon's compromise was vehemently opposed by <a href="/wiki/Matthias_Flacius" title="Matthias Flacius">Matthias Flacius</a> and his followers in Magdeburg, who went to the opposite extreme by claiming that adiaphora cease to be adiaphora in a case of scandal and confession. By 1576 both extremes were rejected by the majority of <a href="/wiki/Lutheranism" title="Lutheranism">Lutherans</a> led by <a href="/wiki/Martin_Chemnitz" title="Martin Chemnitz">Martin Chemnitz</a> and the formulators of the Formula of Concord.</p>
<p>In 1577, the <a href="/wiki/Formula_of_Concord" title="Formula of Concord">Formula of Concord</a> was crafted to settle the question of the nature of genuine <i>adiaphora</i>, which it defined as church rites that are "...neither commanded nor forbidden in the <a href="/wiki/Bible" title="Bible">Word of God</a>." However, the Concord added believers should not yield even in matters of <i>adiaphora</i> when these are being forced upon them by the "enemies of God's Word".</p>
<p>The Lutheran <a href="/wiki/Augsburg_Confession" title="Augsburg Confession">Augsburg Confession</a> states that the true unity of the Church it is enough to agree concerning the doctrine of the Gospel and the administration of the Sacraments. Nor is it necessary that human traditions, that is, rites or ceremonies, instituted by men, should be everywhere alike.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Puritanism">Puritanism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Adiaphora&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Puritanism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>The <a href="/wiki/Westminster_Confession_of_Faith" title="Westminster Confession of Faith">Westminster Confession of Faith</a>, a confession of faith written by the <a href="/wiki/Puritan" class="mw-redirect" title="Puritan">Puritans</a>, which after the <a href="/wiki/English_Civil_War" title="English Civil War">English Civil War</a> was rejected by the <a href="/wiki/Anglicans" class="mw-redirect" title="Anglicans">Anglicans</a>, distinguishes between elements or acts of <a href="/wiki/Worship" title="Worship">worship</a> (worship proper) and the circumstances of worship. The elements of worship must be limited to what has positive warrant in Scripture, a doctrine known as the <a href="/wiki/Regulative_principle_of_worship" title="Regulative principle of worship">regulative principle of worship</a>. In this framework, the elements of worship have included praise (the words and manner of music), prayer, preaching and teaching from the Bible, the taking of vows, and the two sacraments of baptism and the Lord's Supper, while the circumstances of worship have included the building and its necessary furniture and the time of day for worship.</p>
<p>The circumstances of worship are considered adiaphora, although they must be done for edification and to promote peace and order (compare <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://bible.oremus.org/?passage=1+Corinthians+14:26–33:26&amp;version=nrsv">1 Cor. 14:26–33</a>; <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://bible.oremus.org/?passage=Romans+14:19–14:19&amp;version=nrsv">Rom. 14:19</a>). According to the Westminster Confession 20.2,<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> the conscience is left free in general belief and behavior within the realm of whatever is not "contrary to the Word." However, specifically concerning worship and religious faith, the conscience is free from whatever is "besides" Scripture; that is, it is free to worship and believe only according to whatever has positive warrant in Scripture.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Presbyterian" class="mw-redirect" title="Presbyterian">Presbyterians</a> who have subscribed to the Westminster Confession, for instance, sometimes considered the questions of <a href="/wiki/Musical_instruments" class="mw-redirect" title="Musical instruments">musical instruments</a> and of the singing of <a href="/wiki/Hymn" title="Hymn">hymns</a> (as opposed to <a href="/wiki/Exclusive_psalmody" title="Exclusive psalmody">exclusive psalmody</a>) not drawn directly from the Bible as related to the elements of worship, not optional circumstances, and for this reason they rejected musical instruments and hymns because they believed they were neither commanded by scripture nor deduced by good and necessary consequence from it.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Adherence to such a position is rare among modern Presbyterians, however.</p>
<p>The Puritan position on worship is thus in line with the common saying regarding adiaphora: "<a href="/wiki/In_necessariis_unitas,_in_dubiis_libertas,_in_omnibus_caritas" title="In necessariis unitas, in dubiis libertas, in omnibus caritas">In necessary things, unity; in doubtful things, liberty; in all things, charity</a>".</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Latitudinarianism_in_Anglicanism">Latitudinarianism in Anglicanism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Adiaphora&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Latitudinarianism in Anglicanism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Latitudinarianism" class="mw-redirect" title="Latitudinarianism">Latitudinarianism</a> was initially a pejorative term applied to a group of 17th-century English theologians who believed in conforming to official <a href="/wiki/Church_of_England" title="Church of England">Church of England</a> practices but who felt that matters of <a href="/wiki/Doctrine" title="Doctrine">doctrine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Liturgical" class="mw-redirect" title="Liturgical">liturgical</a> practice, and <a href="/wiki/Ecclesiastical" class="mw-redirect" title="Ecclesiastical">ecclesiastical</a> organization were of relatively little importance. Good examples of the latitudinarian philosophy were found among the <a href="/wiki/Cambridge_Platonists" title="Cambridge Platonists">Cambridge Platonists</a>. The latitudinarian Anglicans of the seventeenth century built on <a href="/wiki/Richard_Hooker_(theologian)" class="mw-redirect" title="Richard Hooker (theologian)">Richard Hooker</a>'s position, in <i><a href="/wiki/Of_the_Laws_of_Ecclesiastical_Polity" class="mw-redirect" title="Of the Laws of Ecclesiastical Polity">Of the Laws of Ecclesiastical Polity</a></i>, that God cares about the moral state of the individual soul and that such things as church leadership are "things indifferent". However, they took the position far beyond Hooker's own and extended it to doctrinal matters.</p>
