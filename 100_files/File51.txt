<p><b>Aizanoi</b> (<a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Ancient&#160;Greek</a>: <span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">Αἰζανοί</span>), <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latinized</a> as <b>Aezani</b> was an ancient city in western <a href="/wiki/Anatolia" title="Anatolia">Anatolia</a>. Located in what is now <a href="/wiki/%C3%87avdarhisar" title="Çavdarhisar">Çavdarhisar</a>, <a href="/wiki/K%C3%BCtahya_Province" title="Kütahya Province">Kütahya Province</a>, its ruins are situated astride the River Penkalas, some 1,000&#160;m (3,300&#160;ft) above sea level. The city was an important political and <a href="/wiki/Roman_economy" title="Roman economy">economic</a> centre in <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Rome" title="Ancient Rome">Roman times</a>; surviving remains from the period include a well-preserved <a href="/wiki/Roman_temple" title="Roman temple">Temple</a> of <a href="/wiki/Zeus" title="Zeus">Zeus</a>, unusual combined <a href="/wiki/Roman_theatre_(structure)" title="Roman theatre (structure)">theatre</a>-<a href="/wiki/Stadium#History" title="Stadium">stadium</a> complex, and <a href="/wiki/Macellum" title="Macellum">macellum</a> inscribed with the <a href="/wiki/Edict_on_Maximum_Prices" title="Edict on Maximum Prices">Price Edict of Diocletian</a>. The city fell into decline in <a href="/wiki/Late_Antiquity" class="mw-redirect" title="Late Antiquity">Late Antiquity</a>. Later serving as a citadel, in 2012 the site was submitted for inscription on the <a href="/wiki/UNESCO_World_Heritage_List" class="mw-redirect" title="UNESCO World Heritage List">UNESCO World Heritage List</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-UNESCO_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-UNESCO-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aizanoi&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Settlement in the area is known from the <a href="/wiki/Bronze_Age" title="Bronze Age">Bronze Age</a>. The city may have derived its name from <a href="/wiki/Azan_(mythology)" title="Azan (mythology)">Azan</a>, one of three sons of <a href="/wiki/Arcas" title="Arcas">Arcas</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Nymph" title="Nymph">nymph</a> <a href="/wiki/Erato" title="Erato">Erato</a>, legendary ancestors of the <a href="/wiki/Phrygia" title="Phrygia">Phrygians</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-c_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-c-2">[2]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-gt_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gt-3">[3]</a></sup> During the <a href="/wiki/Hellenistic_period" title="Hellenistic period">Hellenistic period</a> the city changed hands between the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Pergamum" class="mw-redirect" title="Kingdom of Pergamum">Kingdom of Pergamum</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Bithynia#Kingdom_of_Bithynia" title="Bithynia">Kingdom of Bithynia</a>, before being bequeathed to Rome by the former in 133&#160;BC. It continued to mint its own coins.<sup id="cite_ref-UNESCO_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-UNESCO-1">[1]</a></sup> Its <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Roman_architecture" title="Ancient Roman architecture">monumental buildings</a> date from the early <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">Empire</a> to the 3rd century.</p>
<p>Aezani was part of the <a href="/wiki/Roman_province" title="Roman province">Roman province</a> of <a href="/wiki/Phrygia_Pacatiana" class="mw-redirect" title="Phrygia Pacatiana">Phrygia Pacatiana</a>. It became a <a href="/wiki/Christianity" title="Christianity">Christian</a> <a href="/wiki/Bishopric" class="mw-redirect" title="Bishopric">bishopric</a> at an early stage, and its bishop Pisticus (or Pistus) was a participant at the <a href="/wiki/First_Council_of_Nicaea" title="First Council of Nicaea">First Council of Nicaea</a>, the first <a href="/wiki/Ecumenical_council" title="Ecumenical council">ecumenical council</a>, in 325. Pelagius was at a synod that Patriarch <a href="/wiki/John_II_of_Constantinople" class="mw-redirect" title="John II of Constantinople">John II of Constantinople</a> hastily organized in 518 and that condemned <a href="/wiki/Severus_of_Antioch" title="Severus of Antioch">Severus of Antioch</a>; he was also at the <a href="/wiki/Second_Council_of_Constantinople" title="Second Council of Constantinople">Second Council of Constantinople</a> in 553. Gregory was at the <a href="/wiki/Trullan_Council" class="mw-redirect" title="Trullan Council">Trullan Council</a> of 692, John at the <a href="/wiki/Second_Council_of_Nicaea" title="Second Council of Nicaea">Second Council of Nicaea</a> in 787, and Theophanes at both the <a href="/wiki/Council_of_Constantinople_(869)" class="mw-redirect" title="Council of Constantinople (869)">Council of Constantinople (869)</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Council_of_Constantinople_(879)" class="mw-redirect" title="Council of Constantinople (879)">Council of Constantinople (879)</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> The bishopric was at first a <a href="/wiki/Suffragan" class="mw-redirect" title="Suffragan">suffragan</a> of <a href="/wiki/Laodicea_on_the_Lycus" title="Laodicea on the Lycus">Laodicea</a> but, when Phrygia Pacatiana was divided into two provinces, it found itself a suffragan of <a href="/wiki/Hierapolis" title="Hierapolis">Hierapolis</a>, the capital of the new province of Phrygia Pacatiana II.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> No longer a residential bishopric, Aezani is today listed by the <a href="/wiki/Catholic_Church" title="Catholic Church">Catholic Church</a> as a <a href="/wiki/Titular_see" title="Titular see">titular see</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>After the 7th century, Aezani fell into decline. Later, in <a href="/wiki/Seljuks" class="mw-redirect" title="Seljuks">Seljuk</a> times, the temple hill was converted into a <a href="/wiki/Citadel" title="Citadel">citadel</a> (<a href="/wiki/Turkish_language" title="Turkish language">Turkish</a>: <span lang="tr" xml:lang="tr"><i>hisar</i></span>) by Çavdar <a href="/wiki/Tatar" class="mw-redirect" title="Tatar">Tatars</a>, after which the recent settlement of Çavdarhisar is named.<sup id="cite_ref-UNESCO_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-UNESCO-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-c_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-c-2">[2]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-gt_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gt-3">[3]</a></sup> The ruins of Aezani/Aizanoi were discovered by European travellers in 1824. Survey work in the 1830s and 1840s was followed by systematic excavation conducted by the <a href="/wiki/German_Archaeological_Institute" title="German Archaeological Institute">German Archaeological Institute</a> from 1926, resumed in 1970, and still ongoing.<sup id="cite_ref-UNESCO_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-UNESCO-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-c_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-c-2">[2]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-gt_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gt-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Temple_of_Zeus">Temple of Zeus</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aizanoi&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Temple of Zeus">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Reconstitutionzeusaizanoi.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Reconstruction of the Temple of Zeus</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The Temple of <a href="/wiki/Zeus" title="Zeus">Zeus</a>, situated upon a hill, was the city's main <a href="/wiki/Hellenistic_religion" title="Hellenistic religion">sanctuary</a>. Ceramic finds indicate local habitation from the first half of the third millennium BC. According to a recent reading of the architrave inscription, construction of the temple began under <a href="/wiki/Domitian" title="Domitian">Domitian</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Epigraphy" title="Epigraphy">Inscriptions</a> document imperial assistance from <a href="/wiki/Hadrian" title="Hadrian">Hadrian</a> relating to the recovery of unpaid rents as well as the <a href="/wiki/Euergetism" title="Euergetism">euergetism</a> of Marcus Apuleius Eurykles. Later the Çavdar Tatars carved equestrian and battle scenes on the temple.<sup id="cite_ref-c_2-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-c-2">[2]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-gt_3-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gt-3">[3]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> The temple is <a href="/wiki/Pseudodipteral" title="Pseudodipteral">pseudodipteral</a>, with eight columns at the ends and fifteen along the sides (35&#160;m ×&#160;53&#160;m (115&#160;ft ×&#160;174&#160;ft)).<sup id="cite_ref-c_2-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-c-2">[2]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-gt_3-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gt-3">[3]</a></sup> It was damaged by the <a href="/wiki/1970_Gediz_earthquake" title="1970 Gediz earthquake">1970 Gediz earthquake</a> and has since been <a href="/wiki/Architectural_conservation" title="Architectural conservation">restored</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Theatre_and_stadium">Theatre and stadium</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aizanoi&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Theatre and stadium">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Theather,_Aizanoi.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/db/Theather%2C_Aizanoi.jpg/250px-Theather%2C_Aizanoi.jpg" width="250" height="188" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/db/Theather%2C_Aizanoi.jpg/375px-Theather%2C_Aizanoi.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/db/Theather%2C_Aizanoi.jpg/500px-Theather%2C_Aizanoi.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2048" data-file-height="1536" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Theather,_Aizanoi.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Roman_theatre_(structure)" title="Roman theatre (structure)">Theatre</a>-<a href="/wiki/Stadium#History" title="Stadium">stadium</a> complex</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Aizanoi's <a href="/wiki/Roman_theatre_(structure)" title="Roman theatre (structure)">theatre</a>-<a href="/wiki/Stadium#History" title="Stadium">stadium</a> are built adjacent to each other and this combined complex is said to be unique in the ancient world.<sup id="cite_ref-UNESCO_1-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-UNESCO-1">[1]</a></sup> Separating the two is the stage building.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Construction began after 160&#160;A.D. and was complete by the mid-third century. Inscriptions again attest to the benefaction of M. Apuleius Eurycles.<sup id="cite_ref-c_2-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-c-2">[2]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-gt_3-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gt-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Baths">Baths</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aizanoi&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Baths">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Two sets of <a href="/wiki/Thermae" title="Thermae">thermae</a> have been identified. The first, between the theatre-stadium and the temple, dates to the second half of the second century and includes a <a href="/wiki/Palaestra" title="Palaestra">palaestra</a> and marble furnishings. The second, in the north-east of the city, was built a century later; floor <a href="/wiki/Roman_mosaic" title="Roman mosaic">mosaics</a> depict a <a href="/wiki/Satyr" title="Satyr">satyr</a> and <a href="/wiki/Maenad" title="Maenad">maenad</a>. Rebuilt a couple of centuries later, it served as the bishop's seat.<sup id="cite_ref-c_2-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-c-2">[2]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-gt_3-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gt-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Market">Market</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aizanoi&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Market">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Stockmarket_building_of_Aizanoi.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e9/Stockmarket_building_of_Aizanoi.jpg/250px-Stockmarket_building_of_Aizanoi.jpg" width="250" height="188" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e9/Stockmarket_building_of_Aizanoi.jpg/375px-Stockmarket_building_of_Aizanoi.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e9/Stockmarket_building_of_Aizanoi.jpg/500px-Stockmarket_building_of_Aizanoi.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2048" data-file-height="1536" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Stockmarket_building_of_Aizanoi.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Macellum" title="Macellum">Macellum</a>, inscribed with the <a href="/wiki/Edict_on_Maximum_Prices" title="Edict on Maximum Prices">Price Edict of Diocletian</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>A circular <a href="/wiki/Macellum" title="Macellum">macellum</a> dating to the second half of the second century is located in the south. In the fourth century it was inscribed with a copy of the <a href="/wiki/Edict_on_Maximum_Prices" title="Edict on Maximum Prices">Price Edict of Diocletian</a>, dating to 301, an attempt to limit <a href="/wiki/Inflation" title="Inflation">inflation</a> resulting from <a href="/wiki/Debasement" title="Debasement">debasement</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Roman_coinage" class="mw-redirect" title="Roman coinage">coinage</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-UNESCO_1-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-UNESCO-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-c_2-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-c-2">[2]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-gt_3-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gt-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Colonnaded_street_and_stoa">Colonnaded street and stoa</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aizanoi&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Colonnaded street and stoa">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Recent excavations have revealed the existence of a <a href="/wiki/Stoa" title="Stoa">stoa</a>, or covered walkway, dating to ca. 400&#160;AD, and colonnaded street. A Temple of <a href="/wiki/Artemis" title="Artemis">Artemis</a>, dating to the time of <a href="/wiki/Claudius" title="Claudius">Claudius</a> (41-54), was demolished to make way for the colonnaded street which ran for 450&#160;m (1,480&#160;ft) and led to the sanctuary of Meter Steunene.<sup id="cite_ref-c_2-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-c-2">[2]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-gt_3-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gt-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sanctuary_of_Meter_Steunene">Sanctuary of Meter Steunene</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aizanoi&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Sanctuary of Meter Steunene">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A deep tunnel inside a cave, now collapsed, was dedicated to Meter Steunene (an Anatolian <a href="/wiki/Mother_goddess#Earth_Mother_goddess" title="Mother goddess">Earth Mother goddess</a>). Cult figurines made of clay have been found in excavations, along with two round pits apparently used for <a href="/wiki/Religion_in_ancient_Rome#Sacrifice" title="Religion in ancient Rome">animal sacrifice</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-gt_3-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gt-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Necropolis">Necropolis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aizanoi&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Necropolis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The city's large <a href="/wiki/Necropolis" title="Necropolis">necropolis</a> includes examples of door-shaped Phrygian tombstones. Inscriptions give the names of deceased or donor; accompanying decoration includes, for the tombs of men, bulls, lions and eagles, and for those of women, baskets of wool and a mirror.<sup id="cite_ref-gt_3-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gt-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Museum_of_K.C3.BCtahya">Museum of Kütahya</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aizanoi&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Museum of Kütahya">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Some items from Aizanoi, among them a sarcophagus with an <a href="/wiki/Amazonomachy" title="Amazonomachy">Amazonomachy</a>, have been removed to the Archaeological Museum of Kütahya.<sup id="cite_ref-c_2-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-c-2">[2]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-gt_3-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gt-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
