			<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Akan religion</h1>
									<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
									<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
								<div id="contentSub"></div>
												<div id="jump-to-nav" class="mw-jump">

<p><b>Akan religion</b> comprises the traditional beliefs and religious practices of the <a href="/wiki/Akan_people" title="Akan people">Akan people</a> of <a href="/wiki/Ghana" title="Ghana">Ghana</a> and eastern <a href="/wiki/Ivory_Coast" title="Ivory Coast">Ivory Coast</a>. Akan religion is referred to as <b>Akom</b> (from the <a href="/wiki/Twi" title="Twi">Twi</a> word <i>okom</i>, meaning "hunger"). Although most <a href="/wiki/Akan_people" title="Akan people">Akan people</a> have identified as <a href="/wiki/Christianity" title="Christianity">Christians</a> since the early 20th century, Akan religion remains practiced by some, and is often <a href="/wiki/Syncretic_religion" class="mw-redirect" title="Syncretic religion">syncretized</a> with Christianity. The Akan have many subgroups (including the <a href="/wiki/Ashanti_people" title="Ashanti people">Asante</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Akuapem_People" title="Akuapem People">Akuapem</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Wassa" title="Wassa">Wassa</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Abron_tribe" title="Abron tribe">Abron</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Anyi_people" title="Anyi people">Anyi</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Baoul%C3%A9_people" title="Baoulé people">Baoulé</a>, among others), so the religion varies greatly by region and subgroup.</p>
<p>Similar to other <a href="/wiki/African_traditional_religion" class="mw-redirect" title="African traditional religion">traditional religions</a> of <a href="/wiki/West_Africa" title="West Africa">West</a> and <a href="/wiki/Central_Africa" title="Central Africa">Central Africa</a> such as <a href="/wiki/West_African_Vodun" title="West African Vodun">West African Vodun</a>, <a href="/wiki/Yoruba_religion" title="Yoruba religion">Yoruba religion</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Odinani" title="Odinani">Odinani</a>, Akan cosmology consists of a supreme creator god who generally does not interact with humans and many lesser spirits who assist humans.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Anansi" title="Anansi">Anansi</a> the Spider is a folk <a href="/wiki/Hero" title="Hero">hero</a> who is prominent in Asante folktales where he is depicted as a <a href="/wiki/Trickster" title="Trickster">trickster</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Deities">Deities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Akan_religion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Deities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Creator_God">Creator God</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Akan_religion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Creator God">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Followers of Akan religion believe in a supreme god who created the universe. He is distant and does not interact with humans.</p>
<p>The creator god takes on different names depending upon the region of worship, including <i>Nyame</i>, <i>Nyankopon</i>, <i>Brekyirihunuade</i> ("Almighty"), <i>Odomankoma</i> ("infinite inventor")<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESykesKendall2001146_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESykesKendall2001146-1">[1]</a></sup>, <i>Oboadee</i> ("creator") and <i>Anansi Kokuroku</i> ("the great designer" or "the great spider").<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> It is occasionally said that the creator god is a part of a triune deity or triad, which consists of Nyame, Nyankopon and Odomankoma.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELynch201093_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELynch201093-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Supreme Creator is an <a href="/wiki/Omniscience" title="Omniscience">omniscient</a>, <a href="/wiki/Omnipotence" title="Omnipotence">omnipotent</a> <a href="/wiki/Sky_father" title="Sky father">sky father</a>. His wife is <a href="/wiki/Asase_Ya" title="Asase Ya">Asase Yaa</a> (also known as Mother Earth) , considered second to God.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Together they brought forth two children: Bia and Tano.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELynch201094_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELynch201094-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Abosom">Abosom</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Akan_religion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Abosom">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <i>abosom</i>, the lower deities or <a href="/wiki/Spirits" class="mw-redirect" title="Spirits">spirits</a>, assist humans on earth. These are akin to <a href="/wiki/Orisha" title="Orisha">orishas</a> in <a href="/wiki/Yoruba_religion" title="Yoruba religion">Yoruba religion</a>, the vodun in <a href="/wiki/West_African_Vodun" title="West African Vodun">West African Vodun</a> and its derivatives, and the <a href="/wiki/Alusi" title="Alusi">alusi</a> in <a href="/wiki/Odinani" title="Odinani">Odinani</a>. Abosom receive their power from the creator god and are most often connected to the world as it appears in its natural state. Priests serve individual abosom and act as mediators between the abosom and mankind. Many of those who believe in these traditions participate in daily prayer, which includes the pouring of <a href="/wiki/Libation" title="Libation">libations</a> as an offering to both the ancestors who are buried under the land and to the spirits who are everywhere.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nsamanfo">Nsamanfo</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Akan_religion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Nsamanfo">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <i>Nsamanfo</i> are the <a href="/wiki/Ancestor_worship" class="mw-redirect" title="Ancestor worship">ancestors</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_the_Americas">In the Americas</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Akan_religion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: In the Americas">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Jamaica">Jamaica</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Akan_religion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Jamaica">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>According to Long, Akan (then referred to as "<a href="/wiki/Coromantee" title="Coromantee">Coromantee</a>") culture obliterated any other African customs and incoming non-Akan Africans had to submit to the culture of the majority Akan population in <a href="/wiki/Jamaica" title="Jamaica">Jamaica</a>, much like a foreigner learning migrating to a foreign country. Other than Ananse stories, Akan religion made a huge impact. The Akan pantheon of gods referred to as Abosom in <a href="/wiki/Twi" title="Twi">Twi</a> were documented. Enslaved Akan would praise Nyankopong (erroneously written by the British as Accompong, not related to the Maroon leader <a href="/wiki/Accompong" title="Accompong">Accompong</a> [Twi: <i>Akyeampong</i>]); libations would be poured to <a href="/wiki/Asase_Ya" title="Asase Ya">Asase Yaa</a> (erroneously written as 'Assarci') and Epo the sea god. Bonsam was referred to as the god of evil.<sup id="cite_ref-Long_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Long-6">[6]</a></sup> <b>Kumfu</b> (from the word <i>Akom</i> the name of the Akan spiritual system) was documented as <i>Myal</i> and originally only found in books, while the term Kumfu is still used by Jamaican Maroons. The priest of Kumfu was called a <i>Kumfu-man</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Jamaican_Maroon_spirit-possession_language" title="Jamaican Maroon spirit-possession language">Jamaican Maroon spirit-possession language</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Creole_language" title="Creole language">creolized</a> form of <a href="/wiki/Akan_language" title="Akan language">Akan</a>, is used in religious ceremonies of some <a href="/wiki/Jamaican_Maroons" title="Jamaican Maroons">Jamaican Maroons</a>.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Myal_and_Revival">Myal and Revival</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Akan_religion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Myal and Revival">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Kumfu evolved into Revival, a syncretic Christian sect. Kumfu followers gravitated to the American <a href="/wiki/Revival_of_1800" title="Revival of 1800">Revival of 1800 Seventh Day Adventist movement</a> because it observed Saturday as god's day of rest. This was a shared aboriginal belief of the Akan people as this too was the day that the Akan god, Nyame rested after creating the earth. Jamaicans that were aware of their Ashanti past while wanting to keep hidden, mixed their Kumfu spirituality with the American Adventists to create Jamaican Revival in 1860. Revival has two sects: <i>60 order</i>(or Zion Revival, the order of the heavens) and <i>61 order</i>(or Pocomania, the order of the earth). 60 order worships God and spirits of air or the heavens on a Saturday and considers itself to be the more 'clean' sect. 61 order more deals with spirits of the earth. This division of Kumfu clearly shows the dichotomy of Nyame and Asase Yaa's relationship, Nyame representing <i>air</i> and has his 60 order'; <i>Asase Yaa</i> having her 61 order of the <i>earth</i>. Also the Ashanti funerary/war colours: red and black have the same meaning in Revival of <i>vengeance</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Other Ashanti elements include the use of swords and rings as means to guard the spirit from spiritual attack. The Asantehene like the Mother Woman of Revival, has special two swords used to protect himself from witchcraft called an <a href="/wiki/Akrafena" title="Akrafena">Akrafena</a> or <i>soul sword</i> and a Bosomfena or <i>spirit sword</i> <sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Suriname">Suriname</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Akan_religion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Suriname">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Winti" title="Winti">Winti</a> is an <a href="/wiki/Suriname" title="Suriname">Afro-Surinamese</a> religion which is largely derived from Akan religion.</p>
