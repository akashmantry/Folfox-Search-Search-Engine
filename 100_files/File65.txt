<p><b>Amedeo Nazzari</b> (10 December 1907 in <a href="/wiki/Cagliari" title="Cagliari">Cagliari</a> – 5 November 1979) was an Italian actor. Nazzari was one of the leading figures of Italian classic cinema, often considered a local variant of the Australian-American star <a href="/wiki/Errol_Flynn" title="Errol Flynn">Errol Flynn</a>. Although he emerged as a star during the <a href="/wiki/Fascist_era" class="mw-redirect" title="Fascist era">Fascist era</a>, Nazzari's popularity continued well into the post-war years.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_career">Early career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Amedeo_Nazzari&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Amedeo Nazzari was born as <b>Amedeo Carlo Leone Buffa</b> in <a href="/wiki/Cagliari" title="Cagliari">Cagliari</a>, Sardinia in 1907 and he later adopted as his professional name the name of his maternal grandfather, Amedeo Nazzari, a person who had been the <a href="/w/index.php?title=President_of_the_Court_of_Appeal_of&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="President of the Court of Appeal of (page does not exist)">President of the Court of Appeal of</a> <a href="/wiki/Vicenza" title="Vicenza">Vicenza</a> in Venetia and later took the same position in Cagliari. Although Salvatore Amedeo eventually moved to Rome, he always retained a slight trace of his native Sardinian accent.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> While Nazzari was keen on gaining film contracts much of his early experience was in the theatre. He entered a contest organised by <a href="/wiki/Twentieth_Century_Fox" class="mw-redirect" title="Twentieth Century Fox">Twentieth Century Fox</a> to find an Italian actor to fill the boots of the recently deceased screen star <a href="/wiki/Rudolph_Valentino" title="Rudolph Valentino">Rudolph Valentino</a>, but lost out to <a href="/wiki/Alberto_Rabagliati" title="Alberto Rabagliati">Alberto Rabagliati</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> He was rejected after <a href="/wiki/Screen_test" title="Screen test">screen tests</a> by Italian professionals, who found him too tall, thin and thought he had a too gloomy expression.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Nazzari made his debut in <i><a href="/wiki/Ginevra_degli_Almieri" title="Ginevra degli Almieri">Ginevra degli Almieri</a></i> (1935), following a recommendation from <a href="/wiki/Elsa_Merlini" title="Elsa Merlini">Elsa Merlini</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> His first read role came with the 1936 film <i><a href="/wiki/Cavalry_(1936_Italian_film)" title="Cavalry (1936 Italian film)">Cavalry</a></i>, and he followed it up with <i><a href="/wiki/The_Castiglioni_Brothers" title="The Castiglioni Brothers">The Castiglioni Brothers</a></i> (1937). His breakthrough came with the 1938 film <i><a href="/wiki/Luciano_Serra,_Pilot" title="Luciano Serra, Pilot">Luciano Serra, Pilot</a></i> (1938) where he played a <a href="/wiki/First_World_War" class="mw-redirect" title="First World War">First World War</a> veteran who returns to fight for Italy during the <a href="/wiki/Second_Italo%E2%80%93Ethiopian_War" class="mw-redirect" title="Second Italo–Ethiopian War">Abyssinian War</a>. Nazzari was transformed into a <a href="/wiki/Matinee_idol" class="mw-redirect" title="Matinee idol">matinee idol</a>, the most bankable star of Italian cinema.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Following the film, Nazzari was invited to join the <a href="/wiki/Fascist_Party" class="mw-redirect" title="Fascist Party">Fascist Party</a> by <a href="/wiki/Benito_Mussolini" title="Benito Mussolini">Benito Mussolini</a>, but declined saying "Thank You Duce! I would prefer not to concern myself with politics, occupied as I am with more pressing artistic commitments".<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>

<p>Despite declining to join the party Nazzari, along with a handful of other actor such as <a href="/wiki/Fosco_Giachetti" title="Fosco Giachetti">Fosco Giachetti</a>, was considered the model of a Male Hero. Most of his film roles from this point present him as a <a href="/wiki/Masculine" class="mw-redirect" title="Masculine">masculine</a> (often military) figure. His emergence as a star coincided with a major drive by the Italian government to rebuild the country's film industry which had declined since its heyday in the <a href="/wiki/Silent_era" class="mw-redirect" title="Silent era">silent era</a>.</p>
<p>This policy involved large-scale government funding of films and the construction of the massive <a href="/wiki/Cinecitt%C3%A0" title="Cinecittà">Cinecittà</a> studio complex in Rome. The number of films produced each year climbed rapidly, with Nazarri a particularly prolific actor (making six films in 1939 and eight in 1941).<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> During the era he appeared opposite most of the leading Italian actresses including <a href="/wiki/Alida_Valli" title="Alida Valli">Alida Valli</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lilia_Silvi" title="Lilia Silvi">Lilia Silvi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Luisa_Ferida" title="Luisa Ferida">Luisa Ferida</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mariella_Lotti" title="Mariella Lotti">Mariella Lotti</a>, <a href="/wiki/Assia_Noris" title="Assia Noris">Assia Noris</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vera_Carmi" title="Vera Carmi">Vera Carmi</a> and <a href="/wiki/Clara_Calamai" title="Clara Calamai">Clara Calamai</a>, often more than once.</p>
<p>Nazzari was almost always cast as a straightforward hero, and he closely protected his public persona to avoid any negative roles. An exception was the historical comedy-drama film <i><a href="/wiki/The_Jester%27s_Supper" class="mw-redirect" title="The Jester's Supper">The Jester's Supper</a></i> (1942) in which he plays a loutish figure. Nazzari made four films with <a href="/wiki/Alida_Valli" title="Alida Valli">Alida Valli</a>, including <i><a href="/wiki/Unjustified_Absence" title="Unjustified Absence">Unjustified Absence</a></i> (1939).</p>
<p>Following Italy's entry into the <a href="/wiki/Second_World_War" class="mw-redirect" title="Second World War">Second World War</a> in 1940, he combined romances and comedies, with occasional more propagandistic productions. Amongst the more political was <i><a href="/wiki/Bengasi_(film)" title="Bengasi (film)">Bengasi</a></i> (1942), an anti-British war film set in <a href="/wiki/Libya" title="Libya">Libya</a>. Nazzari portrays an Italian patriot who masquerades as a collaborator with the British occupiers of <a href="/wiki/Bengazi" class="mw-redirect" title="Bengazi">Bengazi</a> in order to steal their battle plans. It was the only time he featured alongside the other great male star of the era, Fosco Giachetti.</p>

<p>Star of Italian cinema during the 40's and 50's. He made several melodramas with <a href="/wiki/Raffaello_Matarazzo" title="Raffaello Matarazzo">Raffaello Matarazzo</a>, such as <i><a href="/wiki/Catene_(1949_film)" class="mw-redirect" title="Catene (1949 film)">Catene</a></i> in 1949. Nazzari acts himself in <a href="/wiki/Federico_Fellini" title="Federico Fellini">Federico Fellini</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Nights_of_Cabiria" title="Nights of Cabiria">Nights of Cabiria</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Awards">Awards</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Amedeo_Nazzari&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Awards">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
