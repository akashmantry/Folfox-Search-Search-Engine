			<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Ancient Egyptian royal titulary</h1>
									<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
									<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
								<div id="contentSub"></div>
												<div id="jump-to-nav" class="mw-jump">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
				</div>
				<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><p>The <b>royal titulary</b> or <b>royal protocol</b> of an <a href="/wiki/Egypt" title="Egypt">Egyptian</a> <a href="/wiki/Pharaoh" title="Pharaoh">pharaoh</a> is the standard naming convention taken by the kings of Ancient Egypt. It symbolises worldly power and holy might and also acts as a sort of <a href="/wiki/Mission_statement" title="Mission statement">mission statement</a> for the reign of a monarch (sometimes it even changed during the reign).</p>
<p>The full titulary, consisting of five names, did not come into standard usage until the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Kingdom_of_Egypt" title="Middle Kingdom of Egypt">Middle Kingdom</a> but remained in use as late as the <a href="/wiki/Aegyptus_(Roman_province)" class="mw-redirect" title="Aegyptus (Roman province)">Roman Empire</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Horus_name">Horus name</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ancient_Egyptian_royal_titulary&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Horus name">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Horus_name" title="Horus name">Horus name</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Egypte_louvre_290.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f3/Egypte_louvre_290.jpg/220px-Egypte_louvre_290.jpg" width="220" height="363" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f3/Egypte_louvre_290.jpg/330px-Egypte_louvre_290.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f3/Egypte_louvre_290.jpg/440px-Egypte_louvre_290.jpg 2x" data-file-width="477" data-file-height="788" /></a>

<p>The <a href="/wiki/Horus_name" title="Horus name">Horus name</a> is the oldest form of the pharaoh's name, originating in the <a href="/wiki/Predynastic_Period_of_Egypt" class="mw-redirect" title="Predynastic Period of Egypt">Predynastic Period</a>. Many of the oldest-known Egyptian pharaohs were known only by this title.<sup id="cite_ref-TAW_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TAW-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Horus name was usually written in a <a href="/wiki/Serekh" title="Serekh">serekh</a>, a representation of a palace façade. The name of the pharaoh was written in <a href="/wiki/Egyptian_hieroglyphs" title="Egyptian hieroglyphs">hieroglyphs</a> inside this representation of a palace. Typically an image of the falcon God <a href="/wiki/Horus" title="Horus">Horus</a> was perched on top of or beside it.<sup id="cite_ref-TAW_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TAW-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>At least one Egyptian ruler, the <a href="/wiki/Second_Dynasty_of_Egypt" title="Second Dynasty of Egypt">2nd dynasty</a> <a href="/wiki/Seth-Peribsen" title="Seth-Peribsen">Seth-Peribsen</a>, used an image of the god <a href="/wiki/Set_(mythology)" class="mw-redirect" title="Set (mythology)">Seth</a> instead of Horus, perhaps signifying an internal religious division within the country. He was succeeded by <a href="/wiki/Khasekhemwy" title="Khasekhemwy">Khasekhemwy</a>, who placed the symbols of both Seth and Horus above his name. Thereafter, the image of Horus always appeared alongside the name of the pharaoh.<sup id="cite_ref-TAW_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TAW-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>By the time of the <a href="/wiki/New_Kingdom" class="mw-redirect" title="New Kingdom">New Kingdom</a> the Horus name was often written without the enclosing serekh.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Nebty_.28.22two_ladies.22.29_name">Nebty ("two ladies") name</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ancient_Egyptian_royal_titulary&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Nebty (&quot;two ladies&quot;) name">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Nebty_name" title="Nebty name">Nebty name</a></div>

<p>The <a href="/wiki/Nebty_name" title="Nebty name">Nebty name</a> (lit. "<a href="/wiki/Two_Ladies" title="Two Ladies">two ladies</a>") was associated with the so-called "heraldic" goddesses of Upper and Lower Egypt:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Nekhbet" title="Nekhbet">Nekhbet</a>, patron deity of <a href="/wiki/Upper_Egypt" title="Upper Egypt">Upper Egypt</a>, represented by a <a href="/wiki/Vulture" title="Vulture">vulture</a>, and</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Wadjet" title="Wadjet">Wadjet</a>, patron deity of <a href="/wiki/Lower_Egypt" title="Lower Egypt">Lower Egypt</a>, represented by a <a href="/wiki/Cobra" title="Cobra">cobra</a>.</li>
</ul>
<p>The name is first definitively used by the <a href="/wiki/First_Dynasty" class="mw-redirect" title="First Dynasty">First Dynasty</a> pharaoh <a href="/wiki/Semerkhet" title="Semerkhet">Semerkhet</a>, though it only became a fully independent title by the <a href="/wiki/Twelfth_dynasty_of_Egypt" class="mw-redirect" title="Twelfth dynasty of Egypt">Twelfth Dynasty</a>.</p>
<p>This particular name was not typically framed by a <a href="/wiki/Cartouche" title="Cartouche">cartouche</a> or serekh, but always begins with the hieroglyphs of a vulture and cobra resting upon <a href="/wiki/Basket_(hieroglyph)" title="Basket (hieroglyph)">two baskets</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Dual_(grammatical_number)" title="Dual (grammatical number)">dual</a> noun "nebty".</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Horus_of_Gold">Horus of Gold</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ancient_Egyptian_royal_titulary&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Horus of Gold">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<table class="mw-hiero-table mw-hiero-outer" dir="ltr">
<tr>
<td>
<table class="mw-hiero-table">
<tr>
<td><img style="margin: 1px;" src="/w/extensions/wikihiero/img/hiero_G8.png?a8843" height="38" title="G8" alt="G8" /></td>
</tr>
</table>
</td>

<p>Also known as the <i><a href="/wiki/Gold_(hieroglyph)" title="Gold (hieroglyph)">Golden</a> Horus Name</i>, this form of the pharaoh's name typically featured the image of a Horus falcon perched above or beside the hieroglyph for gold.</p>
<p>The meaning of this particular title has been disputed. One belief is that it represents the triumph of <a href="/wiki/Horus" title="Horus">Horus</a> over his uncle <a href="/wiki/Set_(mythology)" class="mw-redirect" title="Set (mythology)">Seth</a>, as the symbol for gold can be taken to mean that Horus was "superior to his foes". Gold also was strongly associated in the ancient Egyptian mind with eternity, so this may have been intended to convey the pharaoh's eternal Horus name.</p>
<p>Similar to the Nebty name, this particular name typically was not framed by a cartouche or serekh.</p>

<p>The pharaoh's throne name, the first of the two names written inside a <a href="/wiki/Cartouche" title="Cartouche">cartouche</a>, and usually accompanied the title <i>nsw-bity</i> (<i>nesu-bity, nesw-bit, nswt-bjtj</i>). The term literally means "S/He of the <a href="/wiki/Cyperaceae" title="Cyperaceae">Sedge</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bee" title="Bee">Bee</a>", but is often translated for convenience as "King of Upper and of Lower Egypt", the sedge and bee being symbols for Upper and Lower Egypt, respectively.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>The term <i>nsw-bity</i> It has been suggested that the <a href="/wiki/Berber_languages" title="Berber languages">Berber</a> term for "strong man; ruler" might be derived from it (Schneider 1993).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (March 2015)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The epithet <i>nb t3wy</i>, "Lord of the Two Lands", referring to valley and delta regions of Egypt, often occurs as well.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. </tr>
</table>
</td>
</tr>
</table>
<p>This was the name given at birth. The name itself was preceded by the title "Son of <a href="/wiki/Ra" title="Ra">Ra</a>", written with the hieroglyph of a duck (<i>za</i>), a <a href="/wiki/Homonym" title="Homonym">homonym</a> for the word meaning "son" (<i>za</i>), adjacent to an image of the sun, a hieroglyph for the chief solar deity Ra. It was first introduced to the set of royal titles in the <a href="/wiki/Fourth_dynasty_of_Egypt" class="mw-redirect" title="Fourth dynasty of Egypt">Fourth Dynasty</a> and emphasizes the king's role as a representative of the solar god Ra. For women who became pharaoh, the preceding title was interpreted as "daughter" also.</p>
<p>Modern historians typically refer to the ancient kings of Egypt by this name, adding <a href="/wiki/Regnal_number" title="Regnal number">ordinals</a> (e.g. "II", "III") to distinguish between different individuals bearing the same name.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hatshepsut">Hatshepsut</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ancient_Egyptian_royal_titulary&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Hatshepsut">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The full titulary of <a href="/wiki/Eighteenth_Dynasty" class="mw-redirect" title="Eighteenth Dynasty">Eighteenth Dynasty</a> pharaoh <a href="/wiki/Hatshepsut" title="Hatshepsut">Hatshepsut</a>, providing a guide to pronunciation and its equivalent meaning and showing the differences since this pharaoh is a woman, is as follows,</p>
<ul>
<li>Horus name: <i>Wesretkau</i>, "Mighty of Kas"</li>
<li>Nebty name: <i>Wadjrenput</i>, "She of the Two Ladies, Flourishing of years"</li>
<li>Golden Horus: <i>Netjeretkhau</i>, "Divine of appearance" (<i>Netjeret</i> is the feminine form of <i>netery</i> meaning 'godly' or 'divine', and <i>khau</i>, 'appearances')</li>
<li>Praenomen: <i>Maatkare</i>, "Truth [Ma'at] is the Ka of Re"</li>
<li>Nomen: <i>Khnumt-Amun Hatshepsut</i>, "Joined with Amun, Foremost of Noble Ladies"</li>
</ul>
