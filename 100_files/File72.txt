<p><b>Abisin Abbas</b> (<span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_Malay" title="Help:IPA for Malay">[aˈbisɪn aˈbas]</a></span>; 26 February 1902&#160;– 20 October 1961), better known by his pseudonym <b>Andjar Asmara</b> (<span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_Malay" title="Help:IPA for Malay">[anˈdʒar asˈmara]</a></span>), was a dramatist and filmmaker active in the cinema of the <a href="/wiki/Dutch_East_Indies" title="Dutch East Indies">Dutch East Indies</a>. Born in <a href="/wiki/Alahan_Panjang" title="Alahan Panjang">Alahan Panjang</a>, <a href="/wiki/West_Sumatra" title="West Sumatra">West Sumatra</a>, he first worked as a reporter in <a href="/wiki/Jakarta" title="Jakarta">Batavia</a> (modern-day Jakarta). He became a writer for the Padangsche Opera in <a href="/wiki/Padang" title="Padang">Padang</a>, where he developed a new, dialogue-centric style, which later spread throughout the region. After returning to Batavia in 1929, he spent over a year as a theatre and film critic. In 1930 he joined the <a href="/wiki/Dardanella_(theatre_company)" title="Dardanella (theatre company)">Dardanella touring troupe</a> as a writer. He went to India in an unsuccessful bid to film his <a href="/wiki/Stage_play" class="mw-redirect" title="Stage play">stage play</a> <i>Dr Samsi</i>.</p>
<p>After leaving Dardanella in 1936, Andjar established his own troupe. He also worked at a publishers, writing <a href="/wiki/Serial_(literature)" title="Serial (literature)">serials</a> based on successful films. In 1940 he was asked to join <a href="/wiki/The_Teng_Chun" title="The Teng Chun">The Teng Chun</a>'s company, Java Industrial Film, helping with marketing and working as a director for two productions. After the <a href="/wiki/Japanese_occupation_of_Indonesia" class="mw-redirect" title="Japanese occupation of Indonesia">Japanese occupation</a>, during which time he stayed in theatre, Andjar made a brief return to cinema. He directed three films in the late 1940s and wrote four <a href="/wiki/Screenplay" title="Screenplay">screenplays</a>, which were produced as films in the early 1950s. He published a novel, <i>Noesa Penida</i> (1950). Afterward he worked for the remainder of his life writing serials based on local films and publishing film criticism. Historians recognise him as a pioneer of theatre and one of the first <a href="/wiki/Native_Indonesian" class="mw-redirect" title="Native Indonesian">native Indonesian</a> film directors, although he had little creative control of his productions.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life_and_theatre">Early life and theatre</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Andjar_Asmara&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life and theatre">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Andjar was born Abisin Abbas<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEncyclopedia_of_Jakarta.2C_Andjar_Asmara_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEncyclopedia_of_Jakarta.2C_Andjar_Asmara-1">[1]</a></sup> in <a href="/wiki/Alahan_Panjang" title="Alahan Panjang">Alahan Panjang</a>, <a href="/wiki/West_Sumatra" title="West Sumatra">West Sumatra</a>, on 26&#160;February 1902. He gravitated toward traditional theatre at a young age after visits from the wandering Wayang Kassim and Juliana Opera <i>stambul</i> troupes;<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESaid1982136.E2.80.93137_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESaid1982136.E2.80.93137-2">[2]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECohen2003215.E2.80.93216_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECohen2003215.E2.80.93216-3">[3]</a></sup> he pretended to act with his friends in stage plays which they had seen.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTETIM.2C_Andjar_Asmara_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTETIM.2C_Andjar_Asmara-4">[4]</a></sup> After completing his formal education up to the <a href="/wiki/Meer_Uitgebreid_Lager_Onderwijs" title="Meer Uitgebreid Lager Onderwijs">Meer Uitgebreid Lager Onderwijs</a> (junior high school) level&#160;– first in <a href="/wiki/Malay-language" class="mw-redirect" title="Malay-language">Malay-language</a> schools then Dutch ones<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEneste200123_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEneste200123-5">[5]</a></sup>&#160;– he moved to <a href="/wiki/Jakarta" title="Jakarta">Batavia</a> (modern-day Jakarta). He worked as a reporter for two <a href="/wiki/Daily_newspaper" class="mw-redirect" title="Daily newspaper">daily newspapers</a>, <i>Bintang Timoer</i> and <i>Bintang Hindia</i>; he may have also worked on a farm.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTETIM.2C_Andjar_Asmara_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTETIM.2C_Andjar_Asmara-4">[4]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEI.N.1981212_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEI.N.1981212-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Around 1925, having had little success in Batavia, Andjar moved to <a href="/wiki/Padang" title="Padang">Padang</a>, where he was a reporter for the <a href="/wiki/Daily_newspaper" class="mw-redirect" title="Daily newspaper">daily</a> <i>Sinar Soematera</i>. At the same time, he worked with the city's Padangsche Opera, writing <a href="/wiki/Stage_play" class="mw-redirect" title="Stage play">stage plays</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESaid1982136.E2.80.93137_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESaid1982136.E2.80.93137-2">[2]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFilmindonesia.or.id.2C_Dr_Samsi_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFilmindonesia.or.id.2C_Dr_Samsi-7">[7]</a></sup> In contrast to the standard musical theatre of the time, <i><a href="/wiki/Bangsawan" title="Bangsawan">bangsawan</a></i>, he promoted a more natural style, using dialogue instead of song to convey the story;<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTETIM.2C_Andjar_Asmara_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTETIM.2C_Andjar_Asmara-4">[4]</a></sup> he referred to this as <i>toneel</i>, based on the Dutch word for theatre.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECohen2003215.E2.80.93216_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECohen2003215.E2.80.93216-3">[3]</a></sup> Among the works he wrote for the Padangsche Opera were adaptations of <i>Melati van Agam</i>, a 1923 work by Swan Pen,<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[a]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTETIM.2C_Andjar_Asmara_4-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTETIM.2C_Andjar_Asmara-4">[4]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBiran200999.2C_108_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBiran200999.2C_108-9">[8]</a></sup> and <i><a href="/wiki/Sitti_Nurbaya" title="Sitti Nurbaya">Sitti Nurbaya</a></i>, a 1922 novel by <a href="/wiki/Marah_Roesli" title="Marah Roesli">Marah Roesli</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECohen2003215.E2.80.93216_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECohen2003215.E2.80.93216-3">[3]</a></sup> These works were well received.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTETIM.2C_Andjar_Asmara_4-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTETIM.2C_Andjar_Asmara-4">[4]</a></sup></p>

<p>In the late 1920s, after spending some two years in <a href="/wiki/Medan" title="Medan">Medan</a> with the daily <i>Sinar Soematra</i>,<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEI.N.1981214_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEI.N.1981214-10">[9]</a></sup> Andjar returned to Batavia and in 1929 helped establish the magazine <i>Doenia Film</i>, a Malay adaptation of the Dutch-language magazine <i>Filmland</i>; although an adaptation, <i>Doenia Film</i> also contained original coverage of the domestic theatre and film industry.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESaid1982136.E2.80.93137_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESaid1982136.E2.80.93137-2">[2]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBiran200943.E2.80.9344_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBiran200943.E2.80.9344-11">[10]</a></sup> At the time, the cinema of the Indies was becoming established: the first domestic film, <i><a href="/wiki/Loetoeng_Kasaroeng" title="Loetoeng Kasaroeng">Loetoeng Kasaroeng</a></i> (<i>The Lost Lutung</i>), was released in 1926, and four additional films were released in 1927 and 1928.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBiran2009379_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBiran2009379-12">[11]</a></sup> Andjar wrote extensively regarding local cinematic and theatrical productions; for example, the Indonesian film critic Salim Said writes Andjar inspired the marketing for 1929's <i><a href="/wiki/Njai_Dasima_(1929_film)" title="Njai Dasima (1929 film)">Njai Dasima</a></i>, which emphasised the exclusively <a href="/wiki/Native_Indonesians" title="Native Indonesians">native</a> cast.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[b]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESaid198220_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESaid198220-14">[12]</a></sup> In 1930 Andjar left <i>Doenia Film</i> and was replaced by <a href="/wiki/Bachtiar_Effendi" title="Bachtiar Effendi">Bachtiar Effendi</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBiran20096.2C_14.2C_20_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBiran20096.2C_14.2C_20-15">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>Andjar became a writer for the theatrical troupe <a href="/wiki/Dardanella_(theatre_company)" title="Dardanella (theatre company)">Dardanella</a> in November 1930, working under the group's founder Willy A. Piedro.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[c]</a></sup> Andjar believed the troupe to be dedicated to the betterment of the <i>toneel</i> as an art form and not only motivated by financial interests, as were the earlier <i>stambul</i> troupes.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[d]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBiran20096.2C_14.2C_20_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBiran20096.2C_14.2C_20-15">[13]</a></sup> He wrote and published many plays with the group's backing, including <i>Dr Samsi</i> and <i>Singa Minangkabau</i> (<i>The Lion of Minangkabau</i>).<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTETIM.2C_Andjar_Asmara_4-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTETIM.2C_Andjar_Asmara-4">[4]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBiran20096.2C_14.2C_20_15-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBiran20096.2C_14.2C_20-15">[13]</a></sup> Andjar also worked as a theatre critic, writing several pieces on the history of local theatre, sometimes using his birth name and sometimes his pseudonym.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECohen2006347.2C_402_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECohen2006347.2C_402-18">[14]</a></sup> In 1936 Andjar went with Dardanella to India to record a film adaptation of his drama <i>Dr Samsi</i>, which followed a doctor who was blackmailed after an unscrupulous <a href="/wiki/Indo_people" title="Indo people">Indo</a> discovered he had an illegitimate child.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESaid1982136.E2.80.93138_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESaid1982136.E2.80.93138-19">[15]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBiran200923_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBiran200923-20">[16]</a></sup> The deal fell through, however, and Andjar left India with his wife <a href="/wiki/Ratna_Asmara" title="Ratna Asmara">Ratna</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBiran200925_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBiran200925-21">[17]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJCG.2C_Dardanella_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJCG.2C_Dardanella-22">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Film_career_and_death">Film career and death</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Andjar_Asmara&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Film career and death">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Ratna_Asmara_p298.JPG" class="image"><img alt="A woman with her hair tied back, looking at the camera and smiling" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/84/Ratna_Asmara_p298.JPG/170px-Ratna_Asmara_p298.JPG" width="170" height="247" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/84/Ratna_Asmara_p298.JPG/255px-Ratna_Asmara_p298.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/84/Ratna_Asmara_p298.JPG/340px-Ratna_Asmara_p298.JPG 2x" data-file-width="1326" data-file-height="1926" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Ratna_Asmara_p298.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Andjar's wife, <a href="/wiki/Ratna_Asmara" title="Ratna Asmara">Ratna</a>, was the first female film director in Indonesian history.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Upon his return to the Indies, Andjar formed another theatrical troupe, Bolero, with Effendi, but left the troupe around 1940 to work at Kolf Publishers in <a href="/wiki/Surabaya" title="Surabaya">Surabaya</a>. Effendi was left as the head of Bolero,<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESaid1982136.E2.80.93138_19-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESaid1982136.E2.80.93138-19">[15]</a></sup> which then became more politicised.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBaylyHarper2007116_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBaylyHarper2007116-23">[19]</a></sup> At Kolf Andjar edited the publisher's magazine <i>Poestaka Timoer</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEncyclopedia_of_Jakarta.2C_Andjar_Asmara_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEncyclopedia_of_Jakarta.2C_Andjar_Asmara-1">[1]</a></sup> As his work entailed writing synopses and <a href="/wiki/Serial_(literature)" title="Serial (literature)">serials</a> based on popular films for Kolf's magazine, he became increasingly involved in the film industry. He was soon asked by <a href="/wiki/The_Teng_Chun" title="The Teng Chun">The Teng Chun</a>, with whom he had maintained a business relationship, to direct a film for his company Java Industrial Film (JIF);<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESaid1982136.E2.80.93137_2-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESaid1982136.E2.80.93137-2">[2]</a></sup> with this Andjar became one of several noted theatrical personnel who migrated to film following <a href="/wiki/Albert_Balink" title="Albert Balink">Albert Balink</a>'s 1937 hit <i><a href="/wiki/Terang_Boelan" title="Terang Boelan">Terang Boelan</a></i> (<i>Full Moon</i>).<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESaid198227_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESaid198227-24">[20]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBiran2009169_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBiran2009169-25">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>After handling the marketing for <i><a href="/wiki/Rentjong_Atjeh" title="Rentjong Atjeh">Rentjong Atjeh</a></i> (<i>Rencong of Aceh</i>, 1940),<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBiran2009210_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBiran2009210-26">[22]</a></sup> Andjar made his directorial debut in 1940 with <i><a href="/wiki/Kartinah" title="Kartinah">Kartinah</a></i>, a war-time romance starring Ratna Asmara.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESaid1982136.E2.80.93137_2-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESaid1982136.E2.80.93137-2">[2]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBiran2009213_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBiran2009213-27">[23]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Academia" class="mw-redirect" title="Academia">Academia</a></i> was critical of the film, believing it to lack educational value.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBiran2009266_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBiran2009266-28">[24]</a></sup> In 1941 he directed <i><a href="/wiki/Noesa_Penida" title="Noesa Penida">Noesa Penida</a></i>, a tragedy based in <a href="/wiki/Bali" title="Bali">Bali</a>, for JIF;<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBiran2009217.2C_278_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBiran2009217.2C_278-29">[25]</a></sup> the film was remade in 1988.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFilmindonesia.or.id.2C_Filmografi_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFilmindonesia.or.id.2C_Filmografi-30">[26]</a></sup> In these films, he had little creative control, and performed as what the Indonesian <a href="/wiki/Entertainment_journalism" title="Entertainment journalism">entertainment journalist</a> Eddie Karsito describes as a dialogue coach. Camera angles and locations were chosen by the <a href="/wiki/Cinematographer" title="Cinematographer">cinematographer</a>, who was generally also the producer.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKarsito200823_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKarsito200823-31">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>During the <a href="/wiki/Japanese_occupation_of_Indonesia" class="mw-redirect" title="Japanese occupation of Indonesia">Japanese occupation</a> from 1942 to 1945, the nation's film industry nearly ceased to exist: all but one studio were closed, and all films released were <a href="/wiki/Japanese_propaganda_during_World_War_II" class="mw-redirect" title="Japanese propaganda during World War II">propaganda</a> pieces to assist the Japanese war effort and promote the <a href="/wiki/Greater_East_Asia_Co-Prosperity_Sphere" title="Greater East Asia Co-Prosperity Sphere">Greater East Asia Co-Prosperity Sphere</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBiran2009334.E2.80.93351_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBiran2009334.E2.80.93351-32">[28]</a></sup> Andjar was not involved in these but was excited by the artistic merits of <a href="/wiki/Japanese_cinema" class="mw-redirect" title="Japanese cinema">Japanese films</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESaid198235_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESaid198235-33">[29]</a></sup> Although he wrote several short stories during this time, three of which <a href="/wiki/List_of_literary_works_published_in_Asia_Raja" class="mw-redirect" title="List of literary works published in Asia Raja">were published</a> in the pro-Japanese newspaper <i><a href="/wiki/Asia_Raja" class="mw-redirect" title="Asia Raja">Asia Raja</a></i> in 1942,<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMahayana2007209.E2.80.93215_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMahayana2007209.E2.80.93215-34">[30]</a></sup> Andjar focused on theatre, forming the troupe Tjahaya Timoer.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBiran2009329_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBiran2009329-35">[31]</a></sup> He often visited the Cultural Centre (Keimin Bunka Sidosho) in Jakarta, where two employees, <a href="/wiki/D._Djajakusuma" title="D. Djajakusuma">D. Djajakusuma</a> and <a href="/wiki/Usmar_Ismail" title="Usmar Ismail">Usmar Ismail</a>, discussed filmmaking with him. Both became influential film directors during the 1950s.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[e]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESaid198234_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESaid198234-37">[32]</a></sup></p>
<p>After <a href="/wiki/Proclamation_of_Indonesian_Independence" title="Proclamation of Indonesian Independence">Indonesia's independence</a>, Andjar moved to <a href="/wiki/Purwokerto" title="Purwokerto">Purwokerto</a> to lead the daily <i>Perdjoeangan Rakjat</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEncyclopedia_of_Jakarta.2C_Andjar_Asmara_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEncyclopedia_of_Jakarta.2C_Andjar_Asmara-1">[1]</a></sup> After the paper collapsed,<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEI.N.1981215_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEI.N.1981215-38">[33]</a></sup> he returned to film, film a piece entitled <i><a href="/wiki/Djaoeh_Dimata" title="Djaoeh Dimata">Djaoeh Dimata</a></i> for the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands_Indies_Civil_Administration" title="Netherlands Indies Civil Administration">Netherlands Indies Civil Administration</a> in 1948.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESaid198236.E2.80.9337_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESaid198236.E2.80.9337-39">[34]</a></sup> This was followed by two additional films, <i>Anggrek Bulan</i> (<i>Moon Orchid</i>; 1948) and <i><a href="/wiki/Gadis_Desa" title="Gadis Desa">Gadis Desa</a></i> (<i>Maiden from the Village</i>; 1949), both based on plays he wrote several years earlier.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEncyclopedia_of_Jakarta.2C_Andjar_Asmara_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEncyclopedia_of_Jakarta.2C_Andjar_Asmara-1">[1]</a></sup> In 1950, Andjar published his only novel, <i>Noesa Penida</i>, a critique of the <a href="/wiki/Balinese_caste_system" title="Balinese caste system">Balinese caste system</a>, which followed lovers from different levels of the social hierarchy.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMahayanaSofyanDian199586.E2.80.9388_40-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMahayanaSofyanDian199586.E2.80.9388-40">[35]</a></sup> Meanwhile, he continued to write and publish <a href="/wiki/Paperback" title="Paperback">paperback</a> serials adapted from local films.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFilmindonesia.or.id.2C_Abisin_Abbas_41-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFilmindonesia.or.id.2C_Abisin_Abbas-41">[36]</a></sup></p>
<p>Andjar's screenplay <i>Dr Samsi</i> was finally adapted as a film in 1952 by Ratna Asmara, who had become Indonesia's first female film director with her 1950 film <i><a href="/wiki/Sedap_Malam" title="Sedap Malam">Sedap Malam</a></i> (<i>Sweetness of the Night</i>). The adaptation starred Ratna and Raden Ismail.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFilmindonesia.or.id.2C_Dr_Samsi_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFilmindonesia.or.id.2C_Dr_Samsi-7">[7]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESwestin2009104_42-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESwestin2009104-42">[37]</a></sup> It would prove Andjar's last screenwriting credit during his lifetime.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFilmindonesia.or.id.2C_Filmografi_30-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFilmindonesia.or.id.2C_Filmografi-30">[26]</a></sup> Although no longer writing films, Andjar remained active in the country's film industry. In 1955 he headed the inaugural <a href="/wiki/Indonesian_Film_Festival" title="Indonesian Film Festival">Indonesian Film Festival</a>, which was criticized when it gave the Best Picture Award to two films, Usmar Ismail's <i><a href="/wiki/Lewat_Djam_Malam" title="Lewat Djam Malam">Lewat Djam Malam</a></i> (<i>After the Curfew</i>) and <a href="/wiki/Lilik_Sudjio" title="Lilik Sudjio">Lilik Sudjio</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Tarmina" title="Tarmina">Tarmina</a></i>. Critics wrote that <i>Lewat Djam Malam</i> was easily the stronger of the two and suggested that <a href="/wiki/Djamaluddin_Malik" title="Djamaluddin Malik">Djamaluddin Malik</a>, <i>Tarmina</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'s</span> producer, had influenced the jury's decision.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">[f]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESaid198243_44-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESaid198243-44">[38]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1958 Asmara became the head of the entertainment magazine <i>Varia</i>, where the fellow director <a href="/wiki/Raden_Ariffien" class="mw-redirect" title="Raden Ariffien">Raden Ariffien</a> served as his deputy. Asmara held the position until his death; among other roles, he wrote a series of memoires on the history of theatre in the country.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEncyclopedia_of_Jakarta.2C_Andjar_Asmara_1-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEncyclopedia_of_Jakarta.2C_Andjar_Asmara-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPringgodigdoShadily197396.E2.80.9397_45-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPringgodigdoShadily197396.E2.80.9397-45">[39]</a></sup> He died on 20&#160;October 1961 in Cipanas, <a href="/wiki/West_Java" title="West Java">West Java</a>, during a trip to <a href="/wiki/Bandung" title="Bandung">Bandung</a><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFilmindonesia.or.id.2C_Abisin_Abbas_41-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFilmindonesia.or.id.2C_Abisin_Abbas-41">[36]</a></sup> and was buried in Jakarta.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPringgodigdoShadily197396.E2.80.9397_45-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPringgodigdoShadily197396.E2.80.9397-45">[39]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">Legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Andjar_Asmara&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Andjar's <i>toneels</i> were generally based on day-to-day experiences, rather than the tales of princes and ancient wars which were standard at the time.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFilmindonesia.or.id.2C_Dr_Samsi_7-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFilmindonesia.or.id.2C_Dr_Samsi-7">[7]</a></sup> Regarding Andjar's <i>toneels</i>, the Indonesian literary critic <a href="/wiki/Bakri_Siregar" title="Bakri Siregar">Bakri Siregar</a> writes that Andjar's stage plays, as well as those of fellow dramatist <a href="/wiki/Njoo_Cheong_Seng" title="Njoo Cheong Seng">Njoo Cheong Seng</a>, revitalised the genre and made the works more realistic. However, he considered the conflict in these works to have been poorly developed.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESiregar196468_46-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESiregar196468-46">[40]</a></sup> Andjar believed that the Padangsche Opera's performances influenced other troupes in <a href="/wiki/West_Sumatra" title="West Sumatra">West Sumatra</a> to adapt the <i>toneel</i> format, which later spread throughout the Indies.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECohen2003215.E2.80.93216_3-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECohen2003215.E2.80.93216-3">[3]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECohen2006347_47-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECohen2006347-47">[41]</a></sup></p>
<p>Matthew Isaac Cohen, a scholar of Indonesian performing arts, describes Andjar as "Indonesia's foremost theater critic during the colonial period", noting that he wrote extensively on the history of theatre in the Indies. Cohen also believes that Andjar also worked to justify the <i>toneel</i> style and distance it from the earlier <i>stambul</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECohen2006347.E2.80.93348.2C_394_48-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECohen2006347.E2.80.93348.2C_394-48">[42]</a></sup> Even after entering the film industry, Andjar considered the theatre more culturally significant than cinema.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBiran200925_21-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBiran200925-21">[17]</a></sup> However, the Indonesian journalist Soebagijo I.N. writes that Andjar remains best known for his film work.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEI.N.1981212_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEI.N.1981212-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Andjar was one of the first native Indonesian film directors, with Bachtiar Effendi, Soeska, and <a href="/wiki/Inoe_Perbatasari" title="Inoe Perbatasari">Inoe Perbatasari</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">[g]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESaid1982107_50-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESaid1982107-50">[43]</a></sup> Said writes that Andjar was forced to follow the whims of the <a href="/wiki/Chinese_Indonesians" title="Chinese Indonesians">ethnic Chinese</a> film moguls, which resulted in the films' shift toward commercial orientation, rather than the prioritisation of artistic merit.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESaid198230_51-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESaid198230-51">[44]</a></sup> The film historian <a href="/wiki/Misbach_Yusa_Biran" title="Misbach Yusa Biran">Misbach Yusa Biran</a> writes that Andjar and his fellow journalists, upon joining JIF, brought with them new ideas that helped the company flourish until it closed after the arrival of the Japanese;<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBiran2009220_52-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBiran2009220-52">[45]</a></sup> the company and its subsidiaries released fifteen films in two years.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBiran2009383.E2.80.93385_53-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBiran2009383.E2.80.93385-53">[46]</a></sup></p>

<li id="cite_note-17"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-17">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">In his final letter as editor of <i>Doenia Film</i>, as quoted by <a href="#CITEREFBiran2009">Biran (2009</a>, pp.&#160;6, 14, 20), Andjar wrote that he was joining Dardanella to help promote <i>toneel</i> as an art form to the best of his abilities.</span></li>
<li id="cite_note-36"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-36">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">Ismail's 1950 film <i><a href="/wiki/Darah_dan_Doa" title="Darah dan Doa">Darah dan Doa</a></i> (released internationally as <i>The Long March</i>, literally <i>Blood and Prayers</i>) is generally considered the first truly Indonesian film (<a href="#CITEREFBiran2009">Biran 2009</a>, p.&#160;45), while Djajakusuma was known for incorporating various aspects of traditional <a href="/wiki/Indonesian_culture" class="mw-redirect" title="Indonesian culture">Indonesian culture</a> in his films (<a href="#CITEREFSenHill2000">Sen &amp; Hill 2000</a>, p.&#160;156).</span></li>
<li id="cite_note-43"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-43">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text"><a href="#CITEREFSaid1982">Said (1982</a>, p.&#160;44) writes that Malik had previously influenced a contest for favourite actress in 1954, ensuring that an actress from his company, Persari, was chosen.</span></li>
