<p><b>Anna</b> (or <b>Onna</b>; killed 653 or 654) was <a href="/wiki/List_of_monarchs_of_East_Anglia" title="List of monarchs of East Anglia">king of East Anglia</a> from the early 640s until his death. He was a member of the <a href="/wiki/Wuffingas" title="Wuffingas">Wuffingas</a> family, the ruling dynasty of the East Angles. He was one of the three sons of <a href="/wiki/Eni_of_East_Anglia" title="Eni of East Anglia">Eni</a> who ruled the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_East_Anglia" title="Kingdom of East Anglia">kingdom of East Anglia</a>, succeeding some time after <a href="/wiki/Ecgric_of_East_Anglia" title="Ecgric of East Anglia">Ecgric</a> was killed in battle by <a href="/wiki/Penda_of_Mercia" title="Penda of Mercia">Penda of Mercia</a>. Anna was praised by <a href="/wiki/Bede" title="Bede">Bede</a> for his devotion to Christianity and was renowned for the saintliness of his family: his son <a href="/wiki/Jurmin" title="Jurmin">Jurmin</a> and all his daughters&#160;– <a href="/wiki/Seaxburh_of_Ely" title="Seaxburh of Ely">Seaxburh</a>, <a href="/wiki/%C3%86thelthryth" title="Æthelthryth">Æthelthryth</a>, <a href="/wiki/%C3%86thelburh_of_Faremoutiers" title="Æthelburh of Faremoutiers">Æthelburh</a> and possibly a fourth, <a href="/wiki/Wihtburh" title="Wihtburh">Wihtburh</a>&#160;– were canonised.</p>
<p>Little is known of Anna's life or his reign, as few records have survived from this period. In 631 he may have been at <a href="/wiki/Exning" title="Exning">Exning</a>, close to the <a href="/wiki/Devil%27s_Dyke,_Cambridgeshire" title="Devil's Dyke, Cambridgeshire">Devil's Dyke</a>. In 645 <a href="/wiki/Cenwalh_of_Wessex" title="Cenwalh of Wessex">Cenwalh of Wessex</a> was driven from his kingdom by Penda and, due to Anna's influence, he was converted to Christianity while living as an exile at the East Anglian court. Upon his return from exile, Cenwalh re-established Christianity in his own kingdom and the people of Wessex then remained firmly Christian.</p>
<p>Around 651 the land around <a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Ely" title="Isle of Ely">Ely</a> was absorbed into East Anglia, following the marriage of Anna's daughter Æthelthryth. Anna richly endowed the monastery at <a href="/wiki/Cnobheresburg" title="Cnobheresburg">Cnobheresburg</a>. In 651, in the aftermath of an attack by Penda on Cnobheresburg, Anna was forced to flee into exile, perhaps to the western kingdom of the <a href="/wiki/Magons%C3%A6te" title="Magonsæte">Magonsæte</a>. He returned to East Anglia in about 653, but soon afterwards the kingdom was attacked again by Penda and at the Battle of Bulcamp the East Anglian army, led by Anna, was defeated by the Mercians, and Anna and his son Jurmin were both killed. Anna was succeeded by his brother, <a href="/wiki/%C3%86thelhere_of_East_Anglia" title="Æthelhere of East Anglia">Æthelhere</a>. <a href="/wiki/Botwulf_of_Thorney" title="Botwulf of Thorney">Botolph</a>'s monastery at <a href="/wiki/Iken" title="Iken">Iken</a> may have been built in commemoration of the king. After Anna's reign, East Anglia seems to have been eclipsed by its more powerful neighbour, <a href="/wiki/Mercia" title="Mercia">Mercia</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sources">Sources</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anna_of_East_Anglia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Sources">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The kingdom of East Anglia (<a href="/wiki/Old_English_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Old English language">Old English</a>: <span lang="ang" xml:lang="ang"><i>Ēast Engla Rīce</i></span>) was a small independent <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Saxons" title="Anglo-Saxons">Anglo-Saxon</a> kingdom that comprised what are now the English counties of <a href="/wiki/Norfolk" title="Norfolk">Norfolk</a> and <a href="/wiki/Suffolk" title="Suffolk">Suffolk</a> and perhaps the eastern part of the Cambridgeshire <a href="/wiki/Fens" class="mw-redirect" title="Fens">Fens</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-eb11_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-eb11-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>In contrast to the kingdoms of <a href="/wiki/Northumbria" class="mw-redirect" title="Northumbria">Northumbria</a>, Mercia and <a href="/wiki/Wessex" title="Wessex">Wessex</a>, little reliable evidence about the kingdom of the East Angles has survived, because of the destruction of its monasteries and the disappearance of the two East Anglian <a href="/wiki/Episcopal_see" title="Episcopal see">sees</a> that occurred as the result of <a href="/wiki/Viking" class="mw-redirect" title="Viking">Viking</a> raids and settlement.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The main primary sources for information about Anna's life and reign are the <i><a href="/wiki/Historia_ecclesiastica_gentis_Anglorum" class="mw-redirect" title="Historia ecclesiastica gentis Anglorum">Historia ecclesiastica gentis Anglorum</a></i> (<i>Ecclesiastical History of the English People</i>), completed in Northumbria by Bede in 731, and the <i><a href="/wiki/Anglo-Saxon_Chronicle" title="Anglo-Saxon Chronicle">Anglo-Saxon Chronicle</a></i>, initially written in the ninth century, which mentions Anna's death. The mediaeval work known as the <i><a href="/wiki/Liber_Eliensis" title="Liber Eliensis">Liber Eliensis</a></i>, written in <a href="/wiki/Ely_Cathedral" title="Ely Cathedral">Ely</a> in the twelfth century, is a source of information about Anna's daughters Æthelthryth and Seaxburh, and also describes Anna's death and burial.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life_and_marriage">Early life and marriage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anna_of_East_Anglia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Early life and marriage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Devil%27s_Dyke_Cambs.jpg" class="image"><img alt="Colour photograph" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/ff/Devil%27s_Dyke_Cambs.jpg/170px-Devil%27s_Dyke_Cambs.jpg" width="170" height="197" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/ff/Devil%27s_Dyke_Cambs.jpg/255px-Devil%27s_Dyke_Cambs.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/ff/Devil%27s_Dyke_Cambs.jpg/340px-Devil%27s_Dyke_Cambs.jpg 2x" data-file-width="575" data-file-height="668" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Devil%27s_Dyke_Cambs.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The Devil's Dyke, near Exning. Anna may have been at Exning in 631.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Anna was the son of Eni, a member of the ruling Wuffingas family, and nephew of <a href="/wiki/R%C3%A6dwald_of_East_Anglia" title="Rædwald of East Anglia">Rædwald</a>, king of the East Angles from 600 to 625.<sup id="cite_ref-Oxford_DNB_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Oxford_DNB-4">[4]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_East_Anglia" title="Kingdom of East Anglia">East Anglia</a> was an early and long-lived <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Saxon_England" class="mw-redirect" title="Anglo-Saxon England">Anglo-Saxon</a> kingdom in which a duality of a northern and a southern part existed, corresponding with the modern English counties of <a href="/wiki/Norfolk" title="Norfolk">Norfolk</a> and <a href="/wiki/Suffolk" title="Suffolk">Suffolk</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Anna was married: Bede refers to the saint <a href="/wiki/S%C3%A6thryth" title="Sæthryth">Sæthryth</a> as "daughter of the wife of Anna, king of the East Angles".<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Folcard" title="Folcard">Abbott Folcard</a>'s <i>Life of St Botolph</i>, written in the 11th century, Botolph is described as having been at one time the chaplain to the sisters of a king, Æthelmund, whose mother was named Sæwara. Folcard names two of Sæwara's kinsmen as Æthelhere and Æthelwold. Since these are the names of two of Anna's brothers, Steven Plunkett suggests that it is "tempting" to consider that Sæwara was married to Anna, and that Æthelmund might either be Anna's full name, or the name of an otherwise unknown East Anglian sub-king.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> The <i>Liber Eliensis</i> names <a href="/wiki/Hereswith" title="Hereswith">Hereswith</a>, the sister of <a href="/wiki/Hilda_of_Whitby" title="Hilda of Whitby">Hild, abbess of Whitby</a>, as Anna's wife and the mother of Sæthryth, Seaxburh of Ely and Æthelthryth.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> However, the <i>Liber Eliensis</i> is regarded with caution by historians: Rosalind Love says that the mediaeval writers who interpreted Bede's information about Hereswith made an "erroneous assumption" regarding her connection with Anna and his family.<sup id="cite_ref-Oxford_DNB_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Oxford_DNB-4">[4]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Historians now believe that Hereswith was Anna's sister-in-law and that around the time that she married into the East Anglian royal family, Anna had already been king for a decade.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 631 Anna was probably at the Suffolk village of Exning, an important settlement with royal connections,<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> and, according to the <i>Liber Eliensis</i>, the birthplace of his daughter Æthelthryth.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> By tradition, Æthelthryth is said to have been baptised at Exning in a pool known as St Mindred's Well.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Exning was an important place strategically, as it stood just on the East Anglian side of the Devil's Dyke, a major earthwork stretching between the <a href="/wiki/Fen" title="Fen">Fen</a> edge and the headwaters of the <a href="/wiki/River_Stour,_Suffolk" title="River Stour, Suffolk">River Stour</a>, built at an earlier date to defend the East Anglian region from attack. An early Anglo-Saxon cemetery discovered there suggests the existence of an important site nearby, possibly a royal estate or regio.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="King_of_the_East_Angles">King of the East Angles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anna_of_East_Anglia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: King of the East Angles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Accession_and_rule">Accession and rule</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anna_of_East_Anglia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Accession and rule">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a href="/wiki/File:England_green_top.svg" class="image"><img alt="Map of Anglo-Saxon Britain" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0b/England_green_top.svg/250px-England_green_top.svg.png" width="250" height="314" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0b/England_green_top.svg/375px-England_green_top.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0b/England_green_top.svg/500px-England_green_top.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="963" data-file-height="1210" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:England_green_top.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The main Anglo-Saxon kingdoms</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>During 632 or 633 <a href="/wiki/Edwin_of_Northumbria" title="Edwin of Northumbria">Edwin of Northumbria</a>, with his centre of Christian power north of the River <a href="/wiki/Humber" title="Humber">Humber</a>, was overthrown. Edwin was slain and Northumbria was ravaged by <a href="/wiki/Cadwallon_ap_Cadfan" title="Cadwallon ap Cadfan">Cadwallon ap Cadfan</a>, supported by the Mercian king, <a href="/wiki/Penda_of_Mercia" title="Penda of Mercia">Penda</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> The Mercians then turned on the kingdom of the East Angles and their king, <a href="/wiki/Ecgric_of_East_Anglia" title="Ecgric of East Anglia">Ecgric</a>. At an unknown date (possibly in the early 640s),<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> they routed the East Anglian army and Ecgric and his predecessor <a href="/wiki/Sigeberht_of_East_Anglia" title="Sigeberht of East Anglia">Sigeberht</a> were both slain.<sup id="cite_ref-W110-13_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-W110-13-17">[17]</a></sup> D. P. Kirby has suggested that as Sigeberht was alive when the Irish monk <a href="/wiki/Saint_Fursey" title="Saint Fursey">Fursey</a> left for Gaul and found <a href="/wiki/Erchinoald" title="Erchinoald">Erchinoald</a>, (which happened after Erchinoald became <a href="/wiki/Mayor_of_the_Palace" title="Mayor of the Palace">Mayor of the Neustrian palace</a> in 641), Sigeberht was probably killed around 640 or 641.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> Penda's victory marked the end of the line of kings of the East Angles who were directly descended from Rædwald.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> Some time after Penda's victory, Anna became king of the East Angles, though the date of his accession is quite uncertain. The <i>Liber Eliensis</i> says that Anna died in the nineteenth year of his reign, and since he died in the mid-650s this would indicate a date around 635.<sup id="cite_ref-K1_108-9_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-K1_108-9-20">[20]</a></sup> However, the <i>Liber Eliensis</i> is regarded by some historians as unreliable on this point,<sup id="cite_ref-Oxford_DNB_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Oxford_DNB-4">[4]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Barbara_Yorke" title="Barbara Yorke">Barbara Yorke</a> suggests a possible date in the early 640s for Anna's accession, noting that it could not have been after 645 as Anna is recorded as giving refuge to Cenwalh of Wessex in that year.<sup id="cite_ref-Y63_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Y63-21">[21]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[note 1]</a></sup> It is probable that Anna became king with the assistance of the northern Angles.<sup id="cite_ref-Kirby.2C_p.79_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kirby.2C_p.79-25">[24]</a></sup> Throughout his reign he was the victim of Mercian aggression under Penda, but he also seems to have challenged the rise of Penda's power.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[25]</a></sup> The British medievalist <a href="/wiki/David_Dumville" title="David Dumville">David Dumville</a> has written that due to their rivalry for control over the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Angles" title="Middle Angles">Middle Anglian</a> people, Mercia and East Anglia probably became hereditary enemies and Penda repeatedly attacked the East Angles from the mid-630s to 654.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>Anna arranged an important diplomatic marriage between his daughter Seaxburh and <a href="/wiki/Eorcenberht_of_Kent" title="Eorcenberht of Kent">Eorcenberht of Kent</a>, cementing an alliance between the two kingdoms.<sup id="cite_ref-Y65-6_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Y65-6-28">[27]</a></sup> It was by means of marriages such as this that the <a href="/wiki/List_of_monarchs_of_Kent" title="List of monarchs of Kent">kings of Kent</a> could become well-connected to other royal dynasties.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[28]</a></sup> Not all of Anna's daughters were married into other royal families. During the 640s Anna's daughter Æthelburg and his stepdaughter Sæthryth entered <a href="/wiki/Faremoutiers_Abbey" title="Faremoutiers Abbey">Faremoutiers Abbey</a> in <a href="/wiki/Gaul" title="Gaul">Gaul</a> to live religious lives under abbess <a href="/wiki/Saint_Fara" class="mw-redirect" title="Saint Fara">Fara</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Y65-6_28-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Y65-6-28">[27]</a></sup> They were the first royal Anglo-Saxons to become nuns, making religious seclusion "an acceptable and desirable vocation for ex-queens and royal princesses", according to Barbara Yorke.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[note 2]</a></sup></p>
<p>D. P. Kirby uses the presence of East Anglian princesses living under the veil in Gaul as evidence of the Frankish orientation of Anna's kingdom at this time, continued since the reign of his predecessor Rædwald.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[31]</a></sup> The Wuffingas dynasty may have been connected with monastic foundations in the area around Faramoutiers through Anna's predecessor Sigeberht, who had spent several years as an exile in Gaul and had become a devout and learned Christian due to his experiences of monastic life.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[32]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Williamson_p16_3.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/40/Williamson_p16_3.svg/250px-Williamson_p16_3.svg.png" width="250" height="220" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/40/Williamson_p16_3.svg/375px-Williamson_p16_3.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/40/Williamson_p16_3.svg/500px-Williamson_p16_3.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="691" data-file-height="607" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Williamson_p16_3.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The kingdom of East Anglia during the reign of Anna</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In 641 <a href="/wiki/Oswald_of_Northumbria" title="Oswald of Northumbria">Oswald</a> of <a href="/wiki/Northumbria" class="mw-redirect" title="Northumbria">Northumbria</a> was slain in battle by Penda (probably at <a href="/wiki/Oswestry" title="Oswestry">Oswestry</a> in Shropshire). Due to his death, Northumbria was split into two. The northern part, <a href="/wiki/Bernicia" title="Bernicia">Bernicia</a>, accepted Oswald's brother <a href="/wiki/Oswiu_of_Northumbria" class="mw-redirect" title="Oswiu of Northumbria">Oswiu</a> as their new king, but the southern <a href="/wiki/Deira" title="Deira">Deirans</a> refused to accept him and were ruled instead by a king of the original Deiran house, <a href="/wiki/Oswine_of_Deira" title="Oswine of Deira">Oswine</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[33]</a></sup> Soon afterwards <a href="/wiki/Cenwalh_of_Wessex" title="Cenwalh of Wessex">Cenwalh of Wessex</a>, the brother of Oswald's widow and himself married to Penda's sister, renounced his wife.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[34]</a></sup> In 645, according to the <i>Anglo-Saxon Chronicle</i>, Penda drove Cenwalh from his kingdom and into exile. During the following year, while a refugee at Anna's court, he was converted to Christianity,<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">[35]</a></sup> returning in 648 to rule Wessex as a Christian king.<sup id="cite_ref-Oxford_DNB_4-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Oxford_DNB-4">[4]</a></sup> Anna probably provided military support for Cenwalh's return to his throne.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">[36]</a></sup></p>
<p>Anna's hold on the western limits of his kingdom, which bordered on the Fen lands that surrounded the Isle of Ely, was strengthened by the marriage in 651 (or slightly later) of his daughter Æthelthryth to Tondberht, a prince of the South <a href="/wiki/Gyrwas" title="Gyrwas">Gyrwe</a>, a people living in the fens who may have been settled in the area around Ely.<sup id="cite_ref-Y63_21-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Y63-21">[21]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">[note 3]</a></sup> Æthelthryth, accompanied by her minister Owine, travelled from Ely to Northumbria when she married for the second time, to <a href="/wiki/Ecgfrith_of_Northumbria" title="Ecgfrith of Northumbria">Ecgfrith</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Y70-1_41-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Y70-1-41">[38]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Exile">Exile</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anna_of_East_Anglia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Exile">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Wall-of-Burgh-Castle-England-UK.jpg" class="image"><img alt="Monochrome drawing" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1d/Wall-of-Burgh-Castle-England-UK.jpg/220px-Wall-of-Burgh-Castle-England-UK.jpg" width="220" height="193" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1d/Wall-of-Burgh-Castle-England-UK.jpg/330px-Wall-of-Burgh-Castle-England-UK.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1d/Wall-of-Burgh-Castle-England-UK.jpg/440px-Wall-of-Burgh-Castle-England-UK.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1600" data-file-height="1400" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Wall-of-Burgh-Castle-England-UK.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The ruins of <a href="/wiki/Burgh_Castle" title="Burgh Castle">Burgh Castle</a>, the possible site of the monastery at Cnobheresburg, as depicted in 1845</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>During his reign Anna endowed the monastery at <a href="/wiki/Burgh_Castle" title="Burgh Castle">Cnobheresburg</a> with rich buildings and objects.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">[39]</a></sup> The monastery was built in about 633 by Fursey after he arrived in East Anglia. In time, weary of attacks on the kingdom, Fursey left East Anglia for good, leaving the monastery to his brother <a href="/wiki/Foillan" title="Foillan">Foillan</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-W110-13_17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-W110-13-17">[17]</a></sup> When in 651 Penda attacked the monastery, Anna and his men arrived and held the Mercians back. This gave Foillan and his monks enough time to escape with their books and valuables, but Penda defeated Anna and drove him into exile, possibly to the kingdom of <a href="/wiki/Merewalh" title="Merewalh">Merewalh</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Magons%C3%A6te" title="Magonsæte">Magonsætan</a>, in western <a href="/wiki/Shropshire" title="Shropshire">Shropshire</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">[40]</a></sup> He returned to East Anglia in about 654.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">[41]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Death.2C_burial_place_and_successors">Death, burial place and successors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anna_of_East_Anglia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Death, burial place and successors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Soon after 653, when Penda made his son <a href="/wiki/Peada_of_Mercia" title="Peada of Mercia">Peada</a> the ruler of the Middle Angles (but still continued to rule his own country),<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">[42]</a></sup> the Mercian assault on East Anglia was repeated. The opposing armies of Penda and Anna met at Bulcamp, near <a href="/wiki/Blythburgh" title="Blythburgh">Blythburgh</a> in Suffolk. The East Anglians were defeated and many were slain, including King Anna and his son <a href="/wiki/Jurmin" title="Jurmin">Jurmin</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-W110-13_17-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-W110-13-17">[17]</a></sup> Anna's death is mentioned in the <i>Anglo-Saxon Chronicle</i> in the entry for 653 or 654, <i>"Her Anna cining werð ofslagen&#160;..."</i> &#160;– 'Here Anna was killed'&#160;– but no other details of the battle in which he died are given.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">[43]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">[note 4]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Marshland_near_Blythburgh_-_geograph.org.uk_-_192171.jpg" class="image"><img alt="Colour photograph" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e8/Marshland_near_Blythburgh_-_geograph.org.uk_-_192171.jpg/220px-Marshland_near_Blythburgh_-_geograph.org.uk_-_192171.jpg" width="220" height="166" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e8/Marshland_near_Blythburgh_-_geograph.org.uk_-_192171.jpg/330px-Marshland_near_Blythburgh_-_geograph.org.uk_-_192171.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e8/Marshland_near_Blythburgh_-_geograph.org.uk_-_192171.jpg/440px-Marshland_near_Blythburgh_-_geograph.org.uk_-_192171.jpg 2x" data-file-width="599" data-file-height="452" /></a>

Marshland around Blythburgh, near where Anna met his death</div>
</div>
</div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Blythburgh_writing_tablet.jpg" class="image"><img alt="drawing" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/0/0d/Blythburgh_writing_tablet.jpg/220px-Blythburgh_writing_tablet.jpg" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/0/0d/Blythburgh_writing_tablet.jpg/330px-Blythburgh_writing_tablet.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/0/0d/Blythburgh_writing_tablet.jpg/440px-Blythburgh_writing_tablet.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1252" data-file-height="838" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Blythburgh_writing_tablet.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A drawing of the writing-tablet found near a possible monastic site at Blythburgh</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Blythburgh, a mile from Bulcamp and situated near the fordable headwaters of the <a href="/wiki/River_Blyth,_Suffolk" title="River Blyth, Suffolk">Blyth</a> estuary, was afterwards believed to be the location of the tombs of Anna and Jurmin.<sup id="cite_ref-Oxford_DNB_4-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Oxford_DNB-4">[4]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Y70-1_41-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Y70-1-41">[38]</a></sup> It is a candidate for a monastic site or a royal regio (estate). According to Peter Warner, the Latin derivation of part of the nearby place-name 'Bulcamp' indicates its ancient origins, and mediaeval sources which claim continuous Christian worship at Blythburgh throughout the Anglo-Saxon period provide circumstantial evidence of its connections with East Anglian royalty and Christianity.<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">[45]</a></sup> Part of an 8th-century whalebone <a href="/wiki/Diptych" title="Diptych">diptych</a> or writing-tablet, used for liturgical purposes, has been found near the site.<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">[46]</a></sup></p>
<p>Saint Botolph began to build his monastery at Icanho, now conclusively identified as <a href="/wiki/Iken" title="Iken">Iken</a>, Suffolk,<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">[47]</a></sup> in the year that Anna was killed, possibly to commemorate the king.<sup id="cite_ref-Y70-1_41-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Y70-1-41">[38]</a></sup> Anna was succeeded in turn by his two brothers Æthelhere and <a href="/wiki/%C3%86thelwold_of_East_Anglia" title="Æthelwold of East Anglia">Æthelwold</a>, who may have ruled jointly.<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">[48]</a></sup> It is possible that Æthelhere was set up as a puppet ruler by Penda or was his ally, as he was one of the 30 <i>duces</i> that accompanied Penda when he attacked Oswiu of Northumbria at an unidentified location called the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_the_Winw%C3%A6d" class="mw-redirect" title="Battle of the Winwæd">Winwæd</a> in 655 or 656. Penda himself was killed at the Winwæd, after having steadily increased his power over a period of 13 years.<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">[49]</a></sup> Æthelhere (who was also slain at the Battle of the Winwæd) and Æthelwold were succeeded by the descendants of Anna's youngest brother, Æthelric.<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">[50]</a></sup></p>
<p>Bede praised Anna's piety in his <i>Ecclesiastical History of the English People</i>,<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">[51]</a></sup> and modern historians have since regarded Anna as a devout king,<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">[52]</a></sup> but his reputation as a devoted Christian is mainly because he produced a son and four daughters who were all made into <a href="/wiki/List_of_Anglo-Saxon_saints" title="List of Anglo-Saxon saints">Anglo-Saxon saints</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">[53]</a></sup> Five hundred years after his death, his tomb at Blythburgh was (according to the <i>Liber Eliensis</i>) still "venerated by the pious devotion of faithful people".<sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">[54]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Descendants">Descendants</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anna_of_East_Anglia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Descendants">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Anna's children were all canonised. The eldest, Seaxburh, was the wife of Eorcenberht of Kent. She ruled Kent from 664 until her son <a href="/wiki/Ecgberht_of_Kent" title="Ecgberht of Kent">Ecgberht</a> came of age. <a href="/wiki/%C3%86thelthryth" title="Æthelthryth">Æthelthryth</a>, according to the <i>Anglo-Saxon Chronicle</i>, founded the <a href="/wiki/Ely_Cathedral" title="Ely Cathedral">monastery at Ely</a> in 673. Another daughter, <a href="/wiki/%C3%86thelburh_of_Faremoutiers" title="Æthelburh of Faremoutiers">Æthelburh</a>, spent her life at the nunnery of Faremoutiers. Anna's son, <a href="/wiki/Jurmin" title="Jurmin">Jurmin</a>, was of warrior age in 653 when he was killed in battle.</p>
<p>By tradition, Anna is said to have had a fourth daughter, <a href="/wiki/Wihtburh" title="Wihtburh">Wihtburh</a>, an abbess at <a href="/wiki/Dereham" title="Dereham">Dereham</a> (or possibly <a href="/wiki/West_Dereham" title="West Dereham">West Dereham</a>), where there was a royal <a href="/wiki/Double_monastery" title="Double monastery">double monastery</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59">[55]</a></sup> She may never have existed: Bede fails to mention her and she first appears in a calendar in the late 10th century <i>Bosworth Psalter</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60">[56]</a></sup> She may have been a character specifically created by the religious community at Ely, where her remains were supposed to have been taken after being stolen from Dereham<sup id="cite_ref-Y70-1_41-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Y70-1-41">[38]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-61" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-61">[57]</a></sup> and subsequently used as visual proof of the incorruptibility of a saint's body, a substitute for her sister Æthelthryth, whose body had to remain unexamined in her tomb.<sup id="cite_ref-62" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-62">[58]</a></sup> Manuscript F of the <i>Anglo-Saxon Chronicle</i>, which dates from about 1100, mentions Wihtburh's death when it records that her body was found uncorrupted in 798, 55 years after she died. The resulting date for her death of 743 is too far too late for her to have been a sister of Æthelthryth, who was born in 636.<sup id="cite_ref-63" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-63">[59]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-64" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-64">[60]</a></sup></p>

<div class="reflist" style="list-style-type: decimal;">
<ol class="references">
<li id="cite_note-24"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-24">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">Bede gives the story without dates, but makes it clear that Anna was king when Cenwalh came to East Anglia; the <i>Anglo-Saxon Chronicle</i> gives the date as 645 or 646 but does not specify that Anna was king at the time.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></span></li>
<li id="cite_note-32"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-32">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">A Christian king like Anna would have made a priority of demonstrating his commitment to his kingdom's new faith by acting in a way that would mark them out as holy, and the patronage of nunneries would have been of concern to him.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[29]</a></sup> It is not recorded why Anna's daughters took the veil. Yorke comments on the paucity of written documents regarding the princesses of East Anglia, considering the important role they played in the foundation of Anglo-Saxon royal nunneries.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[30]</a></sup></span></li>
<li id="cite_note-40"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-40">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">A <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.anglo-saxons.net/hwaet/?do=get&amp;type=map&amp;id=map8c">map</a> of southern England in the 8th century (drawn by <a href="/wiki/Reginald_Piggott" title="Reginald Piggott">Reginald Piggott</a>) gives an indication of where the Gyrwe people lived&#160;– between East Anglia and the land of the Middle Angles.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">[37]</a></sup></span></li>
<li id="cite_note-48"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-48">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">Manuscript A of the <i>Anglo-Saxon Chronicle</i> dates <i>Onna's</i> death at 654; <i>Anna's</i> demise is dated by Manuscript E at 653. See Swanton, <i>Anglo-Saxon Chronicle</i>, pp. xv–xvi for a discussion of some of the discrepancies between the different manuscripts and possible reasons for these.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">[44]</a></sup></span></li>
