			<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Antoine-Jean Gros</h1>

<p><b>Antoine-Jean Gros</b> (16 March 1771 – 25 June 1835), <a href="/wiki/Baron_of_the_Empire" class="mw-redirect" title="Baron of the Empire">Baron of the Empire</a>, was a French <a href="/wiki/Painting" title="Painting">painter</a>. His work was in the genres of <a href="/wiki/History_painting" title="History painting">history</a> and <a href="/wiki/Neoclassicism" title="Neoclassicism">neoclassical</a> artwork.</p>
<p>Gros studied under <a href="/wiki/Jacques-Louis_David" title="Jacques-Louis David">Jacques-Louis David</a> in Paris and began an independent artistic career during the <a href="/wiki/French_Revolution" title="French Revolution">French Revolution</a>. Forced to leave France, he moved to <a href="/wiki/Genoa" title="Genoa">Genoa</a> and witnessed the nearby <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Arcole" title="Battle of Arcole">Battle of Arcole</a> (1796). Inspired by an event during the battle, he produced <a href="/wiki/Bonaparte_at_the_pont_d%27Arcole" class="mw-redirect" title="Bonaparte at the pont d'Arcole">a portrait of the French commander</a>, <a href="/wiki/Napoleon_Bonaparte" class="mw-redirect" title="Napoleon Bonaparte">Napoleon Bonaparte</a>, then a newly-promoted general. The portrait brought Gros to public attention and gained the patronage of Napoleon.</p>
<p>After travelling with Napoleon's army for several years, he returned to Paris in 1799. Gros produced several large paintings of battles and other events in Napoleon's life. These were mostly in a neoclassical style, but <i><a href="/wiki/Napol%C3%A9on_on_the_Battlefield_of_Eylau" title="Napoléon on the Battlefield of Eylau">Napoléon on the Battlefield of Eylau</a></i> adopted a more realistic portrayal of the horrors of war. Gros also painted portraits of officers in the French army and members of French high society. After the fall of Napoleon, he shifted his artistic focus and produced more history paintings, which art historians regard as less impressive than his earlier work.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life_and_training">Early life and training</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Antoine-Jean_Gros&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life and training">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:242px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Antoine-Jean_Gros_-_Equestrian_portrait_of_prince_Boris_Yusupov_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/66/Antoine-Jean_Gros_-_Equestrian_portrait_of_prince_Boris_Yusupov_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg/240px-Antoine-Jean_Gros_-_Equestrian_portrait_of_prince_Boris_Yusupov_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg" width="240" height="289" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/66/Antoine-Jean_Gros_-_Equestrian_portrait_of_prince_Boris_Yusupov_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg/360px-Antoine-Jean_Gros_-_Equestrian_portrait_of_prince_Boris_Yusupov_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/66/Antoine-Jean_Gros_-_Equestrian_portrait_of_prince_Boris_Yusupov_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg/480px-Antoine-Jean_Gros_-_Equestrian_portrait_of_prince_Boris_Yusupov_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3327" data-file-height="4001" /></a>

Equestrian portrait of prince <a href="/wiki/Boris_Yusupov" class="mw-redirect" title="Boris Yusupov">Boris Yusupov</a>, 1809</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Born in <a href="/wiki/Paris" title="Paris">Paris</a>, Gros began to learn to draw at the age of six from his father, Jean-Antoine Gros,<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> who was a miniature painter, and showed himself as a gifted artist. Towards the close of 1785, Gros, by his own choice, entered the studio of <a href="/wiki/Jacques-Louis_David" title="Jacques-Louis David">Jacques-Louis David</a>, which he frequented assiduously, continuing at the same time to follow the classes of the <a href="/wiki/Coll%C3%A8ge_Mazarin" class="mw-redirect" title="Collège Mazarin">Collège Mazarin</a>.</p>
<p>The death of his father, whose circumstances had been embarrassed by the <a href="/wiki/French_Revolution" title="French Revolution">French Revolution</a>, threw Gros, in 1791, upon his own resources. He now devoted himself wholly to his profession, and competed (unsuccessfully) in 1792 for the <i>grand prix</i>. About this time, however, on the recommendation of the <a href="/wiki/%C3%89cole_des_Beaux_Arts" class="mw-redirect" title="École des Beaux Arts">École des Beaux Arts</a>, he was employed on the execution of portraits of the members of the <a href="/wiki/National_Convention" title="National Convention">National Convention</a>, and disturbed by the development of the Revolution, Gros left France in 1793 for Italy.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Genoa_and_Bonaparte">Genoa and Bonaparte</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Antoine-Jean_Gros&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Genoa and Bonaparte">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:1801_Antoine-Jean_Gros_-_Bonaparte_on_the_Bridge_at_Arcole.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f0/1801_Antoine-Jean_Gros_-_Bonaparte_on_the_Bridge_at_Arcole.jpg/220px-1801_Antoine-Jean_Gros_-_Bonaparte_on_the_Bridge_at_Arcole.jpg" width="220" height="311" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f0/1801_Antoine-Jean_Gros_-_Bonaparte_on_the_Bridge_at_Arcole.jpg/330px-1801_Antoine-Jean_Gros_-_Bonaparte_on_the_Bridge_at_Arcole.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f0/1801_Antoine-Jean_Gros_-_Bonaparte_on_the_Bridge_at_Arcole.jpg/440px-1801_Antoine-Jean_Gros_-_Bonaparte_on_the_Bridge_at_Arcole.jpg 2x" data-file-width="990" data-file-height="1400" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:1801_Antoine-Jean_Gros_-_Bonaparte_on_the_Bridge_at_Arcole.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<i>Bonaparte at the pont d'Arcole</i>, 1796</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>He supported himself at <a href="/wiki/Genoa" title="Genoa">Genoa</a> by the same means, producing a great quantity of miniatures and fixes. He visited <a href="/wiki/Florence" title="Florence">Florence</a>, but returned to Genoa where he made the acquaintance of <a href="/wiki/Jos%C3%A9phine_de_Beauharnais" title="Joséphine de Beauharnais">Joséphine de Beauharnais</a>. He followed her to <a href="/wiki/Milan" title="Milan">Milan</a>, where he was well received by her husband, <a href="/wiki/Napoleon_Bonaparte" class="mw-redirect" title="Napoleon Bonaparte">Napoleon Bonaparte</a>.</p>
<p>On 15 November 1796, Gros was present with the army near <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Arcole" title="Battle of Arcole">Arcola</a> when Bonaparte planted the <a href="/wiki/Flag_of_France" title="Flag of France">French tricolor</a> on the bridge. Gros seized on this incident, and showed by his treatment of it (entitled <i><a href="/wiki/Bonaparte_at_the_pont_d%27Arcole" class="mw-redirect" title="Bonaparte at the pont d'Arcole">Bonaparte at the pont d'Arcole</a></i>) that he had found his vocation. Bonaparte at once gave him the post of <i>inspecteur aux revues</i>, which enabled him to follow the army, and in 1797 nominated him on the commission charged to select the spoils which should enrich the <a href="/wiki/Louvre" title="Louvre">Louvre</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Paris">Paris</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Antoine-Jean_Gros&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Paris">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Antoine-Jean_Gros_-_Bataille_d%27Aboukir,_25_juillet_1799_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1e/Antoine-Jean_Gros_-_Bataille_d%27Aboukir%2C_25_juillet_1799_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg/220px-Antoine-Jean_Gros_-_Bataille_d%27Aboukir%2C_25_juillet_1799_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg" width="220" height="131" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1e/Antoine-Jean_Gros_-_Bataille_d%27Aboukir%2C_25_juillet_1799_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg/330px-Antoine-Jean_Gros_-_Bataille_d%27Aboukir%2C_25_juillet_1799_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1e/Antoine-Jean_Gros_-_Bataille_d%27Aboukir%2C_25_juillet_1799_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg/440px-Antoine-Jean_Gros_-_Bataille_d%27Aboukir%2C_25_juillet_1799_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg 2x" data-file-width="6060" data-file-height="3617" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Antoine-Jean_Gros_-_Bataille_d%27Aboukir,_25_juillet_1799_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>

</div>
<p>In 1799, having escaped from the besieged city of Genoa, Gros made his way to Paris, and in the beginning of 1801 took up his quarters in the Capucins. His esquisse of the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Nazareth" title="Battle of Nazareth">Battle of Nazareth</a> (now in the Musée de Nantes) gained the prize offered in 1802 by the <a href="/wiki/French_Consulate" title="French Consulate">consuls</a>, but was not carried out, owing it is said to the jealousy of <a href="/wiki/Jean-Andoche_Junot" title="Jean-Andoche Junot">Jean-Andoche Junot</a> felt by Napoleon; but he indemnified Gros by commissioning him to paint his own visit to the pest-house of <a href="/wiki/Jaffa" title="Jaffa">Jaffa</a>. <i>Les Pestiférés de Jaffa</i> (Louvre) was followed by <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Abukir_(1799)" title="Battle of Abukir (1799)"><i>The Battle of Aboukir, 1806</i></a> (Versailles), and <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Eylau" title="Battle of Eylau"><i>The Battle of Eylau, 1808</i></a> (Louvre).<sup id="cite_ref-PrendergastC-1997_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PrendergastC-1997-2">[2]</a></sup> According to the article about Gros in the <i>Encyclopædia Britannica</i>, Eleventh Edition, 1911, these three subjects – the popular leader facing the pestilence unmoved, challenging the splendid instant of victory, heart-sick with the bitter cost of a hard-won field – gave Gros his chief title to fame.</p>
<p><i>Britannica</i> further remarks that as long as the military element remained bound up with French national life, Gros received from it a fresh and energetic inspiration which carried him to the very heart of the events which he depicted; but as the army and its general separated from the people, Gros, called on to illustrate episodes representative only of the fulfilment of personal ambition, ceased to find the nourishment necessary to his genius, and the defect of his artistic position became evident. Trained in the sect of the Classicists, he was shackled by their rules, even when by his naturalistic treatment of types, and appeal to picturesque effect in color and tone, he seemed to run counter to them.</p>

<p>At the Salon of 1804, Gros debuted his painting <a href="/wiki/Bonaparte_visiting_the_plague-victims_of_Jaffa" class="mw-redirect" title="Bonaparte visiting the plague-victims of Jaffa"><i>Bonaparte Visiting the Plague Victims of Jaffa</i></a>. The painting launched his career as a successful painter. It depicts Bonaparte in Jaffa visiting soldiers infected with the bubonic plague. He is portrayed reaching out to one of the sick, unfazed by the illness. While Bonaparte did actually visit the pesthouse, later, as his army prepared to withdraw from Syria, he ordered the poisoning (with laudanum) of about fifty of his plague-infected men.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> The painting was commissioned as damage control when word spread of his actions. The painting is in the Neo-Classical style, though it shows elements such as the lighting and a taste for the exotic that are precursors to the upcoming Romantic ideals.</p>
<p>In 1810, his <i>Madrid</i> and <i>Napoleon at the Pyramids</i> (Versailles) show that his star had deserted him. His <a href="/wiki/Francis_I_of_France" title="Francis I of France">Francis I</a> and <a href="/wiki/Charles_V,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Charles V, Holy Roman Emperor">Charles V</a>, 1812 (Louvre), had considerable success; but the decoration of the dome of St. Genevieve (begun in 1811 and completed in 1824) is the only work of Gros's later years which shows his early force and vigour, as well as his skill. The "Departure of <a href="/wiki/Louis_XVIII_of_France" title="Louis XVIII of France">Louis XVIII</a>" (Versailles), the <i>Embarkation of Madame d'Angoulême</i> (Bordeaux), the <i>plafond</i> of the Egyptian room in the Louvre, and finally his <i>Hercules and Diomedes</i>, exhibited in 1835, testify only that Gros's efforts – in accordance with the frequent counsels of his old master David – to stem the rising tide of <a href="/wiki/Romanticism" title="Romanticism">Romanticism</a> only damaged his once brilliant reputation.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Death">Death</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Antoine-Jean_Gros&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Death">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Again citing <i>Britannica</i>, "Exasperated by criticism and the consciousness of failure, Gros sought refuge in the gros[ser] pleasures of life." On 25 June 1835, he was found drowned on the shores of the <a href="/wiki/Seine" title="Seine">Seine</a> at <a href="/wiki/Meudon" title="Meudon">Meudon</a>, near <a href="/wiki/S%C3%A8vres" title="Sèvres">Sèvres</a>. From a paper which he had placed in his hat, it became known that "tired of life, and betrayed by last faculties which rendered it bearable, he had resolved to end it."</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Renown">Renown</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Antoine-Jean_Gros&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Renown">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Gros was decorated and named <a href="/wiki/Baron_of_the_Empire" class="mw-redirect" title="Baron of the Empire">Baron of the Empire</a> by Napoleon, after the Salon of 1808, at which he had exhibited the <i>Battle of Eylau</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-PrendergastC-1997_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PrendergastC-1997-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>The number of Gros's pupils was very great, and was considerably augmented when, in 1815, David quit Paris and gave over his own classes to him. Under the <a href="/wiki/Bourbon_Restoration" title="Bourbon Restoration">Restoration</a>. he became a member of the Institute, professor at the <a href="/wiki/%C3%89cole_des_Beaux_Arts" class="mw-redirect" title="École des Beaux Arts">École des Beaux Arts</a>, and was named chevalier of the <a href="/wiki/Order_of_Saint_Michael" title="Order of Saint Michael">Order of Saint Michael</a>.</p>
<p>Gros had also been an inspiration to <a href="/wiki/Eugene_Delacroix" class="mw-redirect" title="Eugene Delacroix">Eugene Delacroix</a>, especially with his work in lithography. The two both worked in the same time period, and both did portraits of Napoleon. Though at one point, Gros had referred to Delacroix's <i>Chios and Missolonghi</i> as "a massacre of art".</p>
<p>G. Dargenty produced a book titled <i>Les Artistes Celebres</i> ("Famous Artists") <i>Le Baron Gros</i> GILBERT WOOD &amp; Co. London</p>
<p>M. Delcluze gives a brief notice of his life in <i>Louis David et son temps</i> ("Louis David and his Times"), and Julius Meyer's <i>Geschichte der modernen französischen Malerei</i> ("History of Modern French Painting") contains what <i>Britannica</i> cites as an excellent criticism on his works.</p>
