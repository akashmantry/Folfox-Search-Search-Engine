			<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Armenian Assembly of America</h1>
									<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">

				<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><p>The <b>Armenian Assembly of America</b> is the largest Washington-based nationwide organization promoting public understanding and awareness of Armenian issues. The organization aims to "strengthen U.S./<a href="/wiki/Armenia" title="Armenia">Armenia</a> and U.S. relations, promotes Armenia's democratic development and economic prosperity and seeks universal affirmation of the <a href="/wiki/Armenian_Genocide" title="Armenian Genocide">Armenian Genocide</a>" via "research, education and <a href="/wiki/Advocacy_group" title="Advocacy group">advocacy</a>."<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Armenian_Assembly_of_America&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the early 70's, Armenian-Americans decided to create an organization, headquartered in our nation's capital, to represent and promote Armenian interests. With Armenian-American community members across the country, they launched the Armenian Assembly in 1972. Ultimately, the coalition did not hold, but in its place was born a non-partisan organization dedicated to promoting public understanding and awareness of Armenian issues.</p>
<p>The Assembly would go on to launch a broad array of programs and initiatives and firmly establish itself as an Armenian voice within U.S. public policy circles. It would pioneer initiatives to commemorate and reaffirm the Armenian Genocide. And as early as 1977, the Assembly would introduce a summer intern program in Washington, DC that, some 32 years later, could claim more than 900 graduates.</p>
<p>Beginning in 1988 and thereafter, the Assembly addressed the unprecedented challenges of Armenia's earthquake, Nagorno Karabakh's struggle for self-determination and Armenia's independence movement.</p>
<p>With Administration support, Congress mandated first-ever earthquake relief funding to then Soviet Armenia. Annual U.S. assistance became the norm, the next ten years totaling more than $1.4 billion. The Assembly was also instrumental in encouraging our friends in Congress to form a Congressional Caucus on Armenian Issues.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> It would become one of the largest caucuses, working side-by-side with the Assembly in supporting Armenian-American interests.</p>
<p>The Armenia Tree Project (ATP)<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> was established in 1993 to assist the Armenian people in using trees to advance their social, economic and environmental recovery. Some 300,000 plantings later, ATP's vision continues to bloom throughout Armenia and Nagorno Karabakh.</p>
<p>Efforts also continued unabated to secure universal reaffirmation of the Armenian Genocide, enhanced significantly in 1997 with the launch of the Assembly's Armenian National Institute (ANI).<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> ANI is at the forefront of efforts to affirm the Armenian Genocide, responding to denial and advance knowledge and understanding of the Genocide and its consequences.</p>
<p>Completing three decades of non-partisan service, the Assembly family of organizations remains in the forefront of strengthening the U.S./Armenia and U.S./Karabakh relationships.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="NGO_Training_and_Resource_Center">NGO Training and Resource Center</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Armenian_Assembly_of_America&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: NGO Training and Resource Center">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In April 1994, the Armenian Assembly of America established the <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.ngoc.am/">NGO Training and Resource Center</a> with support from the "Save the Children" <a href="/wiki/USAID" class="mw-redirect" title="USAID">USAID</a> fund. The center aims to strengthen domestic Armenian <a href="/wiki/Non-Governmental_Organization" class="mw-redirect" title="Non-Governmental Organization">Non-Governmental Organizations</a> (NGOs) in hopes of encouraging wider participation in the democratic processes. The center provides, to more than 300 local registered NGOs, the following services:<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>ten-week training program focusing on the leadership and management of the organization;</li>
<li>technical assistance to help participants implement change within their organization;</li>
<li>public relations department to raise public awareness about NGO Sector in Armenia and to promote their activities and projects;</li>
<li>seminar/workshop series.</li>
</ul>
