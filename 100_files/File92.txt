<p><b>Augustine of Canterbury</b> (born first third of the 6th century&#160;– died probably 26 May 604) was a <a href="/wiki/Order_of_Saint_Benedict" title="Order of Saint Benedict">Benedictine</a> <a href="/wiki/Monk" title="Monk">monk</a> who became the first <a href="/wiki/Archbishop_of_Canterbury" title="Archbishop of Canterbury">Archbishop of Canterbury</a> in the year 597. He is considered the "Apostle to the English" and a founder of the English Church.<sup id="cite_ref-DictSaint_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DictSaint-4">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Augustine was the <a href="/wiki/Prior" title="Prior">prior</a> of a monastery in Rome when Pope <a href="/wiki/Pope_Gregory_I" title="Pope Gregory I">Gregory the Great</a> chose him in 595 to lead a mission, usually known as the <a href="/wiki/Gregorian_mission" title="Gregorian mission">Gregorian mission</a>, to Britain to <a href="/wiki/Christianization" title="Christianization">Christianize</a> King <a href="/wiki/%C3%86thelberht_of_Kent" title="Æthelberht of Kent">Æthelberht</a> and his <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Kent" title="Kingdom of Kent">Kingdom of Kent</a> from <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Saxon_paganism" title="Anglo-Saxon paganism">Anglo-Saxon paganism</a>. Kent was probably chosen because Æthelberht had married a Christian princess, <a href="/wiki/Bertha_of_Kent" title="Bertha of Kent">Bertha</a>, daughter of <a href="/wiki/Charibert_I" title="Charibert I">Charibert I</a> the <a href="/wiki/List_of_Frankish_kings" title="List of Frankish kings">King of Paris</a>, who was expected to exert some influence over her husband. Before reaching Kent, the missionaries had considered turning back, but Gregory urged them on, and in 597, Augustine landed on the <a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Thanet" title="Isle of Thanet">Isle of Thanet</a> and proceeded to Æthelberht's main town of <a href="/wiki/Canterbury" title="Canterbury">Canterbury</a>.</p>
<p>King Æthelberht converted to Christianity and allowed the missionaries to preach freely, giving them land to found a monastery outside the city walls. Augustine was consecrated as a <a href="/wiki/Bishop" title="Bishop">bishop</a> and converted many of the king's subjects, including thousands during a mass baptism on <a href="/wiki/Christmas" title="Christmas">Christmas Day</a> in 597. Pope Gregory sent more missionaries in 601, along with encouraging letters and gifts for the churches, although attempts to persuade the native <a href="/wiki/Celts" title="Celts">Celtic</a> bishops to submit to Augustine's authority failed. Roman bishops were established at London and Rochester in 604, and a school was founded to train <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Saxons" title="Anglo-Saxons">Anglo-Saxon</a> priests and missionaries. Augustine also arranged the consecration of his successor, <a href="/wiki/Laurence_of_Canterbury" title="Laurence of Canterbury">Laurence of Canterbury</a>. The archbishop probably died in 604 and was soon revered as a <a href="/wiki/Saint" title="Saint">saint</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background_to_the_mission">Background to the mission</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Augustine_of_Canterbury&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background to the mission">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>After the withdrawal of the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">Roman</a> <a href="/wiki/Roman_legion" title="Roman legion">legions</a> from their province of <a href="/wiki/Britannia" title="Britannia">Britannia</a> in 410, the inhabitants were left to defend themselves against the attacks of the <a href="/wiki/Saxons" title="Saxons">Saxons</a>. Before the Roman withdrawal, Britannia had been converted to Christianity and produced the <a href="/wiki/Ascetic" class="mw-redirect" title="Ascetic">ascetic</a> <a href="/wiki/Pelagius" title="Pelagius">Pelagius</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Hindley3_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hindley3-5">[4]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Mayr78_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mayr78-6">[5]</a></sup> Britain sent three bishops to the <a href="/wiki/Synod_of_Arles#Council_of_Arles_in_314" title="Synod of Arles">Council of Arles</a> in 314, and a <a href="/wiki/Gaulish" class="mw-redirect" title="Gaulish">Gaulish</a> bishop went to the island in 396 to help settle disciplinary matters.<sup id="cite_ref-Frend80_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Frend80-7">[6]</a></sup> Material remains testify to a growing presence of Christians, at least until around 360.<sup id="cite_ref-Frend82_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Frend82-8">[7]</a></sup> After the Roman legions departed, pagan tribes settled the southern parts of the island while western Britain, beyond the Anglo-Saxon kingdoms, remained Christian. This native <a href="/wiki/Celtic_Christianity" title="Celtic Christianity">British Church</a> developed in isolation from Rome under the influence of missionaries from Ireland<sup id="cite_ref-Hindley3_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hindley3-5">[4]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Mayr78_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mayr78-6">[5]</a></sup> and was centred on monasteries instead of bishoprics. Other distinguishing characteristics were its calculation of the date of <a href="/wiki/Easter" title="Easter">Easter</a> and the style of the <a href="/wiki/Tonsure" title="Tonsure">tonsure</a> haircut that clerics wore.<sup id="cite_ref-Mayr78_6-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mayr78-6">[5]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Yorke115_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Yorke115-9">[8]</a></sup> Evidence for the survival of Christianity in the eastern part of Britain during this time includes the survival of the cult of <a href="/wiki/Saint_Alban" title="Saint Alban">Saint Alban</a> and the occurrence in place names of <i>eccles</i>, derived from the Latin <i>ecclesia</i>, meaning "church".<sup id="cite_ref-Yorke121_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Yorke121-10">[9]</a></sup> There is no evidence that these native Christians tried to convert the Anglo-Saxons.<sup id="cite_ref-ASE102_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ASE102-11">[10]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Harting32_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Harting32-12">[11]</a></sup> The invasions destroyed most remnants of Roman civilisation in the areas held by the Saxons and related tribes, including the economic and religious structures.<sup id="cite_ref-Kirby23_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kirby23-13">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>It was against this background that Pope Gregory I decided to send a mission, often called the Gregorian mission, to convert the Anglo-Saxons to Christianity in 595.<sup id="cite_ref-ASE104_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ASE104-14">[13]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Jones_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jones-15">[14]</a></sup> The Kingdom of Kent was ruled by Æthelberht, who married a Christian princess named Bertha before 588,<sup id="cite_ref-ASE105_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ASE105-16">[15]</a></sup> and perhaps earlier than 560.<sup id="cite_ref-Kirby24_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kirby24-17">[16]</a></sup> Bertha was the daughter of Charibert I, one of the <a href="/wiki/Merovingian" class="mw-redirect" title="Merovingian">Merovingian</a> kings of the <a href="/wiki/Franks" title="Franks">Franks</a>. As one of the conditions of her marriage, she brought a bishop named <a href="/wiki/Liudhard" title="Liudhard">Liudhard</a> with her to Kent.<sup id="cite_ref-DNBBertha_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DNBBertha-18">[17]</a></sup> Together in Canterbury, they restored a church that dated to Roman times<sup id="cite_ref-Hindley33_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hindley33-19">[18]</a></sup>—possibly the current <a href="/wiki/St_Martin%27s_Church,_Canterbury" title="St Martin's Church, Canterbury">St Martin's Church</a>. Æthelberht was a pagan at this point but allowed his wife freedom of worship. One biographer of Bertha states that under his wife's influence, Æthelberht asked Pope Gregory to send missionaries.<sup id="cite_ref-DNBBertha_18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DNBBertha-18">[17]</a></sup> The historian <a href="/wiki/Ian_N._Wood" title="Ian N. Wood">Ian N. Wood</a> feels that the initiative came from the Kentish court as well as the queen.<sup id="cite_ref-Wood10_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wood10-20">[19]</a></sup> Other historians, however, believe that Gregory initiated the mission, although the exact reasons remain unclear. <a href="/wiki/Bede" title="Bede">Bede</a>, an 8th-century monk who wrote a history of the English church, recorded a famous story in which Gregory saw fair-haired Saxon slaves from Britain in the Roman slave market and was inspired to try to convert their people.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[b]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Harting57_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Harting57-23">[21]</a></sup> More practical matters, such as the acquisition of new provinces acknowledging the primacy of the papacy, and a desire to influence the emerging power of the Kentish kingdom under Æthelberht, were probably involved.<sup id="cite_ref-Hindley33_19-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hindley33-19">[18]</a></sup> The mission may have been an outgrowth of the missionary efforts against the <a href="/wiki/Lombards" title="Lombards">Lombards</a> who, as pagans and <a href="/wiki/Arian" class="mw-redirect" title="Arian">Arian</a> Christians, were not on good relations with the Catholic church in Rome.<sup id="cite_ref-DNB_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DNB-24">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>Aside from Æthelberht's granting of freedom of worship to his wife, the choice of Kent was probably dictated by a number of other factors. Kent was the dominant power in southeastern Britain. Since the eclipse of King <a href="/wiki/Ceawlin_of_Wessex" title="Ceawlin of Wessex">Ceawlin of Wessex</a> in 592, Æthelberht was the leading Anglo-Saxon ruler; Bede refers to Æthelberht as having <a href="/wiki/Imperium" title="Imperium">imperium</a> (overlordship) south of the <a href="/wiki/Humber" title="Humber">River Humber</a>. Trade between the Franks and Æthelberht's kingdom was well established, and the <a href="/wiki/Language_barrier" title="Language barrier">language barrier</a> between the two regions was apparently only a minor obstacle, as the interpreters for the mission came from the Franks. Lastly, Kent's proximity to the Franks allowed support from a Christian area.<sup id="cite_ref-Brooks7_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brooks7-25">[23]</a></sup> There is some evidence, including Gregory's letters to Frankish kings in support of the mission, that some of the Franks felt that they had a claim to overlordship over some of the southern British kingdoms at this time. The presence of a Frankish bishop could also have lent credence to claims of overlordship, if Bertha's Bishop Liudhard was felt to be acting as a representative of the Frankish church and not merely as a spiritual advisor to the queen. Frankish influence was not merely political; archaeological remains attest to a cultural influence as well.<sup id="cite_ref-Kirby27_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kirby27-26">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 595, Gregory chose Augustine, who was the prior of the <a href="/wiki/San_Gregorio_Magno_al_Celio" title="San Gregorio Magno al Celio">Abbey of St Andrew's</a> in Rome, to head the mission to Kent.<sup id="cite_ref-ASE104_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ASE104-14">[13]</a></sup> The pope selected monks to accompany Augustine and sought support from the Frankish royalty and clergy in a series of letters, of which some copies survive in Rome. He wrote to King <a href="/wiki/Theuderic_II" title="Theuderic II">Theuderic II</a> of <a href="/wiki/Burgundy_(region)" class="mw-redirect" title="Burgundy (region)">Burgundy</a> and to King <a href="/wiki/Theudebert_II" title="Theudebert II">Theudebert II</a> of <a href="/wiki/Austrasia" title="Austrasia">Austrasia</a>, as well as their grandmother <a href="/wiki/Brunhilda_of_Austrasia" title="Brunhilda of Austrasia">Brunhild</a>, seeking aid for the mission. Gregory thanked King <a href="/wiki/Chlothar_II" title="Chlothar II">Chlothar II</a> of <a href="/wiki/Neustria" title="Neustria">Neustria</a> for aiding Augustine. Besides hospitality, the Frankish bishops and kings provided interpreters and Frankish priests to accompany the mission.<sup id="cite_ref-Brooks4_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brooks4-27">[25]</a></sup> By soliciting help from the Frankish kings and bishops, Gregory helped to assure a friendly reception for Augustine in Kent, as Æthelbert was unlikely to mistreat a mission which visibly had the support of his wife's relatives and people.<sup id="cite_ref-Brooks6_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brooks6-28">[26]</a></sup> Moreover, the Franks appreciated the chance to participate in mission that would extend their influence in Kent. Chlothar, in particular, needed a friendly realm across the Channel to help guard his kingdom's flanks against his fellow Frankish kings.<sup id="cite_ref-Wood9_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wood9-29">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>Sources make no mention of why Pope Gregory chose a monk to head the mission. Pope Gregory once wrote to Æthelberht complimenting Augustine's knowledge of the <a href="/wiki/Bible" title="Bible">Bible</a>, so Augustine was evidently well educated. Other qualifications included administrative ability, for Gregory was the abbot of St Andrews as well as being pope, which left the day-to-day running of the abbey to Augustine, the prior.<sup id="cite_ref-Fletcher116_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fletcher116-30">[28]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Arrival_and_first_efforts">Arrival and first efforts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Augustine_of_Canterbury&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Arrival and first efforts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">

<p>Augustine was accompanied by Laurence of Canterbury, his eventual successor to the <a href="/wiki/Archbishop" title="Archbishop">archbishopric</a>, and a group of about 40 companions, some of whom were monks.<sup id="cite_ref-ASE105_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ASE105-16">[15]</a></sup> Soon after leaving Rome, the missionaries halted, daunted by the nature of the task before them. They sent Augustine back to Rome to request papal permission to return. Gregory refused and sent Augustine back with letters encouraging the missionaries to persevere.<sup id="cite_ref-Blair116_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Blair116-31">[29]</a></sup> In 597, Augustine and his companions landed in Kent.<sup id="cite_ref-ASE105_16-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ASE105-16">[15]</a></sup> They achieved some initial success soon after their arrival:<sup id="cite_ref-DNB_24-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DNB-24">[22]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Fletcher116_30-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fletcher116-30">[28]</a></sup> Æthelberht permitted the missionaries to settle and preach in his capital of Canterbury where they used the church of St Martin's for services.<sup id="cite_ref-Brooks8_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brooks8-32">[30]</a></sup> Neither Bede nor Gregory mentions the date of Æthelberht's conversion,<sup id="cite_ref-Woods11_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Woods11-33">[31]</a></sup> but it probably took place in 597.<sup id="cite_ref-Brooks8_32-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brooks8-32">[30]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[c]</a></sup> In the <a href="/wiki/Early_Middle_Ages" title="Early Middle Ages">early medieval</a> period, large-scale conversions required the ruler's conversion first, and Augustine is recorded as making large numbers of converts within a year of his arrival in Kent.<sup id="cite_ref-Brooks8_32-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brooks8-32">[30]</a></sup> Also, by 601, Gregory was writing to both Æthelberht and Bertha, calling the king his son and referring to his baptism.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[d]</a></sup> A <a href="/wiki/Late_Middle_Ages" title="Late Middle Ages">late medieval</a> tradition, recorded by the 15th-century chronicler <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Elmham" title="Thomas Elmham">Thomas Elmham</a>, gives the date of the king's conversion as <a href="/wiki/Pentecost" title="Pentecost">Whit Sunday</a>, or 2 June 597; there is no reason to doubt this date, although there is no other evidence for it.<sup id="cite_ref-Brooks8_32-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brooks8-32">[30]</a></sup> Against a date in 597 is a letter of Gregory's to Patriarch <a href="/wiki/Eulogius_of_Alexandria" title="Eulogius of Alexandria">Eulogius of Alexandria</a> in June 598, which mentions the number of converts made by Augustine, but does not mention any baptism of the king. However, it is clear that by 601 the king had been converted.<sup id="cite_ref-Kirby28_34-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kirby28-34">[32]</a></sup> His baptism likely took place at Canterbury.<sup id="cite_ref-Convert56_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Convert56-37">[33]</a></sup></p>
<p>Augustine established his <a href="/wiki/Episcopal_see" title="Episcopal see">episcopal see</a> at Canterbury.<sup id="cite_ref-DNB_24-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DNB-24">[22]</a></sup> It is not clear when and where Augustine was consecrated as a bishop. Bede, writing about a century later, states that Augustine was consecrated by the Frankish Archbishop Ætherius of <a href="/wiki/Arles" title="Arles">Arles</a>, Gaul (France) after the conversion of Æthelberht. Contemporary letters from Pope Gregory, however, refer to Augustine as a bishop before he arrived in England. A letter of Gregory's from September 597 calls Augustine a bishop, and one dated ten months later says Augustine had been consecrated on Gregory's command by bishops of the German lands.<sup id="cite_ref-Brooks5_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brooks5-38">[34]</a></sup> The historian R. A. Markus discusses the various theories of when and where Augustine was consecrated, and suggests he was consecrated before arriving in England, but argues the evidence does not permit deciding exactly where this took place.<sup id="cite_ref-Markus24_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Markus24-39">[35]</a></sup></p>
<p>Soon after his arrival, Augustine founded the <a href="/wiki/Monastery" title="Monastery">monastery</a> of Saints <a href="/wiki/Saint_Peter" title="Saint Peter">Peter</a> and <a href="/wiki/Paul_the_Apostle" title="Paul the Apostle">Paul</a>, which later became <a href="/wiki/St_Augustine%27s_Abbey" title="St Augustine's Abbey">St Augustine's Abbey</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-DNB_24-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DNB-24">[22]</a></sup> on land donated by the king.<sup id="cite_ref-Blair61_40-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Blair61-40">[36]</a></sup> This foundation has often been claimed as the first Benedictine abbey outside Italy, and that by founding it, Augustine introduced the <a href="/wiki/Rule_of_St._Benedict" class="mw-redirect" title="Rule of St. Benedict">Rule of St. Benedict</a> into England, but there is no evidence the abbey followed the Benedictine Rule at the time of its foundation.<sup id="cite_ref-Monasticism55_41-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Monasticism55-41">[37]</a></sup> In a letter Gregory wrote to the <a href="/wiki/Patriarch_of_Alexandria" title="Patriarch of Alexandria">patriarch of Alexandria</a> in 598, he claimed that more than 10,000 Christians had been baptised; the number may be exaggerated but there is no reason to doubt that a mass conversion took place.<sup id="cite_ref-ASE105_16-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ASE105-16">[15]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Fletcher116_30-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fletcher116-30">[28]</a></sup> However, there were probably some Christians already in Kent before Augustine arrived, remnants of the Christians who lived in Britain in the later Roman Empire.<sup id="cite_ref-Harting32_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Harting32-12">[11]</a></sup> Little literary traces remain of them, however.<sup id="cite_ref-Frend79_42-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Frend79-42">[38]</a></sup> One other effect of the king's conversion by Augustine's mission was that the Frankish influence on the southern kingdoms of Britain was decreased.<sup id="cite_ref-Kirby29_43-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kirby29-43">[39]</a></sup></p>
<p>After these conversions, Augustine sent Laurence back to Rome with a report of his success, along with questions about the mission.<sup id="cite_ref-ASE106_44-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ASE106-44">[40]</a></sup> Bede records the letter and Gregory's replies in chapter 27 of his <i><a href="/wiki/Historia_ecclesiastica_gentis_Anglorum" class="mw-redirect" title="Historia ecclesiastica gentis Anglorum">Historia ecclesiastica gentis Anglorum</a></i>; this section of the <i>History</i> is usually known as the <i><a href="/wiki/Libellus_responsionum" title="Libellus responsionum">Libellus responsionum</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Laurentius_45-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Laurentius-45">[41]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Bede71_46-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bede71-46">[42]</a></sup> Augustine asked for Gregory's advice on a number of issues, including how to organise the church, the punishment for church robbers, guidance on who was allowed to marry whom, and the consecration of bishops. Other topics were relations between the churches of Britain and Gaul, childbirth and baptism, and when it was lawful for people to receive communion and for a priest to celebrate mass.<sup id="cite_ref-Bede71_46-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bede71-46">[42]</a></sup></p>
<p>Further missionaries were sent from Rome in 601. They brought a <a href="/wiki/Pallium" title="Pallium">pallium</a> for Augustine and a present of sacred vessels, <a href="/wiki/Vestment" title="Vestment">vestments</a>, <a href="/wiki/Relic" title="Relic">relics</a>, and books.<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">[e]</a></sup> The pallium was the symbol of <a href="/wiki/Metropolitan_bishop" title="Metropolitan bishop">metropolitan</a> status, and signified that Augustine was now an archbishop unambiguously associated with the Holy See. Along with the pallium, a letter from Gregory directed the new archbishop to consecrate 12 <a href="/wiki/Suffragan_bishop" title="Suffragan bishop">suffragan bishops</a> as soon as possible and to send a bishop to <a href="/wiki/York" title="York">York</a>. Gregory's plan was that there would be two metropolitans, one at York and one at London, with 12 suffragan bishops under each archbishop. As part of this plan, Augustine was expected to transfer his archiepiscopal see to London from Canterbury. The move from Canterbury to London never happened; no contemporary sources give the reason,<sup id="cite_ref-Brooks9_52-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brooks9-52">[47]</a></sup> but it was probably because London was not part of Æthelberht's domains. Instead, London was part of the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Essex" title="Kingdom of Essex">kingdom of Essex</a>, ruled by Æthelberht's nephew <a href="/wiki/Saebert_of_Essex" class="mw-redirect" title="Saebert of Essex">Saebert of Essex</a>, who converted to Christianity in 604.<sup id="cite_ref-Hindley33_19-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hindley33-19">[18]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Fletcher453_53-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fletcher453-53">[48]</a></sup> The historian S. Brechter has suggested that the metropolitan see was indeed moved to London, and that it was only with the abandonment of London as a see after the death of Æthelberht that Canterbury became the archiepiscopal see. This theory contradicts Bede's version of events, however.<sup id="cite_ref-Brooks11_54-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brooks11-54">[49]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Additional_work">Additional work</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Augustine_of_Canterbury&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Additional work">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>In 604, Augustine founded two more bishoprics in Britain. Two men who had come to Britain with him in 601 were consecrated, <a href="/wiki/Mellitus" title="Mellitus">Mellitus</a> as <a href="/wiki/Bishop_of_London" title="Bishop of London">Bishop of London</a> and <a href="/wiki/Justus" title="Justus">Justus</a> as <a href="/wiki/Bishop_of_Rochester" title="Bishop of Rochester">Bishop of Rochester</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Hindley33_19-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hindley33-19">[18]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-BEASE267_55-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BEASE267-55">[50]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-BEASE305_56-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BEASE305-56">[51]</a></sup> Bede relates that Augustine, with the help of the king, "recovered" a church built by Roman Christians in Canterbury.<sup id="cite_ref-Brooks50_57-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brooks50-57">[52]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60">[f]</a></sup> It is not clear if Bede meant that Augustine rebuilt the church or that Augustine merely reconsecrated a building that had been used for pagan worship. Archaeological evidence seems to support the latter interpretation; in 1973 the remains of an aisled building dating from the <a href="/wiki/Romano-British" class="mw-redirect" title="Romano-British">Romano-British</a> period were uncovered just south of the present <a href="/wiki/Canterbury_Cathedral" title="Canterbury Cathedral">Canterbury Cathedral</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Brooks50_57-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brooks50-57">[52]</a></sup> The historian Ian Wood argues that the existence of the <i>Libellus</i> points to more contact between Augustine and the native Christians because the topics covered in the work are not restricted to conversion from paganism, but also dealt with relations between differing styles of Christianity.<sup id="cite_ref-Wood170_61-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wood170-61">[55]</a></sup></p>
<p>Augustine failed to extend his authority to the Christians in Wales and <a href="/wiki/Dumnonia" title="Dumnonia">Dumnonia</a> to the west. Gregory had decreed that these Christians should submit to Augustine and that their bishops should obey him,<sup id="cite_ref-Harting70_62-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Harting70-62">[56]</a></sup> apparently believing that more of the Roman governmental and ecclesiastical organisation survived in Britain than was actually the case.<sup id="cite_ref-Conversion118_63-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Conversion118-63">[57]</a></sup> According to the narrative of Bede, the Britons in these regions viewed Augustine with uncertainty, and their suspicion was compounded by a diplomatic misjudgement on Augustine's part.<sup id="cite_ref-ASE110_64-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ASE110-64">[58]</a></sup> In 603, Augustine and Æthelberht summoned the British bishops to a meeting south of the Severn. These guests retired early to confer with their people,<sup id="cite_ref-Hindley8_65-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hindley8-65">[59]</a></sup> who, according to Bede, advised them to judge Augustine based upon the respect he displayed at their next meeting. When Augustine failed to rise from his seat on the entrance of the British bishops,<sup id="cite_ref-Bede100_66-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bede100-66">[60]</a></sup> they refused to recognise him as their archbishop.<sup id="cite_ref-Hindley8_65-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hindley8-65">[59]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Harting72_67-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Harting72-67">[61]</a></sup> There were, however, deep differences between Augustine and the British church that perhaps played a more significant role in preventing an agreement. At issue were the tonsure, the observance of Easter, and practical and deep-rooted differences in approach to asceticism, missionary endeavours, and how the church itself was organised.<sup id="cite_ref-ASE110_64-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ASE110-64">[58]</a></sup> Some historians believe that Augustine had no real understanding of the history and traditions of the British church, damaging his relations with their bishops.<sup id="cite_ref-Harting72_67-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Harting72-67">[61]</a></sup> Also, there were political dimensions involved, as Augustine's efforts were sponsored by the Kentish king, and at this period the Wessex and Mercian kingdoms were expanding to the west, into areas held by the Britons.<sup id="cite_ref-Conversion119_68-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Conversion119-68">[62]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Further_success">Further success</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Augustine_of_Canterbury&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Further success">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Gregory also instructed Augustine on other matters. Temples were to be consecrated for Christian use,<sup id="cite_ref-Thomson8_69-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Thomson8-69">[63]</a></sup> and feasts, if possible, moved to days celebrating Christian martyrs. One religious site was revealed to be a shrine of a local St Sixtus, whose worshippers were unaware of details of the martyr's life or death. They may have been native Christians, but Augustine did not treat them as such. When Gregory was informed, he told Augustine to stop the cult and use the shrine for the Roman <a href="/wiki/Pope_Sixtus_II" title="Pope Sixtus II">St Sixtus</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Blair24_70-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Blair24-70">[64]</a></sup></p>
<p>Gregory legislated on the behaviour of the laity and the clergy. He placed the new mission directly under papal authority and made it clear that English bishops would have no authority over Frankish counterparts nor vice versa. Other directives dealt with the training of native clergy and the missionaries' conduct.<sup id="cite_ref-ASE107_71-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ASE107-71">[65]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/The_King%27s_School,_Canterbury" title="The King's School, Canterbury">The King's School, Canterbury</a> claims Augustine as its founder, which would make it the world's oldest existing school, but the first documentary records of the school date from the 16th century.<sup id="cite_ref-72" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-72">[66]</a></sup> Augustine did establish a school, and soon after his death Canterbury was able to send teachers out to support the <a href="/wiki/East_Anglia" title="East Anglia">East Anglian</a> mission.<sup id="cite_ref-Brooks94_73-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brooks94-73">[67]</a></sup> Augustine received <a href="/wiki/Liturgical_book" title="Liturgical book">liturgical books</a> from the pope, but their exact contents are unknown. They may have been some of the new mass books that were being written at this time. The exact liturgy that Augustine introduced to England remains unknown, but it would have been a form of the <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> language liturgy in use at Rome.<sup id="cite_ref-Harting173_74-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Harting173-74">[68]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Death_and_legacy">Death and legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Augustine_of_Canterbury&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Death and legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:AgCant-tomb.jpg" class="image"><img alt="Pile of stones marked with a tag reading &quot;St. Augustine, Site of Grave, First Archbishop of Canterbury" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/27/AgCant-tomb.jpg/220px-AgCant-tomb.jpg" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/27/AgCant-tomb.jpg/330px-AgCant-tomb.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/27/AgCant-tomb.jpg/440px-AgCant-tomb.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1024" data-file-height="682" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:AgCant-tomb.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Augustine's gravesite at Canterbury</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Before his death, Augustine consecrated <a href="/wiki/Laurence_of_Canterbury" title="Laurence of Canterbury">Laurence of Canterbury</a> as his successor to the archbishopric, probably to ensure an orderly transfer of office.<sup id="cite_ref-Hindley43_75-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hindley43-75">[69]</a></sup> Although at the time of Augustine's death, 26 May 604,<sup id="cite_ref-DNB_24-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DNB-24">[22]</a></sup> the mission barely extended beyond Kent, his undertaking introduced a more active missionary style into the British Isles. Despite the earlier presence of Christians in Ireland and Wales, no efforts had been made to try to convert the Saxon invaders. Augustine was sent to convert the descendants of those invaders, and eventually became the decisive influence in Christianity in the British Isles.<sup id="cite_ref-ASE110_64-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ASE110-64">[58]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Collins185_76-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Collins185-76">[70]</a></sup> Much of his success came about because of Augustine's close relationship with Æthelberht, which gave the archbishop time to establish himself.<sup id="cite_ref-Harting249_77-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Harting249-77">[71]</a></sup> Augustine's example also influenced the great missionary efforts of the Anglo-Saxon Church.<sup id="cite_ref-Harting264_78-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Harting264-78">[72]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Wood8_79-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wood8-79">[73]</a></sup></p>
<p>Augustine's body was originally buried in the portico of what is now St Augustine's, Canterbury,<sup id="cite_ref-Blair61_40-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Blair61-40">[36]</a></sup> but it was later exhumed and placed in a tomb within the abbey church, which became a place of pilgrimage and veneration. After the <a href="/wiki/Norman_conquest_of_England" title="Norman conquest of England">Norman Conquest</a> the cult of St Augustine was actively promoted.<sup id="cite_ref-DNB_24-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DNB-24">[22]</a></sup> After the Conquest, his <a href="/wiki/Shrine" title="Shrine">shrine</a> in St Augustine's Abbey held a central position in one of the axial chapels, flanked by the shrines of his successors Laurence and Mellitus.<sup id="cite_ref-Nilson67_80-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Nilson67-80">[74]</a></sup> King <a href="/wiki/Henry_I_of_England" title="Henry I of England">Henry I of England</a> granted St. Augustine's Abbey a six-day <a href="/wiki/Fair" title="Fair">fair</a> around the date on which Augustine's relics were <a href="/wiki/Translation_(relics)" class="mw-redirect" title="Translation (relics)">translated</a> to his new shrine, from 8 September through 13 September.<sup id="cite_ref-Shrines_81-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Shrines-81">[75]</a></sup></p>
<p>A life of Augustine was written by <a href="/wiki/Goscelin" title="Goscelin">Goscelin</a> around 1090, but this life portrays Augustine in a different light than Bede's account. Goscelin's account has little new historical content, mainly being filled with miracles and imagined speeches.<sup id="cite_ref-Gameson17_82-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gameson17-82">[76]</a></sup> Building on this account, later medieval writers continued to add new miracles and stories to Augustine's life, often quite fanciful.<sup id="cite_ref-Gameson19_83-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gameson19-83">[77]</a></sup> These authors included <a href="/wiki/William_of_Malmesbury" title="William of Malmesbury">William of Malmesbury</a>, who claimed that Augustine founded <a href="/wiki/Cerne_Abbey" title="Cerne Abbey">Cerne Abbey</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-Gameson20_84-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gameson20-84">[78]</a></sup> the author (generally believed to be <a href="/wiki/John_Brompton" title="John Brompton">John Brompton</a>) of a late medieval chronicle containing invented letters from Augustine,<sup id="cite_ref-Gameson24_85-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gameson24-85">[79]</a></sup> and a number of medieval writers who included Augustine in their <a href="/wiki/Romance_(heroic_literature)" class="mw-redirect" title="Romance (heroic literature)">romances</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Gameson22_86-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gameson22-86">[80]</a></sup> Another problem with investigating Augustine's saintly cult is the confusion resulting because most medieval liturgical documents mentioning Augustine do not distinguish between Augustine of Canterbury and <a href="/wiki/Augustine_of_Hippo" title="Augustine of Hippo">Augustine of Hippo</a>, a fourth-century saint. Medieval Scandinavian liturgies feature Augustine of Canterbury quite often, however.<sup id="cite_ref-Blair513_87-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Blair513-87">[81]</a></sup> During the <a href="/wiki/English_Reformation" title="English Reformation">English Reformation</a>, Augustine's shrine was destroyed and his relics were lost.</p>
<p>Augustine's shrine was re-established in March 2012 at the church of <a href="/wiki/Pugin%27s_Church_and_Shrine_of_St_Augustine" class="mw-redirect" title="Pugin's Church and Shrine of St Augustine">St. Augustine</a> in <a href="/wiki/Ramsgate" title="Ramsgate">Ramsgate</a>, Kent, very close to the mission's landing site.<sup id="cite_ref-88" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-88">[82]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-89" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-89">[83]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/St_Augustine%27s_Cross" title="St Augustine's Cross">St Augustine's Cross</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Celtic_cross" title="Celtic cross">Celtic cross</a> erected in 1884, marks the spot in <a href="/wiki/Ebbsfleet,_Thanet" title="Ebbsfleet, Thanet">Ebbsfleet, Thanet</a>, <a href="/wiki/East_Kent" class="mw-redirect" title="East Kent">East Kent</a>, where Augustine is said to have landed.<sup id="cite_ref-Cross_90-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cross-90">[84]</a></sup></p>
