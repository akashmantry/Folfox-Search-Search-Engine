			<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Automotive industry in Germany</h1>

<p>The <b>automotive industry in Germany</b> is one of the largest employers in the country, with a labour force of over 747,000 (2009) working in the <a href="/wiki/Automotive_industry" title="Automotive industry">industry</a>.</p>
<p>Being home to the <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_automobile#19th_century" title="History of the automobile">modern car</a>, the German automobile industry is regarded as the most competitive and innovative in the world,<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> and has the third highest automobile production in the world,<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> and fourth highest <a href="/wiki/List_of_countries_by_motor_vehicle_production" title="List of countries by motor vehicle production">total motor vehicle production</a>. With an annual output close to six million and a 35.6% share of the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a> (2008),</p>
<p>Germany is the absolute leader of auto production in <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> since the 1960s.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>German-designed cars won in the <a href="/wiki/European_Car_of_the_Year" title="European Car of the Year">European Car of the Year</a>, the <a href="/wiki/International_Car_of_the_Year" title="International Car of the Year">International Car of the Year</a>, the <a href="/wiki/World_Car_of_the_Year" title="World Car of the Year">World Car of the Year</a> annual awards the most times among all countries. The <a href="/wiki/Volkswagen_Beetle" title="Volkswagen Beetle">Volkswagen Beetle</a> and <a href="/wiki/Porsche_911" title="Porsche 911">Porsche 911</a> took 4th and 5th places in the <a href="/wiki/Car_of_the_Century" title="Car of the Century">Car of the Century</a> award.</p>

<p>Germany is considered to be the birthplace of the automobile, as motor-car pioneers <a href="/wiki/Karl_Benz" title="Karl Benz">Karl Benz</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nikolaus_Otto" title="Nikolaus Otto">Nikolaus Otto</a> independently developed four-stroke <a href="/wiki/Internal_combustion_engine" title="Internal combustion engine">internal combustion engines</a> in the late 1870s, with Benz fitting his design to a coach in 1887, which led to the modern day motor car. By 1901, Germany was producing about 900 cars a year.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> In 1926, <a href="/wiki/Daimler-Benz" title="Daimler-Benz">Daimler-Benz</a> was formed from the predecessor companies of <a href="/wiki/Karl_Benz" title="Karl Benz">Karl Benz</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gottlieb_Daimler" title="Gottlieb Daimler">Gottlieb Daimler</a> and produced cars under the marque of <a href="/wiki/Mercedes-Benz" title="Mercedes-Benz">Mercedes-Benz</a>. In 1916 <a href="/wiki/BMW" title="BMW">BMW</a> was founded, but didn't start auto production until 1928.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">American</a> <a href="/wiki/Economics" title="Economics">economist</a> <a href="/wiki/Robert_A._Brady_(economist)" title="Robert A. Brady (economist)">Robert A. Brady</a> extensively documented the <a href="/wiki/Rationalization_(economics)" title="Rationalization (economics)">rationalization</a> movement that shaped German industry in the 1920s, and although his general model of the movement applied to the automotive industry, the sector was in poor health in the later years of the <a href="/wiki/Weimar_Republic" title="Weimar Republic">Weimar Republic</a>. Germany's slow development of the industry left the market open for major American auto manufacturers such as <a href="/wiki/General_Motors" title="General Motors">General Motors</a> who took over German company <a href="/wiki/Opel" title="Opel">Opel</a> in 1929, and the <a href="/wiki/Ford_Motor_Company" title="Ford Motor Company">Ford Motor Company</a> which maintained the successful German subsidiary <a href="/wiki/Ford_Germany" title="Ford Germany">Ford-Werke</a>, beginning in 1925.<sup id="cite_ref-reich_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-reich-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>The collapse of the global economy during the <a href="/wiki/Great_Depression" title="Great Depression">Great Depression</a> in the early 1930s plunged Germany's auto industry into a severe crisis. While eighty-six auto companies had existed in Germany during the 1920s, barely twelve survived the depression, including <a href="/wiki/Daimler-Benz" title="Daimler-Benz">Daimler-Benz</a>, <a href="/wiki/Opel" title="Opel">Opel</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ford_Germany" title="Ford Germany">Ford</a>'s factory in <a href="/wiki/Cologne" title="Cologne">Cologne</a>. In addition, four of the country's major car manufacturers — <a href="/wiki/Horch" title="Horch">Horch</a>, <a href="/wiki/DKW" title="DKW">Dampf Kraft Wagen</a> (DKW), <a href="/wiki/Wanderer_(car)" class="mw-redirect" title="Wanderer (car)">Wanderer</a> and <a href="/wiki/Audi" title="Audi">Audi</a> — formed a joint venture known as the <a href="/wiki/Auto_Union" title="Auto Union">Auto Union</a> in 1932, which was to play a leading role in Germany's comeback from the depression.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>The turnabout for the German motor industry came about in the mid 1930s following the election of the <a href="/wiki/Nazi_Party" title="Nazi Party">Nazi Party</a> to power. The Nazis instituted a policy known as <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Motorisierung&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Motorisierung (page does not exist)">Motorisierung</a><span class="noprint" style="font-size:85%; font-style: normal;">&#160;(<a href="https://de.wikipedia.org/wiki/Motorisierung" class="extiw" title="de:Motorisierung">de</a>)</span></i> ("motorization"), a transport policy which <a href="/wiki/Adolf_Hitler" title="Adolf Hitler">Adolf Hitler</a> himself considered a key element of attempts to legitimise the Nazi government by raising the people's <a href="/wiki/Standard_of_living" title="Standard of living">standard of living</a>. In addition to development and extensions of major <a href="/wiki/Highway" title="Highway">highway</a> schemes (which saw the completion of the first <a href="/wiki/Autobahn" title="Autobahn">Autobahn</a> in 1935), the <a href="/wiki/Volkswagen" title="Volkswagen">Volkswagen</a> project was also conceived to design and construct a robust but inexpensive "people's car", the product of which was the <a href="/wiki/Volkswagen_Beetle" title="Volkswagen Beetle">Volkswagen Beetle</a>, launched in 1937. A new city (known as <a href="/wiki/Wolfsburg" title="Wolfsburg">Wolfsburg</a> from 1945) was developed around the factory to house its huge workforce.<sup id="cite_ref-reich_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-reich-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>By the end of <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, most of the auto factories had been destroyed or badly damaged. Germany needed debt relief. The <a href="/wiki/London_Agreement_on_German_External_Debts" title="London Agreement on German External Debts">London Agreement on German External Debts</a> of 1953 provided that repayments were only due while West Germany ran a trade surplus, and that repayments were limited to 3% of export earnings. This gave Germany’s creditors a powerful incentive to import German goods, assisting reconstruction of the Car Industry.<sup id="cite_ref-observer-20150118_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-observer-20150118-6">[6]</a></sup> In addition, the eastern part of Germany was under control of the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a>, which dismantled much of the machinery that was left and sent it back to the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a> as war reparations. Some manufacturers, such as <a href="/wiki/Maybach" title="Maybach">Maybach</a> and <a href="/wiki/Adler_(automobile)" title="Adler (automobile)">Adler (automobile)</a>, started up again, but did not continue making passenger cars. The <a href="/wiki/Volkswagen" title="Volkswagen">Volkswagen</a> production facility in <a href="/wiki/Wolfsburg" title="Wolfsburg">Wolfsburg</a> continued making the <a href="/wiki/Volkswagen_Beetle" title="Volkswagen Beetle">Volkswagen Beetle</a> (Type 1) in 1945, a car which it had intended to make prior to the war (under the name of KdF-Wagen), except that the factory was converted to military truck production during the war. By 1955 VW had made one million <a href="/wiki/Volkswagen_Beetle" title="Volkswagen Beetle">Volkswagen Beetles</a>, and by 1965 had built 10 million, as it gained popularity on export markets as well as on the home market. Other auto manufacturers rebuilt their plants and slowly resumed production, with initial models mostly based on pre-war designs. <a href="/wiki/Mercedes-Benz" title="Mercedes-Benz">Mercedes-Benz</a> resumed production in 1946 with the pre-war–designed <a href="/wiki/Mercedes-Benz_W136" title="Mercedes-Benz W136">170 series</a>. In 1951 they introduced the <a href="/wiki/Mercedes-Benz_W187" title="Mercedes-Benz W187">220</a> <a href="/wiki/Mercedes-Benz_Ponton" title="Mercedes-Benz Ponton">series</a>, which came with a more modern engine, and the <a href="/wiki/Mercedes-Benz_300" title="Mercedes-Benz 300">300 series</a>. <a href="/wiki/Opel" title="Opel">Opel</a> revived the pre-war cars <a href="/wiki/Opel_Olympia" title="Opel Olympia">Opel Olympia</a> in 1947 and the <a href="/wiki/Opel_Kapit%C3%A4n" title="Opel Kapitän">Opel Kapitän</a> in 1948. (Toolings for the <a href="/wiki/Opel_Kadett" title="Opel Kadett">Opel Kadett</a> were taken by the Soviets and used to make the <a href="/wiki/Moskvitch_400-420" title="Moskvitch 400-420">Moskvitch 400-420</a>., which had resumed production of trucks in 1945, began building the pre-war <a href="/wiki/Ford_Taunus" title="Ford Taunus">Ford Taunus</a> in 1948. <a href="/wiki/Porsche" title="Porsche">Porsche</a> began production of their <a href="/wiki/Porsche_356" title="Porsche 356">Porsche 356</a> sports car in 1948, and replaced it with their long-lived <a href="/wiki/Porsche_911" title="Porsche 911">Porsche 911</a> in 1964 (which remains in production more than 50 years and several incarnations later).</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Borgward" title="Borgward">Borgward</a> began production in 1949, and <a href="/wiki/Goliath_(car)" class="mw-redirect" title="Goliath (car)">Goliath</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lloyd_(car)" class="mw-redirect" title="Lloyd (car)">Lloyd</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gutbrod" title="Gutbrod">Gutbrod</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Auto_Union" title="Auto Union">Auto Union</a> (<a href="/wiki/DKW" title="DKW">DKW</a>) began in 1950. <a href="/wiki/BMW" title="BMW">BMW</a>'s first cars after the war were the luxurious <a href="/wiki/BMW_501" title="BMW 501">BMW 501</a> and <a href="/wiki/BMW_502" class="mw-redirect" title="BMW 502">BMW 502</a> in 1952. In 1957 <a href="/wiki/NSU_Motorenwerke" title="NSU Motorenwerke">NSU Motorenwerke</a> re-entered the car market.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Automobile manufacturers in <a href="/wiki/East_Germany" title="East Germany">East Germany</a> after the war included <a href="/wiki/Eisenacher_Motorenwerk" title="Eisenacher Motorenwerk">Eisenacher Motorenwerk</a> (EMW), which also made the <a href="/wiki/Wartburg_(car)" class="mw-redirect" title="Wartburg (car)">Wartburg (car)</a>, and <a href="/wiki/VEB_Sachsenring_Automobilwerke_Zwickau" class="mw-redirect" title="VEB Sachsenring Automobilwerke Zwickau">VEB Sachsenring Automobilwerke Zwickau</a>, which made the <a href="/wiki/IFA_F8" class="mw-redirect" title="IFA F8">IFA F8</a> (derived from the <a href="/wiki/DKW_F8" title="DKW F8">DKW F8</a>) and the <a href="/wiki/Trabant" title="Trabant">Trabant</a>. East Germany's status as a communist country was reflecting in the relatively primitive design and refinement of these cars, although they both continued in production until the early 1990s, shortly after the <a href="/wiki/Revolutions_of_1989" title="Revolutions of 1989">fall of the communist rule</a> and the <a href="/wiki/German_reunification" title="German reunification">German reunification</a>.</p>
<p>Initial production by EMW after the war were models that were essentially pre-war <a href="/wiki/BMW_326" title="BMW 326">BMW 326</a> and <a href="/wiki/BMW_327" title="BMW 327">BMW 327</a> models, as the plant in <a href="/wiki/Eisenach" title="Eisenach">Eisenach</a> was formerly owned by BMW.</p>
<p>During the mid-to-late 1950s the <a href="/wiki/Bubble_car" title="Bubble car">Bubble car</a> became popular. BMW was the largest maker, with the <a href="/wiki/BMW_Isetta" class="mw-redirect" title="BMW Isetta">BMW Isetta</a> and <a href="/wiki/BMW_600" title="BMW 600">BMW 600</a>. Other makes included the <a href="/wiki/Messerschmitt_KR175" title="Messerschmitt KR175">Messerschmitt KR175</a> and <a href="/wiki/Messerschmitt_KR200" title="Messerschmitt KR200">KR200</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Heinkel_Kabine" title="Heinkel Kabine">Heinkel Kabine</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Z%C3%BCndapp_Janus" title="Zündapp Janus">Zündapp Janus</a>. <a href="/wiki/Microcars" class="mw-redirect" title="Microcars">Microcars</a> such as the <a href="/wiki/Glas" title="Glas">Glas</a> <a href="/wiki/Goggomobile" class="mw-redirect" title="Goggomobile">Goggomobile</a>, <a href="/wiki/BMW_700" title="BMW 700">BMW 700</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Lloyd_600" title="Lloyd 600">Lloyd 600</a> also were popular. However, the "Bubble car" concept had been abandoned by 1970.</p>
<p>In the late-1950s, BMW developed financial difficulties and control of the company was acquired by the <a href="/wiki/Quandt_family" class="mw-redirect" title="Quandt family">Quandt family</a>. BMW acquired <a href="/wiki/Glas" title="Glas">Glas</a> in 1966. In 1961, the <a href="/wiki/Borgward" title="Borgward">Borgward</a> auto group, including Goliath and Lloyd went out of business. In 1958 Auto Union was acquired by <a href="/wiki/Daimler_AG" title="Daimler AG">Daimler AG</a>, but then in turn it was sold in stages from 1964 to 1966 to <a href="/wiki/Volkswagen_AG" class="mw-redirect" title="Volkswagen AG">Volkswagen AG</a> (at which time the DKW marque was ended and the <a href="/wiki/Audi" title="Audi">Audi</a> name was resurrected). In 1969, <a href="/wiki/Volkswagen_AG" class="mw-redirect" title="Volkswagen AG">Volkswagen AG</a> acquired <a href="/wiki/NSU_Motorenwerke" title="NSU Motorenwerke">NSU Motorenwerke</a> (developer of the <a href="/wiki/Wankel_engine" title="Wankel engine">Wankel engine</a>) and merged it with Auto Union, but the NSU nameplate disappeared by 1977 when production of the <a href="/wiki/Ro80" class="mw-redirect" title="Ro80">Ro80</a> rotary-engine saloon (<a href="/wiki/European_Car_of_the_Year" title="European Car of the Year">European Car of the Year</a> on its launch 10 years earlier) was stopped largely due to disappointing sales and a poor reputation for reliability.</p>
<p>Ford merged its <a href="/wiki/Ford_Germany" title="Ford Germany">German</a> and <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ford_Great_Britain&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Ford Great Britain (page does not exist)">British</a> operations in 1967, with the intention of producing identical cars at its German and British factories. Ford had also opened a factory at <a href="/wiki/Genk" title="Genk">Genk</a>, <a href="/wiki/Belgium" title="Belgium">Belgium</a>, in 1963. In 1976, it also opened a factory in <a href="/wiki/Valencia" title="Valencia">Valencia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a>, where production of the new <a href="/wiki/Ford_Fiesta" title="Ford Fiesta">Fiesta</a> supermini (the first Ford of this size to be built in any country) was concentrated. The <a href="/wiki/Ford_Escort" class="mw-disambig" title="Ford Escort">Escort</a>, launched in 1967, was the first new Ford to be produced at both the German and British factories. At the beginning of 1969, Ford launched a new sporting coupe, the <a href="/wiki/Ford_Capri" title="Ford Capri">Capri</a>, which like the Escort was produced throughout Europe. The <a href="/wiki/Ford_Taunus" title="Ford Taunus">Taunus</a> of 1970 made use of the same basic design as the British <a href="/wiki/Ford_Cortina" title="Ford Cortina">Cortina MK3</a>, but had slightly different exterior styling, although those styling differences were ironed out with the launch of the 1976 Taunus. Ford's new flagship model, the <a href="/wiki/Ford_Granada" class="mw-disambig" title="Ford Granada">Granada</a>, was built in Britain, Germany and Spain from the beginning of 1972, although British production was withdrawn after a few years.</p>
<p>Volkswagen was faced by major financial difficulties in the early 1970s; with its ageing Beetle still selling strongly all over the world but its newer models had been less successful. However, the company then enjoyed a revival with the arrival of the popular <a href="/wiki/Volkswagen_Passat" title="Volkswagen Passat">Passat</a> in 1973, <a href="/wiki/Volkswagen_Golf" title="Volkswagen Golf">Golf</a> in 1974 and <a href="/wiki/Volkswagen_Polo" title="Volkswagen Polo">Polo</a> in 1975 - all of these cars featured the new front-wheel drive hatchback layout which was enjoying a rise in popularity across <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> after first being patented by <a href="/wiki/Renault" title="Renault">Renault</a> of <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a> with the <a href="/wiki/Renault_16" title="Renault 16">R16</a> in 1965. The Polo was Volkswagen's new entry-level model, and was aimed directly at modern small hatchbacks like the <a href="/wiki/Fiat_127" title="Fiat 127">Fiat 127</a> and <a href="/wiki/Renault_5" title="Renault 5">Renault 5</a>. The mid-range Golf was seen as the car to eventually replace the Beetle, and was easily the first popular hatchback of this size in Europe, leading to most leading carmakers having a similar-sized hatchback by the early 1980s. Production of the Beetle finished in Germany in 1978, although it continued to be produced in <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">Mexico</a> and <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">Brazil</a> until 2003, with a small number of models being imported to Germany and the rest of <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> during its final 25 years. The Passat was marketed as a more advanced alternative to traditional larger saloon cars like the <a href="/wiki/Ford_Taunus" title="Ford Taunus">Ford Taunus</a>/<a href="/wiki/Ford_Cortina" title="Ford Cortina">Cortina</a>, <a href="/wiki/Opel_Ascona" title="Opel Ascona">Opel Ascona</a> (sold in Britain from 1975 as the <a href="/wiki/Vauxhall_Cavalier" title="Vauxhall Cavalier">Vauxhall Cavalier</a>) and the <a href="/wiki/Renault_12" title="Renault 12">Renault 12</a>.</p>
<p>The Scirocco coupe of 1974 was also a success in the smaller sports car market, competing against the likes of the <a href="/wiki/Ford_Capri" title="Ford Capri">Ford Capri</a> and <a href="/wiki/Opel_Manta" title="Opel Manta">Opel Manta</a>. Its partner company Audi also enjoyed an upturn thanks to the success of its 100 range (launched in 1968) and the smaller 80 (launched in 1972 and voted European Car of the Year). Both of the new Audi models featured front-wheel drive. The Volkswagen Polo was in fact a rebadged version of the <a href="/wiki/Audi_50" title="Audi 50">Audi 50</a>, but the Audi original was a slower seller than the Volkswagen that it spawned and was only available in certain markets.</p>
<p>Volkswagen and Audi both enjoyed a growing rise in popularity in overseas markets during the 1970s and this continued throughout the 1980s. Audi launched a well-received large saloon model, the <a href="/wiki/Audi_100" title="Audi 100">Audi 100</a>, in 1968, and followed this four years later with the smaller <a href="/wiki/Audi_80" title="Audi 80">Audi 80</a>, winner of the <a href="/wiki/European_Car_of_the_Year" title="European Car of the Year">European Car of the Year</a> award for 1973. In 1980, Audi moved into the sports car market with its front-wheel drive <a href="/wiki/Audi_Coupe" class="mw-redirect" title="Audi Coupe">Coupe</a> and the four-wheel drive, high-performance version, the <a href="/wiki/Audi_Quattro" title="Audi Quattro">Quattro</a>. The Quattro four-wheel drive system was later adopted on Audi's saloon models.</p>
<p>During the 1970s and early 1980s, <a href="/wiki/General_Motors" title="General Motors">General Motors</a> integrated Opel with the British <a href="/wiki/Vauxhall" title="Vauxhall">Vauxhall</a> brand so that designs were shared with the only difference being the names. Faced with fierce competition from up-to-date designs from Volkswagen, General Motors moved to a front-wheel drive hatchback in 1979 with the latest version of the Opel Kadett, followed in 1981 by new Ascona (which retained the Vauxhall Cavalier name for the British market). In 1982 it opened a new plant <a href="/wiki/Zaragoza" title="Zaragoza">Zaragoza</a>, <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a>, to produce the new <a href="/wiki/Opel_Corsa" title="Opel Corsa">Opel Corsa</a> supermini; this car was later imported to Britain as the <a href="/wiki/Vauxhall_Nova" class="mw-redirect" title="Vauxhall Nova">Vauxhall Nova</a>. Production of the Kadett/Astra and Ascona/Cavalier models was divided between factories in Germany, Belgium, Spain and Britain. The <a href="/wiki/Vauxhall_Carlton" title="Vauxhall Carlton">Vauxhall Carlton</a> was briefly built in Britain from its 1978 launch, but within a few years production was fully concentrated in Germany, where it was built alongside the identical <a href="/wiki/Opel_Rekord" title="Opel Rekord">Opel Rekord</a>.</p>
<p>The final version of the Opel Kadett was voted <a href="/wiki/European_Car_of_the_Year" title="European Car of the Year">European Car of the Year</a> on its launch in 1984, as was the Opel Rekord's successor - the <a href="/wiki/Opel_Omega" title="Opel Omega">Omega</a> - two years afterwards. The Ascona's successor, the <a href="/wiki/Opel_Vectra" title="Opel Vectra">Vectra</a> (still the Vauxhall Cavalier in Britain]], was launched in 1988, but missed out of the European Car of the Year accolade to the <a href="/wiki/Fiat_Tipo" title="Fiat Tipo">Fiat Tipo</a>.</p>
<p>BMW and Mercedes-Benz remained committed to rear-wheel drive on its saloons and booted coupes during these years. BMW, however, developed its model ranges more comprehensively in the 1980s and early 1990s. The original <a href="/wiki/BMW_3_Series" title="BMW 3 Series">BMW 3 Series</a>, launched in 1975, was sold as a two-door saloon or cabriolet. The second generation model launched in 1982, however, was eventually available also as a four-door saloon and five-door estate, and during the 1990s the third generation model range eventually included a three-door hatchback as well. The <a href="/wiki/BMW_5_Series" title="BMW 5 Series">BMW 5 Series</a>, the mid-range model launched in 1972, was only sold as a four-door saloon for its first two generations, but a third generation model was available as an estate from 1991.</p>
<p>With the radical changes in car design that took place throughout the 1970s and into the 1980s, Ford responded by substantially altering its model line-up. After launching the Fiesta supermini in 1976, it switched to front-wheel drive and a hatchback on the MK3 <a href="/wiki/Ford_Escort" class="mw-disambig" title="Ford Escort">Escort</a> on its launch in 1980, and opted to replace the Taunus/Cortina with the <a href="/wiki/Ford_Sierra" title="Ford Sierra">Sierra</a> in 1982 - abandoning the hugely popular saloon format for an aerodynamic hatchback, although a saloon version was added in 1987. In 1983, Ford had also responded to the continuing demand for family saloons by launching the <a href="/wiki/Ford_Orion" title="Ford Orion">Orion</a>, the saloon version of the Escort. The <a href="/wiki/Ford_Scorpio" title="Ford Scorpio">Scorpio</a> replaced the <a href="/wiki/Ford_Granada" class="mw-disambig" title="Ford Granada">Granada</a> as Ford's European flagship in 1985, and was solely produced at the <a href="/wiki/Cologne" title="Cologne">Cologne</a> plant in Germany. The Scorpio was originally available only as a hatchback, and despite its popularity, Ford eventually expanded the Scorpio range by launching a saloon model in 1990 and an estate model in 1992. The declining demand for sporting coupes led to Ford's decision not to directly replace the Capri, which was discontinued after 1986.</p>
<p>After its rejuvenation during the 1970s, VW modernised its model ranges during the first half of the 1980s and continued to enjoy strong sales in Germany and most other European markets. The Polo, Passat and Scirocco all entered their second generation during 1981, and the MK2 Golf was launched in 1983. A saloon version of the MK1 Golf, the <a href="/wiki/Volkswagen_Jetta" title="Volkswagen Jetta">Jetta</a>, had been available since 1979, and the MK2 Jetta was launched in 1984. 1988 saw the launch of the MK3 Passat and a new coupe, the <a href="/wiki/Volkswagen_Corrado" title="Volkswagen Corrado">Corrado</a>, which was produced alongside the Scirocco until the older car's demise in 1992.</p>
<p>The VW Polo was updated in 1990, an all-new model finally arriving in 1994, and the MK3 Golf was voted <a href="/wiki/European_Car_of_the_Year" title="European Car of the Year">European Car of the Year</a> shortly after its launch in 1991. The saloon version of the MK3 Golf, the <a href="/wiki/Volkswagen_Vento" title="Volkswagen Vento">Vento</a>, was launched in 1992. The Passat was updated in 1993 before an all-new model was launched in 1996. The Corrado was discontinued in 1996 without an immediate replacement. VW moved into the MPV market with the Sharan in 1995, built in <a href="/wiki/Portugal" title="Portugal">Portugal</a> as part of a venture with Ford, which produced the identical Galaxy. A new Beetle, with front-wheel drive and a front-mounted engine, was launched in 1998, but like the later versions of the original model it was produced in <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">Mexico</a> rather than Germany. The MK4 Golf was launched in late 1997, and joined a year later by a saloon version, the <a href="/wiki/Volkswagen_Bora" class="mw-redirect" title="Volkswagen Bora">Bora</a>.</p>
<p>The West of Germany was far more technically advanced in comparison with the East (more than 4.5 millions against 200 thousands annual production of auto vehicles in the 1980s), with the divide ending with <a href="/wiki/German_reunification" title="German reunification">German reunification</a> in 1990.</p>
<p>During the 1980s and 1990s, the German auto industry engaged in major acquisitions and international expansion all over the World. Besides of direct export, German manufacturers found or bought plants in European, Asian, Latin American countries and in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> even. Auto industry of <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">Mexico</a>, <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">Brazil</a>, <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>, <a href="/wiki/Turkey" title="Turkey">Turkey</a>, some post-socialist East European countries gained by German investments in a significant share.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Volkswagen" title="Volkswagen">Volkswagen</a> set up a joint venture with <a href="/wiki/Shanghai_Automotive_Industry_Corporation" class="mw-redirect" title="Shanghai Automotive Industry Corporation">Shanghai Automotive Industry Corporation</a> in 1984 (named <a href="/wiki/Shanghai_Volkswagen_Automotive" class="mw-redirect" title="Shanghai Volkswagen Automotive">Shanghai Volkswagen Automotive</a>), and in 1990 established <a href="/wiki/FAW-Volkswagen" title="FAW-Volkswagen">FAW-Volkswagen</a> to produce VWs and Audis in China. VW also acquired <a href="/wiki/SEAT" title="SEAT">SEAT</a> of <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a> in 1986 and <a href="/wiki/%C5%A0koda_Auto" title="Škoda Auto">Škoda</a> of <a href="/wiki/Czechoslovakia" title="Czechoslovakia">Czechoslovakia</a> in 1991, improving the model ranges of these manufacturers and helping increase their market share significantly across Europe. Volkswagen had even shifted Polo production to a SEAT factory in Spain after its acquisition of SEAT, and the 1993 <a href="/wiki/SEAT_Ibiza" title="SEAT Ibiza">SEAT Ibiza</a> formed the basis for the following year's new Polo.</p>
<p>VW also made use of its components across the different marques; for instance, by the year 2000, the floorpan of the Volkswagen Golf for instance had spawned the <a href="/wiki/Audi_A3" title="Audi A3">Audi A3</a>, <a href="/wiki/Audi_TT" title="Audi TT">Audi TT</a>, <a href="/wiki/SEAT_Toledo" title="SEAT Toledo">SEAT Toledo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Seat_Leon" class="mw-redirect" title="Seat Leon">Seat Leon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Skoda_Octavia" class="mw-redirect" title="Skoda Octavia">Skoda Octavia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Volkswagen_Bora" class="mw-redirect" title="Volkswagen Bora">Volkswagen Bora</a>.</p>
<p>By the end of the 1990s, VW moved into the luxury and supercar end of the market and acquired <a href="/wiki/Bentley" title="Bentley">Bentley</a> of Britain and the <a href="/wiki/Bugatti" title="Bugatti">Bugatti</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lamborghini" title="Lamborghini">Lamborghini</a> marques from <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a>.</p>
<p>Ford had concentrated <a href="/wiki/Ford_Sierra" title="Ford Sierra">Sierra</a> production in Belgium rather than Germany and Britain from the end of the 1980s, and its successor - the <a href="/wiki/Ford_Mondeo" title="Ford Mondeo">Mondeo</a> - was solely produced in Belgium when it went into production around the end of 1992. The Escort remained in production throughout Europe until 2000, although its successor, the <a href="/wiki/Ford_Focus" title="Ford Focus">Focus</a>, launched in 1998, was only produced in Germany for European buyers. The <a href="/wiki/Ford_Scorpio" title="Ford Scorpio">Scorpio</a> was discontinued in 1998 and not directly replaced, with Ford instead directing potential Scorpio buyers to high-specification versions of the smaller Mondeo. The Scorpio's demise occurred around the same time that Ford took over <a href="/wiki/Volvo" title="Volvo">Volvo</a>, which already had a strong presence in the executive car market, while Ford had taken over British luxury carmaker <a href="/wiki/Jaguar" title="Jaguar">Jaguar</a> in 1989 and was about to launch the <a href="/wiki/Jaguar_S-Type" title="Jaguar S-Type">Jaguar S-Type</a>.</p>
<p>At the beginning of 1990s, Ford and Volkswagen agreed a venture to produce an MPV together at the same factory with the same basic design. The result of this venture was the <a href="/wiki/Ford_Galaxy" title="Ford Galaxy">Ford Galaxy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Volkswagen_Sharan" title="Volkswagen Sharan">Volkswagen Sharan</a>, but these vehicles were produced in <a href="/wiki/Portugal" title="Portugal">Portugal</a> rather than Germany from their launch in 1995. They were joined a year later by the <a href="/wiki/SEAT_Alhambra" title="SEAT Alhambra">SEAT Alhambra</a>.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/BMW" title="BMW">BMW</a> acquired the British <a href="/wiki/Rover_Group" title="Rover Group">Rover Group</a> in 1994, but large losses led to its sale in 2000. However, BMW retained the <a href="/wiki/Mini_(marque)" title="Mini (marque)">Mini (marque)</a> name for a line of new cars, all built in Britain from 2001. During the 1990s, BMW opened a production facility for <a href="/wiki/SUV" class="mw-redirect" title="SUV">SUVs</a> in <a href="/wiki/Spartanburg_County,_South_Carolina" title="Spartanburg County, South Carolina">Spartanburg County, South Carolina</a>. BMW also acquired the <a href="/wiki/Rolls-Royce_Motor_Cars" title="Rolls-Royce Motor Cars">Rolls-Royce Motor Cars</a> name, effective as of 2003, and in the same year established a joint venture in China named <a href="/wiki/BMW_Brilliance" title="BMW Brilliance">BMW Brilliance</a>. Daimler-Benz entered into what was initially called a "merger of equals" with <a href="/wiki/Chrysler_Corporation" class="mw-redirect" title="Chrysler Corporation">Chrysler Corporation</a> in 1998. However, cultural differences and operating losses led to its dissolution in 2007, although Daimler-Benz kept Chrysler's Chinese joint venture, renamed <a href="/wiki/Beijing_Benz" title="Beijing Benz">Beijing Benz</a>. The company also launched the <a href="/wiki/Smart_(marque)" title="Smart (marque)">Smart</a> in 1998 and relaunched the <a href="/wiki/Maybach" title="Maybach">Maybach</a> brand in 2002. In addition, during the 1990s they opened a production facility for SUVs in <a href="/wiki/Tuscaloosa_County,_Alabama" title="Tuscaloosa County, Alabama">Tuscaloosa County, Alabama</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 5 July 2012, Volkswagen AG announced a deal with Porsche resulting in VW's full ownership of Porsche on 1 August 2012. The deal was classified as a restructuring rather than a takeover due to the transfer of a single share as part of the deal. Volkswagen AG paid Porsche shareholders $5.61 billion for the remaining 50.1% it did not own.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Present_day">Present day</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Automotive_industry_in_Germany&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Present day">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Currently, five German companies and seven marques dominate the automotive industry in the country: <a href="/wiki/Volkswagen_AG" class="mw-redirect" title="Volkswagen AG">Volkswagen AG</a> (and subsidiaries <a href="/wiki/Audi" title="Audi">Audi</a> and <a href="/wiki/Porsche" title="Porsche">Porsche</a>), <a href="/wiki/BMW_AG" class="mw-redirect" title="BMW AG">BMW AG</a>, <a href="/wiki/Daimler_AG" title="Daimler AG">Daimler AG</a>, <a href="/wiki/Adam_Opel_AG" class="mw-redirect" title="Adam Opel AG">Adam Opel AG</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ford_Germany" title="Ford Germany">Ford-Werke GmbH</a>. Nearly six million vehicles are produced in Germany each year, and approximately 5.5 million are produced overseas by German brands.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Alongside the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a> and <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a>, Germany is one of the top 4 automobile manufacturers in the world.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> The Volkswagen Group is one of the three biggest automotive companies of the world (along with <a href="/wiki/Toyota" title="Toyota">Toyota</a> and <a href="/wiki/General_Motors" title="General Motors">General Motors</a>).</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Chevrolet_Volt" title="Chevrolet Volt">Chevrolet Volt</a> and its <a href="/wiki/GM_Voltec_powertrain" title="GM Voltec powertrain">GM Voltec powertrain</a> Technology were invented and developed first and foremost by the former German <a href="/wiki/Opel" title="Opel">Opel</a> engineer <a href="/wiki/Frank_Weber" title="Frank Weber">Frank Weber</a> and—still today—some of the most important parts of the development of GM's electric vehicles is done in Germany.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
