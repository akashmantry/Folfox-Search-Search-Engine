<p><b>Mustafa Bülent Ecevit</b> (<small>Turkish:&#160;</small><span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_Turkic_dialects" title="Help:IPA for Turkic dialects">[byˈlænt edʒeˈvit]</a></span>; 28 May 1925 – 5 November 2006) was a Turkish politician, poet, writer, scholar, and journalist, who served as the <a href="/wiki/Prime_Minister_of_Turkey" title="Prime Minister of Turkey">Prime Minister of Turkey</a> four times between 1974 and 2002. He served as prime minister of Turkey in 1974, 1977, 1978–79, and 1999–2002. He was the leader of the <a href="/wiki/Republican_People%27s_Party_(Turkey)" title="Republican People's Party (Turkey)">Republican People's Party (CHP)</a> between 1972 and 1980, and in 1989 he became the leader of the <a href="/wiki/Democratic_Left_Party_(Turkey)" title="Democratic Left Party (Turkey)">Democratic Left Party (DSP)</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=B%C3%BClent_Ecevit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>He was born in <a href="/wiki/Istanbul" title="Istanbul">Istanbul</a> to a middle-class family. Ecevit's father Fahri Ecevit was a professor in <a href="/wiki/Ankara_University" title="Ankara University">Ankara University</a>. His mother, Fatma Nazlı, was among the first women in Turkey to paint professionally.<sup id="cite_ref-NYT1_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NYT1-1">[1]</a></sup> Ecevit said his mother was of <a href="/wiki/Bosniak" class="mw-redirect" title="Bosniak">Bosniak</a> ancestry and his paternal grandfather was of <a href="/wiki/Kurdish_people" class="mw-redirect" title="Kurdish people">Kurdish</a> ancestry.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1944, Ecevit graduated from <a href="/wiki/Robert_College" title="Robert College">Robert College</a> in <a href="/wiki/Istanbul" title="Istanbul">Istanbul</a> and started working as a translator at the General Directorate for Press and Publication (Basın Yayın Genel Müdürlüğü). In 1946, shortly after marrying his classmate <a href="/wiki/Rah%C5%9Fan_Ecevit" title="Rahşan Ecevit">Rahşan Aral</a> in 1946, he moved to London to work for Turkey's press attaché. During his stay in London, he studied Bengali, Sanskrit and Art History at the <a href="/wiki/School_of_Oriental_and_African_Studies" class="mw-redirect" title="School of Oriental and African Studies">School of Oriental and African Studies</a>, but did not graduate.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> He later went to the United States in the mid–1950s on a State Department fellowship, and worked at two newspapers in <a href="/wiki/North_Carolina" title="North Carolina">North Carolina</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_political_life">Early political life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=B%C3%BClent_Ecevit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Early political life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Ecevit was elected into the <a href="/wiki/Turkish_Grand_National_Assembly" class="mw-redirect" title="Turkish Grand National Assembly">Turkish parliament</a> for the first time in 1957. He was a Member of the Parliament between 1960 and 1961 during the <a href="/wiki/26th_government_of_Turkey" title="26th government of Turkey">26th</a>, <a href="/wiki/27th_government_of_Turkey" title="27th government of Turkey">27th</a> and <a href="/wiki/28th_government_of_Turkey" title="28th government of Turkey">28th</a> governments. Ecevit served as the <a href="/wiki/Ministry_of_Labour_and_Social_Security_(Turkey)" title="Ministry of Labour and Social Security (Turkey)">Minister of Labour</a> between 1961 and 1965, contributing to the acceptance of the right to strike and collective agreement. In 1966 he became the secretary general of the <a href="/wiki/Republican_People%27s_Party_(Turkey)" title="Republican People's Party (Turkey)">Republican People's Party</a> (<a href="/wiki/Turkish_language" title="Turkish language">Turkish</a>: <span lang="tr" xml:lang="tr"><i>Cumhuriyet Halk Partisi</i></span>, CHP). In 1971 he resigned from the post as a protest to the party decision to support the transitional government established by a military intervention.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Leader_of_CHP">Leader of CHP</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=B%C3%BClent_Ecevit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Leader of CHP">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Carter_ecevit.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/85/Carter_ecevit.jpg/220px-Carter_ecevit.jpg" width="220" height="314" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/85/Carter_ecevit.jpg/330px-Carter_ecevit.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/8/85/Carter_ecevit.jpg 2x" data-file-width="350" data-file-height="500" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Carter_ecevit.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
President <a href="/wiki/Jimmy_Carter" title="Jimmy Carter">Jimmy Carter</a> with Turkish Prime Minister Bulent Ecevit at the <a href="/wiki/White_House" title="White House">White House</a>, 31 May 1978.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In 1972, he succeeded <a href="/wiki/%C4%B0smet_%C4%B0n%C3%B6n%C3%BC" title="İsmet İnönü">İsmet İnönü</a> as the leader of the party and became <a href="/wiki/List_of_Prime_Ministers_of_Turkey" title="List of Prime Ministers of Turkey">Prime Minister</a> in a coalition with the National Salvation Party of <a href="/wiki/Necmettin_Erbakan" title="Necmettin Erbakan">Necmettin Erbakan</a> (the <a href="/wiki/37th_government_of_Turkey" title="37th government of Turkey">37th government of Turkey</a>). This government is known for ordering the <a href="/wiki/Turkish_invasion_of_Cyprus" title="Turkish invasion of Cyprus">Turkish invasion of Cyprus</a> on 20 July 1974. So he was nicknamed "Conqueror of Cyprus" (<i><a href="/wiki/Turkish_language" title="Turkish language">Turkish</a>: "Kıbrıs Fatihi"</i>) by Turkish people after that successful operation.</p>
<p>Upon assuming the leadership of the CHP, Ecevit played a major role in redefining the party's political position in the <a href="/wiki/Centre-left" class="mw-redirect" title="Centre-left">centre-left</a> (Turkish: <i>Ortanın solu</i>), which proved controversial. In the <a href="/wiki/Turkish_general_election,_1973" title="Turkish general election, 1973">1973 general election</a>, the CHP won a plurality of the votes and seats in parliament. Despite the party's <a href="/wiki/Secularism" title="Secularism">secular</a> credentials, Ecevit formed a coalition with the Islamist <a href="/wiki/National_Salvation_Party" title="National Salvation Party">National Salvation Party</a> (MSP) headed by <a href="/wiki/Necmettin_Erbakan" title="Necmettin Erbakan">Necmettin Erbakan</a>. Despite lasting only ten months, Ecevit's first government was responsible for the successful <a href="/wiki/Turkish_invasion_of_Cyprus" title="Turkish invasion of Cyprus">Turkish invasion of Cyprus</a>, for which he is nicknamed the 'conqueror of Cyprus' (Turkish: <i>Kıbrıs Fatihi</i>). Despite winning an increased share of the votes and seats in the <a href="/wiki/Turkish_general_election,_1977" title="Turkish general election, 1977">1977 general election</a>, Ecevit was unable to form a coalition and instead formed a <a href="/wiki/40th_government_of_Turkey" title="40th government of Turkey">minority government</a> which lasted just one month. <a href="/wiki/Justice_Party_(Turkey)" title="Justice Party (Turkey)">Justice Party</a> leader <a href="/wiki/S%C3%BCleyman_Demirel" title="Süleyman Demirel">Süleyman Demirel</a> subsequently took over as Prime Minister and formed a <a href="/wiki/41st_government_of_Turkey" title="41st government of Turkey">three-party nationalist coalition</a>. Ecevit's CHP was able to bring down Demirel's government by 1978, after which he became Prime Minister for a third time by forming <a href="/wiki/42nd_government_of_Turkey" title="42nd government of Turkey">a government</a> supported by some <a href="/wiki/Independent_(politician)" class="mw-redirect" title="Independent (politician)">independent</a> MPs. Ecevit resigned as Prime Minister in 1979 following an election defeat in the <a href="/wiki/Turkish_senate_elections,_1979" title="Turkish senate elections, 1979">1979 senate elections</a>.</p>
<p>Bülent Ecevit recalled that he learned for the first time of the existence of <a href="/wiki/Operation_Gladio" title="Operation Gladio">Operation Gladio</a>, a secret "<a href="/wiki/Stay-behind" title="Stay-behind">stay-behind</a>" NATO army, in 1974. He has also said he suspected "<a href="/wiki/Counter-Guerrilla" title="Counter-Guerrilla">Counter-Guerrilla</a>", the Turkish branch of Gladio, of responsibility for the 1 May 1977 <a href="/wiki/Taksim_Square_massacre" title="Taksim Square massacre">Taksim Square massacre</a> in Istanbul, during which snipers fired on a protest rally of 500,000 citizens, killing 38 and injuring hundreds. CHP defeated <a href="/wiki/Justice_Party_(Turkey)" title="Justice Party (Turkey)">AP</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Turkish_general_election,_1977" title="Turkish general election, 1977">1977 general elections</a> by gathering 41% of the votes (the election came just after the events of 1 May). But with 213 seats out of 450 Ecevit could not receive a vote of confidence (see <a href="/wiki/40th_government_of_Turkey" title="40th government of Turkey">40th government of Turkey</a>). In 1978 Ecevit formed his third government (<a href="/wiki/42nd_government_of_Turkey" title="42nd government of Turkey">42nd government of Turkey</a>). However, after a defeat in by-elections in 1979, he resigned.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Leader_of_DSP">Leader of DSP</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=B%C3%BClent_Ecevit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Leader of DSP">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Following the <a href="/wiki/1980_Turkish_coup_d%27%C3%A9tat" title="1980 Turkish coup d'état">1980 coup</a> led by General <a href="/wiki/Kenan_Evren" title="Kenan Evren">Kenan Evren</a>, Ecevit was incarcerated and suspended from active politics for life along with the other political leaders of the time. A referendum in 1987 lifted his ban from politics, and he became the chairman of the <a href="/wiki/Democratic_Left_Party_(Turkey)" title="Democratic Left Party (Turkey)">Democratic Left Party</a> (<a href="/wiki/Turkish_language" title="Turkish language">Turkish</a>: <span lang="tr" xml:lang="tr"><i>Demokratik Sol Parti</i></span>, DSP), inheriting the position from his wife, <a href="/wiki/Rah%C5%9Fan_Ecevit" title="Rahşan Ecevit">Rahşan Ecevit</a>. His party failed to enter the National Assembly at the 1987 national elections, and in spite of passing the electoral barrier in 1991 managed to win only 7 seats in parliament. DSP's fortunes changed after the 1995 elections, when the party won 75 seats (out of 550). After two short-lived governments (formed by <a href="/wiki/Mesut_Y%C4%B1lmaz" title="Mesut Yılmaz">Mesut Yılmaz</a> and Necmettin Erbakan, respectively), Ecevit became a deputy prime minister in the last government of Mesut Yılmaz. In 1998–99 he was briefly the caretaker Prime Minister in the run-up to the 1999 general elections (<a href="/wiki/56th_government_of_Turkey" title="56th government of Turkey">56th government of Turkey</a>). In those elections – also helped by the fact that <a href="/wiki/Abdullah_%C3%96calan" title="Abdullah Öcalan">Abdullah Öcalan</a>, head of the separatist (PKK) was apprehended in Kenya and flown to Turkey during this period – Ecevit's party gained the largest number of seats, leading to Ecevit's final term as Prime Minister in a coalition with the <a href="/wiki/Motherland_Party_(Turkey)" title="Motherland Party (Turkey)">Motherland Party</a> (<a href="/wiki/Turkish_language" title="Turkish language">Turkish</a>: <span lang="tr" xml:lang="tr"><i>Anavatan Partisi</i></span>, ANAP) of Mesut Yılmaz and the <a href="/wiki/Nationalist_Movement_Party" title="Nationalist Movement Party">Nationalist Movement Party</a> (<a href="/wiki/Turkish_language" title="Turkish language">Turkish</a>: <span lang="tr" xml:lang="tr"><i>Milliyetçi Hareket Partisi</i></span>, MHP) of <a href="/wiki/Devlet_Bah%C3%A7eli" title="Devlet Bahçeli">Devlet Bahçeli</a>.</p>
<p>Ecevit's government undertook a number of reforms aimed at stabilizing the Turkish economy in preparation for accession negotiations with the European Union. Despite initial attempts to bring about economic reforms, an argument between Ecevit and President <a href="/wiki/Ahmet_Necdet_Sezer" title="Ahmet Necdet Sezer">Ahmet Necdet Sezer</a> in 2001 led to a <a href="/wiki/2001_Turkish_economic_crisis" title="2001 Turkish economic crisis">financial crash</a> due to the instability of both the government and the economic situation. The downfall of the government was speculated to also bring an end to an existing bailout package funded by the <a href="/wiki/International_Monetary_Fund" title="International Monetary Fund">International Monetary Fund</a>. Despite this, the government made swift progress in bringing about an economic recovery, spearheaded by the new <a href="/wiki/Ministry_of_Economy_(Turkey)" title="Ministry of Economy (Turkey)">Minister of Economic Affairs</a> <a href="/wiki/Kemal_Dervi%C5%9F" title="Kemal Derviş">Kemal Derviş</a>. Nevertheless, almost half of DSP group in the parliament issued from DSP to form <a href="/wiki/%C4%B0smail_Cem_%C4%B0pek%C3%A7i" class="mw-redirect" title="İsmail Cem İpekçi">İsmail Cem İpekçi</a>'s <a href="/wiki/New_Turkey_Party" title="New Turkey Party">New Turkey Party</a> (YTP). On the other hand, allegations of corruption, the economic crisis, as well as Ecevit's poor health made early elections unavoidable and the DSP faced an electoral wipeout in the <a href="/wiki/Turkish_general_election,_2002" title="Turkish general election, 2002">2002 general election</a>, losing all of its MPs. Ecevit resigned as DSP leader in 2004.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="As_a_poet_and_writer">As a poet and writer</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=B%C3%BClent_Ecevit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: As a poet and writer">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Bülent Ecevit was not only a politician but also a poet and a writer. He translated works by <a href="/wiki/Rabindranath_Tagore" title="Rabindranath Tagore">Rabindranath Tagore</a>, <a href="/wiki/T._S._Eliot" title="T. S. Eliot">T. S. Eliot</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Bernard_Lewis" title="Bernard Lewis">Bernard Lewis</a> into <a href="/wiki/Turkish_language" title="Turkish language">Turkish</a>. Ecevit, who also studied at the American <a href="/wiki/Robert_College" title="Robert College">Robert College</a>, one of the most prestigious high schools in Istanbul, was successful in these literary endeavors despite never having graduated from a university, a fact that also prevented him from ever running for the Presidency of the Turkish Republic.</p>

<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Ecevitmezari.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6f/Ecevitmezari.jpg/220px-Ecevitmezari.jpg" width="220" height="293" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6f/Ecevitmezari.jpg/330px-Ecevitmezari.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6f/Ecevitmezari.jpg/440px-Ecevitmezari.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1536" data-file-height="2048" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Ecevitmezari.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Ecevit's tomb at the State Cemetery in Ankara, Turkey.</div>
</div>

<p>Ecevit was hospitalized in Ankara and placed in a medically induced coma after suffering a <a href="/wiki/Cerebral_hemorrhage" class="mw-redirect" title="Cerebral hemorrhage">cerebral hemorrhage</a> on 18 May 2006, which struck him after he attended a friend's funeral. He died there from respiratory failure on 5 November 2006 at 20:40 (UTC), aged 81. He was buried in the <a href="/wiki/Turkish_State_Cemetery" title="Turkish State Cemetery">Turkish State Cemetery</a> (<a href="/wiki/Turkish_language" title="Turkish language">Turkish</a>: <span lang="tr" xml:lang="tr"><i>Devlet Mezarlığı</i></span>) in Ankara in a <a href="/wiki/State_funeral" title="State funeral">state funeral</a> on 11 November 2006. The funeral was attended by approximately a million people from all 81 provinces as well as politicians from Ukraine.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
